# Goldstein & Winner — the reverse-causation evidence, and why the cascade's key prediction is untested

**This is the KEY test of the cascade claim, and the answer is uncomfortable for it in a specific way: the evidence runs L2→L1 (training in portrayal produces understanding), and nobody has tested L1→L2 (understanding predicts portrayal quality) at all.**

**Sourcing note:** the 2012 paper (*Journal of Cognition and Development* 13(1), 19–37) is paywalled; ResearchGate/Academia/tandfonline all 403'd. Abstract and design captured from ERIC (EJ955776) and ArtsEdSearch; **the 2012 effect sizes could not be retrieved and are still missing.** The 2009 effect size below comes from a third party's power analysis and is second-hand. The failed replication (Ligthelm 2012) is an open PDF and is quoted verbatim with confidence.

## Study 1: Goldstein, Wu & Winner (2009) — actors are good at ToM but NOT empathy

*Imagination, Cognition and Personality* 29(2), 115–133. "Actors are Skilled in Theory of Mind but Not Empathy."

> "Adolescent actors outperformed adolescents without acting experience in their ability to imagine the mental states expressed by pictures of peoples' eyes (a theory of mind measure), **but did not excel on empathy as measured by a self-report scale.** These findings show that **adolescent and adult actors are skilled in reading others' mental states, but do not report above average levels of empathy.**"

As summarised in the 2020 Frontiers review: "Goldstein et al. (2009): adolescent and adult actors have above average skill in ToM tasks, **but not above average levels of empathy.**"

**Effect size (second-hand, from Ligthelm's power analysis):**
> "The study aimed to have a total sample size of 50 participants based on **the effect size of 0.55 from Goldstein et al. (2009) where the total sample consisted of 168 participants.**"

So: **d ≈ 0.55, n = 168.** A moderate effect. Note this is a *cross-sectional* comparison — actors vs non-actors — and therefore cannot distinguish selection from training.

**The dissociation matters independently:** actors are better at *reading* mental states but not at *feeling with* people. ToM and empathy come apart in exactly the population whose job is portrayal. This is Diderot's "penetration and no sensibility" showing up in a psychometric battery.

## Study 2: Goldstein & Winner (2012) — the longitudinal/causal attempt

**Abstract (via ERIC):**
> "Two cohorts participated: elementary school children and high school freshmen, each receiving one year of either acting or alternative arts instruction (visual arts or music). Researchers measured empathy and theory of mind both before and after training."
> "**Acting training (but not other arts) produced significant empathy gains in both studies. For adolescents specifically, acting training also yielded significant improvements on the Empathic Accuracy Paradigm, a naturalistic theory of mind measure.**"

**Design (via ArtsEdSearch):**
> "Quasi-experimental study" with two investigations: high school students (average age 14) and elementary students (average age 9). "High school students were freshmen at arts-focused schools; elementary students participated in after-school activities. Both groups assessed at baseline and **after ten months**."
- High school: "Acting students received **9 hours weekly** instruction; visual art/music peers served as comparison groups"
- Elementary: "Acting group met weekly for **90-minute sessions**; visual arts participants formed the comparison condition"

**Measures:**
> "tests created by the researchers, like the Fiction Emotion-Matching task, and previously used tests like the Empathic Accuracy Paradigm and the Basic Empathy Scale." Also "the Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test, Strange Stories Test, and Index of Empathy for Children."

**Results pattern:**
> "Elementary acting students showed empathy gains but no theory-of-mind improvement. High school acting students increased in both measures over ten months. The visual arts/music control group demonstrated smaller gains in both areas."

**Stated limitations (verbatim):**
> "**'True randomization couldn't be achieved'** since students were already enrolled. Additionally, stimulus materials 'may not have been realistic enough' for deep responses, and elementary students received less instruction than their high school counterparts."

⚠️ **Effect sizes for the 2012 study were not recoverable from any open source. Do not cite a number.** The design is quasi-experimental with a self-selection confound the authors acknowledge.

## Corroborating and conflicting evidence

From the Frontiers review "Could Acting Training Improve Social Cognition and Emotional Control?" (2020, PMC7538666):

> "**Schellenberg (2004):** 6-year-old children assigned with 6 weeks of acting training had improved behavioral ToM measures compared to children assigned to 6 weeks of music practice."
> "**Goldstein et al. (2013):** adolescents majoring in acting at high school (compared to other art majors e.g., music) used less suppression."
> "**Goldstein & Lerner (2018):** 4–5-years olds **randomly assigned** to an 8-week drama condition showed increased emotional control."
> "**Nettle (2006):** actors score slightly higher levels of empathy than non-actor controls."

The review's proposed mechanism — note the direction of the arrow:
> "actors, **by repeatedly engaging the first-person fictional perspective required to produce a character, may induce changes to the cortical networks underlying social cognition.**"

And its honest caveat:
> "a goal of further research should be to tease apart the conditions (e.g., age, acting experience, acting techniques, personality measures) under which such improvements occur."

The review reports **no effect sizes anywhere.**

## The failed replication — Ligthelm (2012), University of Cape Town

An open honours thesis, "Acting Training, Theory of Mind, and Empathy: Is There a Relationship?" (supervisor: Johann Louw). **Verbatim abstract:**

> "Acting requires an actor to understand the character's mental world and the experience of the character's feelings. Acting thus involves what is referred to in psychological terms as theory of mind… and empathy… This exploratory study compared the ToM and empathy of **20 theatre and performance students with different levels of acting training.** The ToM measures used were Movie for the Assessment of Social Cognition (MASC) and Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (RMET). The empathy measures used were the Interpersonal Reactivity Index and the Empathy Quotient (EQ). **No significant differences were found between the levels of ToM and empathy of the novice and experienced group.** The small sample size of this study raises the possibility that studies with larger sample sizes might still find that acting training teaches and develops ToM and empathy. Additionally the study also investigated the measures used for ToM and empathy. **The results of the ToM measures raised concerns regarding whether these measures are sensitive enough for high functioning individuals, particularly actors.** Both empathy measures were found to be reliable."

**Results (verbatim):**
> "Theory of Mind Analysis of the results showed that there were **no significant main effects for year (F[1,18] = 0.15, p = 0.96, η² = 0.05)** or gender (F[1,18] = 1.16, p = 0.37, η²= 0.26). The results thus show that the experienced group did not perform better than…"
> "Empathy MANOVA showed that there were **no significant main effects for year (F[1,18] = 0.15, p = 0.96, η² = 0.05)** or gender (F[1,18] = 1.16, p = 0.37, η² = 0.26)."
> "Age run as a covariate yielded no significant effects for the IRI (F[1,4] = .57, p = .46, η² = .04) or EQ (F[1,4] = .24, p = .63, η² = .02)."

**Measure validity (useful for us):**
> "Pearson's correlation coefficient was calculated for MASC and RMET and was found to be **positive, moderate and significant (r[18] = .47, p = .04)**. The measures for ToM thus appear to have moderate construct validity"
> "the correlation coefficient for the IRI and EQ was significant and revealed these measures to be **positively and highly correlated (r[18] = .74, p = .001)**. According to these results the measures for empathy have high construct validity."

**Underpowered (n=20 against a planned 50), so this is weak disconfirmation — but it is the only attempt at the dose-response version of the question (more training → more ToM?) and it found nothing.** The ceiling-effect concern is real and interesting: standard ToM instruments may not discriminate among high-functioning adults, which is a measurement-validity problem that should sound extremely familiar to anyone building rubrics for strong models.

## Implications for the L1→L2→L3 cascade

**Verdict: The evidence base points the WRONG WAY for the cascade — the causal arrow that has actually been tested is L2→L1, not L1→L2. And the cascade's own core claim has never been tested.**

1. **This is the reverse-causation result the task was looking for, and it is real.** Goldstein & Winner (2012), Schellenberg (2004), and Goldstein & Lerner (2018 — *randomised*) all test **acting training → social cognition gains**. Nobody assigned people to a *theory-of-mind* intervention and measured whether their *acting* improved. **The entire empirical literature runs portrayal → understanding.** Our framework asserts understanding → portrayal. The best available evidence is oriented against us, and the mechanism the reviewers propose is explicitly the reverse one: "by repeatedly engaging the first-person fictional perspective required to produce a character, may induce changes to the cortical networks underlying social cognition."
2. **The 2009 dissociation is a direct hit on a conflated L1.** Actors are +0.55 SD on ToM but **at baseline on empathy**. If L1 is "understand the character," actors have it. If L1 has smuggled in "feel with the character," actors demonstrably *don't* have it and are excellent anyway. **L1 must be defined as cognitive-only or it is empirically false as a prerequisite.** (Same conclusion as `psych-diderot-paradox-actor.md`, arrived at independently.)
3. **THE CRITICAL GAP — state this plainly in the framework doc:** *no study in this literature measures acting quality.* Goldstein measures ToM and empathy in actors. Brown measures brain activity during portrayal. Konijn measures felt emotion. Dumas measures personality and divergent thinking. **Not one of them regresses portrayal quality on comprehension.** The cascade's central empirical claim — that understanding a character predicts portraying it well — **has never been tested in humans.** It is not refuted; it is *unexamined*. That is a much more precise and more useful finding than "the pedagogy disagrees," and it is what I'd lead with.
4. **What weak evidence exists points at a null.** Ligthelm found no ToM/empathy difference between novice and experienced acting students (η²=0.05, p=0.96, n=20, underpowered). Dumas et al. found professional actors scored *near the mean* on other-emotional appraisal while *student* actors scored highest (see `psych-actor-self-concept.md`) — i.e. **the more accomplished performers were not the better mind-readers.** Two independent nulls/inversions on the dose-response question.
5. **The selection-vs-training confound cuts both ways and is unresolved.** Goldstein 2012 is quasi-experimental — "true randomization couldn't be achieved." Goldstein & Lerner (2018) *is* randomised but measures emotional control in 4–5 year olds, not ToM in actors. So the strongest causal evidence is on the youngest sample and the least relevant DV.
6. **Measurement lesson we should steal:** "The results of the ToM measures raised concerns regarding whether these measures are sensitive enough for high functioning individuals." **Ceiling effects on comprehension instruments in an expert population.** If our L1 items are easy for frontier models, L1 scores will show no variance, and a no-variance layer cannot predict anything downstream — which would produce a *spurious* refutation of the cascade for purely psychometric reasons. Before concluding L1 doesn't predict L2 from our own data, check L1 isn't at ceiling. Cross-ref `psycho-classical-test-theory.md`, `psycho-measurement-invariance-dif.md`.
7. **The one experiment that would settle it, which nobody has run:** measure character comprehension (L1) and independently-rated portrayal quality (L2/L3) in the same performers, and check whether L1 predicts L2 *within* the trained population. **We can run the model version of this cheaply, and it would be a genuinely novel contribution — it is a gap in the human literature, not just in ours.**
