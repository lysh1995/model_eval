# Personality COHERENCE vs personality CONSISTENCY

**This distinction is the conceptual hinge of the whole cross-check.** The team's metric measures
*consistency*. The construct they actually want is *coherence*. These are different things, and the
field separated them deliberately.

## The distinction, stated

| | **Consistency** | **Coherence** |
|---|---|---|
| Means | sameness, invariance across situations | lawful organization, predictable patterning |
| Metric | cross-situational correlation of behavior | stability of the *pattern* of variation |
| Variability is | error to be removed | **the phenomenon itself** |
| Human ceiling | r ≈ .20–.30 | r ≈ .40–.50 (profile stability) |
| Violated by | any situational responsiveness | only by *unpredictable* variation |

**Consistency asks "did he do the same thing?" Coherence asks "did he do the thing that makes sense
for him, given this situation?"** A character can be maximally coherent and minimally consistent at
the same time. **The team is measuring the axis on which a good character is expected to score
low.**

Cervone & Shoda's framing of why coherence is the field's real subject matter:

> "**Were it not for the coherence of personality functioning, there might be little need for a
> psychology of personality**" (Cervone & Shoda)

And the explicit break with trait models:

> "**People exhibit coherent patterns of experience and action that cannot be fully described or
> explained by personality trait models**"; "across contexts, **people display predictable patterns of
> behavioral variation that cannot be captured by trait constructs, which correspond to mean levels
> of response.**"

Note the precise diagnosis: **trait constructs correspond to *mean levels of response*.** A character
sheet is a vector of mean levels. It is, by construction, incapable of expressing the patterning that
constitutes coherence. **This is not a flaw in the team's sheet; it is a flaw in sheets.**

Coherence defined:

> "Personality coherence refers to **the lawful organization and patterning of psychological constructs
> within the individual**" — "the extent to which a person's characteristics are **coordinated,
> unified, and integrated**."

> "intraindividual stability **in the organization and patterning of behavior** incorporates
> psychological situations into the idiographic analysis of personality."

Cervone's "bottom-up" program: "the individual's characteristic personality style is **explained in
terms of an underlying system of social-cognitive and affective processes, rather than by locating
the person within a system of high-level trait dimensions.**"

## THE DECISIVE PASSAGE — Mischel & Shoda (1995, p. 262)

This is the clearest statement in the literature of why the team's metric is measuring the wrong
thing, and it comes with the prescription attached.

> "The proposed theory dissolves this apparent dilemma because it considers **the variability of
> behaviors within individuals across situations not as 'error' nor as 'due to situation rather than
> to the person,' but as a meaningful reflection of the enduring personality system.** It predicts
> that **the person's behaviors in a domain will change from one situation to another—when the *if*
> changes, so will the *then*—even if the personality system were to remain entirely unchanged.**"

**"…even if the personality system were to remain entirely unchanged."** Behavior changes; the person
does not. The team's metric infers a changed person from changed behavior. **That inference is
invalid, and this sentence is the refutation of it.**

> "It resolves the person-situation debate, **not merely by recognizing that person and situation are
> important, as has long been acknowledged, but by conceptualizing the personality system in ways
> that make variability of behavior across situations an essential aspect of its behavioral
> expression and underlying stability.**"

### And then, the prescription — verbatim (p. 262)

> "The theory also has important implications for the levels of behavioral consistency across
> situations that should be expected. **To the degree that people are characterized by stable and
> distinctive patterns of variations in their behavior across situations, it will intrinsically limit
> the degree of cross-situational consistency that can be obtained**, as has been demonstrated
> elsewhere (Shoda, 1990). **Because intraindividual variability in behavior necessarily implies
> changes across situations in the person's rank ordering with respect to a behavior, it constrains
> the level of consistency, as traditionally defined, that one should expect theoretically.**
> Consequently, **researchers committed to demonstrating consistency in personality may do so more
> effectively by identifying the stable patterns of behavior variability that characterize an
> individual or a type, rather than pursuing higher cross-situational consistency coefficients.**"

**That last sentence is the answer to "what should we measure instead," written in 1995.** Substitute
"teams committed to demonstrating character consistency in roleplay models" and it needs no further
adaptation. It also states the tension **as a theorem, not an observation**: strong signatures
*mathematically constrain* cross-situational consistency. **The two metrics cannot both be
maximized.** The team is currently maximizing the wrong one.

## Structure vs. state — the level distinction (Mischel & Shoda, 1995, p. 263)

> "**The structure of the personality system can remain stable across situations, but the personality
> state changes readily when the situational features that are active change**, or when they are
> alternatively encoded or cognitively and emotionally transformed."

> "The **personality state** refers to the pattern of activation among cognitions and affects at a
> given time in this system. It thus depends on the particular context and the psychological
> situations experienced by the individual at that moment."

**Coherence is a property of the structure. Consistency is (wrongly) demanded of the state.** The
character sheet describes structure; the transcript exhibits states. **The team is scoring states
against a structural specification** — a category error. A stable structure *predicts* variable
states. That is what a structure is *for*.

## "Which one is the real one?" — the rejection-sensitivity case (pp. 264–265)

Mischel & Shoda's worked clinical example. **This is directly transferable to companion-character
evaluation**, because it is precisely the intimate-relationship domain.

Rejection-sensitive individuals (Downey & Feldman, 1994): in intimate relationships, cues construable
as uncaring ("partner is attentive to someone else") trigger "she doesn't love me," then expectations
of abandonment, anger, "scripts for coercive and controlling behaviors."

> "On the other hand, **the same individuals also may be characterized by an exceptionally strong
> tendency for romantic and attentive behaviors and feelings in seemingly similar situations that
> have different active ingredients.** A defining situation-behavior profile for this disposition—**its
> behavioral signature**—may include **both** being more prone than others to anger, disapproval, and
> coercive behaviors in certain types of situations in intimate relationships, **and** being more
> supportive, caring, and romantic than most people, for example, in initial encounters with
> potential partners who are not yet committed to them, or later in the relationship when they are
> about to lose the partner."

> "The profile analysis of these individuals suggests that **the same rejection-sensitive man who
> coerces and abuses his partner also can behave in exceedingly tender and loving ways.** In semantic
> terms, **he is both hurtful and kind, caring and uncaring, violent and gentle.** **Traditional
> analyses of such 'inconsistencies' in personality lead to the question, which one of these two
> people is the real one? What is simply the effect of the situation?**"

**The answer:**

> "In contrast, **the hypothesized personality system allows the same person to have contradictory
> facets that are equally genuine. The surface contradictions become comprehensible when one analyzes
> the network of relations among cognitions and affects to identify their psychological
> organization.** The research problem becomes to understand **when and why** different cognitions and
> affects become activated predictably in relation to different external and internal features of
> situations."

> "**The theory views the individual's distinctive patterns of variability not necessarily as internal
> contradictions but as the potentially predictable expressions of a stable underlying system that
> itself may remain quite unchanged in its organization.** The challenge is to **discriminate,
> understand, and predict when each aspect will be activated**, and the dynamics that underlie the
> pattern."

**"Contradictory facets that are equally genuine."** The team's metric asks the exact question Mischel
& Shoda identify as the *symptom of the wrong framework*: "which one of these two people is the real
one?" It resolves that question by declaring the character sheet the real one and every contextual
departure a defect. **The literature's answer is that the question is malformed.**

## Elevation and shape — the two components of a signature (pp. 264, 266)

> "The behavioral manifestations of a disposition and its processing dynamics are seen in **the
> elevations and shapes of the situation-behavior profiles—the dispositional signatures—that
> distinguish its exemplars.**"

> "It calls for measurement **not only of characteristic mean levels of relevant behaviors but also of
> the distinctive behavioral signatures—the if...then... profiles that define the disposition.**"

> "Compare, for example, the verbal aggression profile of two individuals (9 and 28), shown in Figure
> 2. **Even if both persons have similarly elevated levels of overall verbal aggression, one is most
> aggressive when warned by counselors, whereas the other becomes most aggressive when peers try to
> approach him to make positive contact, suggesting that the profiles may reflect very different
> processing dynamics.**"

**Two orthogonal measurables:**
- **Elevation** = mean level across situations. ← *this is all a character sheet encodes, and all the
  current metric checks.*
- **Shape** = the if-then pattern. ← *this is what distinguishes two characters with identical
  sheets, and the current metric is blind to it.*

**Children 9 and 28 have the same elevation and opposite shapes.** The team's metric would score them
identically. They are, per the paper, different people.

## Cervone's KAPA: five paths to coherence (2022)

Cervone, D. (2022), *EJP* 36(3), 319–346. KAPA distinguishes three classes of social-cognitive
knowledge structures — **beliefs, goals, and evaluative standards** — grounding **five aspects of
personality coherence**:

1. **Belief-Based**
2. **Goal-Based**
3. **Evaluative Standards-Based**
4. **Intra-Psychic** — "coherent functional interrelations **among** personality systems"
5. **Phenomenological**

**Note path 4.** Coherence *is partly constituted by* interrelations among systems — i.e. **by what
the team calls entanglement.** In KAPA, a personality whose subsystems did *not* interrelate would be
*less* coherent, not more. See `psych-metatraits-trait-intercorrelation.md`.

## Implications for the team's "character consistency" metric

1. **Rename and re-operationalize the metric. "Character consistency" is a term of art that names the
   discredited construct.** What the team wants — "does this feel like the same person?" — is
   **character coherence**. The rename is not cosmetic: consistency is measured as *invariance of
   behavior*, coherence as *stability of the pattern of variation*. The metric changes with the name.

2. **The literature already wrote their prescription.** Mischel & Shoda (1995, p. 262): "researchers
   committed to demonstrating consistency in personality may do so more effectively by **identifying
   the stable patterns of behavior variability that characterize an individual or a type, rather than
   pursuing higher cross-situational consistency coefficients.**" Concretely, this means:
   - **Measure profile stability** (ipsatized if-then profile, split-half across the session) — see
     `psych-caps-behavioral-signatures.md` for the exact Wediko procedure and human benchmarks
     (.41–.48).
   - **Measure elevation and shape separately.** Elevation = does the mean match the sheet (human
     benchmark ~.90, per Fleeson). Shape = is the if-then pattern stable (human benchmark ~.45).
     **These are two numbers and they fail differently.** Elevation failure = the character drifted.
     Shape instability = the character is *incoherent* (random, not situationally responsive).
     **Shape instability is the true defect the team is hunting, and their metric cannot see it.**

3. **The "same person turn 3 vs turn 97" intuition survives — relocated to structure.** Coherence
   says the *structure* must be stable, not the *state*. So the legitimate question is: **"is the
   if-then profile derived from turns 1–48 the same as the one derived from turns 49–97?"** That is a
   correlation between two profiles, it has a human benchmark, and it is exactly what the team means
   by "the same person." Their current metric instead asks "is the *state* at turn 97 the same as the
   *state* at turn 3?" — which, per the structure/state distinction, **should be answered "no" by any
   working personality.**

4. **The failure mode is unpredictable variation, not variation.** This is the line that lets the team
   keep a real defect metric. Coherence is violated when variation is **not lawfully related to
   situational features** — when the *then* changes without the *if* changing. **Operationally: for
   each character, fit the if-then profile on half the data and use it to *predict* behavior on the
   held-out half.** Predictable variation = coherent = good. Unpredictable variation = incoherent =
   **the genuine bug**. Invariant behavior = rigid = also bad. **Three outcomes, not two** — and the
   current binary (matches sheet / deviates from sheet) can express none of them.

5. **The rejection-sensitivity case is the team's product domain, and it breaks their metric hardest.**
   Companion characters live in exactly the intimate-relationship space where Mischel & Shoda's
   example lives. A character who is "tender in early encounters, coercive when feeling abandoned" has
   a **textbook coherent signature** — and would be scored by the current metric as a catastrophic
   character break, because "hurtful and kind, caring and uncaring, violent and gentle" is maximal
   sheet deviation. **The most psychologically realistic companion character the team could build is
   the one their metric would reject most confidently.** That is the refutation, stated in their own
   product terms.

6. **On trait entanglement / crosstalk — coherence reframes it from bug to constituent.** KAPA path 4
   defines part of coherence as "**coherent functional interrelations among personality systems**."
   Mischel & Shoda locate personality in "the network of relations among cognitions and affects."
   **A system with zero crosstalk has zero intra-psychic coherence** — it is five independent
   processes wearing one name, which is what the field means by *incoherent*. So the diagnostic
   question is not "is there crosstalk?" but **"is the crosstalk lawful?"**:
   - Perturb "shy" → other traits move **in a stable, reproducible, sheet-explicable pattern**
     → **coherence**. Keep it.
   - Perturb "shy" → other traits move **differently on every run, or in ways no reading of the
     character explains** → **incoherence**. Fix it.
   **Reproducibility across runs is the discriminator**, and it is cheap to test: run the perturbation
   N times and correlate the crosstalk vectors. The team already has the harness to do this; they are
   just currently asking it the wrong question.
