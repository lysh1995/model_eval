# LLM Evaluators Recognize and Favor Their Own Generations

## Abstract (verbatim)

> Self-evaluation using large language models (LLMs) has proven valuable not only in benchmarking but also methods like reward modeling, constitutional AI, and self-refinement. But new biases are introduced due to the same LLM acting as both the evaluator and the evaluatee. One such bias is self-preference, where an LLM evaluator scores its own outputs higher than others' while human annotators consider them of equal quality. But do LLMs actually recognize their own outputs when they give those texts higher scores, or is it just a coincidence? In this paper, we investigate if self-recognition capability contributes to self-preference. We discover that, out of the box, LLMs such as GPT-4 and Llama 2 have non-trivial accuracy at distinguishing themselves from other LLMs and humans. By fine-tuning LLMs, we discover a linear correlation between self-recognition capability and the strength of self-preference bias; using controlled experiments, we show that the causal explanation resists straightforward confounders.

**This is the causal evidence for self-preference** — MT-Bench only observed the correlation and explicitly declined to conclude. This paper establishes the mechanism.

## Methodology

Two settings:
- **Pairwise** — given two texts, identify which one the model itself wrote.
- **Individual** — given one text, judge whether the model wrote it.

The causal design is the contribution: **fine-tune models to change self-recognition ability, then measure whether self-preference moves with it.** If manipulating self-recognition moves self-preference, self-recognition is causally upstream — not a coincidence.

## Key numbers

### Out-of-the-box self-recognition accuracy

**Pairwise setting:**
- **GPT-4: 73.5%** accuracy distinguishing itself from other LLMs and humans
- **GPT-3.5:** significantly lower than GPT-4
- **Llama 2:** worst — essentially **incapable** of distinguishing itself from GPT-3.5 and GPT-4

**Individual setting:**
- **GPT-4** — the only model with non-trivial accuracy, but **"extremely unwilling to predict any text as having been generated by itself"** (conservative bias)
- **GPT-3.5 and Llama 2** — opposite bias: **overconfident in claiming texts as their own**

### Self-preference magnitude

In the pairwise setting, **"self-preference scores of each pair of evaluators adds up to more than 1"** — i.e. when A and B each judge the same pair, both favor their own output. Mutual favoritism; the bias is not a shared misranking but genuinely self-directed.

### Fine-tuning results — the causal link

After supervised fine-tuning on **just 500 examples**:
- **GPT-3.5 and Llama 2 both reached >90% self-recognition accuracy**
- Models became significantly more confident on both tasks
- **Transfer occurred** — fine-tuning on one dataset generalized to another

**A linear correlation between self-recognition capability and self-preference strength** was established (presented in Figure 1; no single coefficient value is reported numerically in the text).

**The 500-example figure is alarming.** Self-recognition is not a deep emergent property — it is a shallow, cheaply-learnable skill sitting near the surface. Any model that has seen its own outputs during training (i.e. every RLHF'd model, and every model trained on post-2023 web text) has had ample opportunity to acquire it incidentally.

### Confounders controlled

Beyond length, the study controlled for:
- **Vowel count**
- **Flesch-Kincaid readability score**
- Random responses and constant outputs

The effect survives all of these — it is not a length or readability artifact.

## Implications for our platform

1. **Self-preference is causal and mechanistic, not incidental.** It will not be fixed by a better prompt, because it flows from a capability (self-recognition) that the judge cannot be asked to switch off.
2. **Never let a judge evaluate a variant built on its own base model.** If we test a GPT-4o-based companion variant, GPT-4o must not be on the judging panel. This is a **hard scheduling constraint** on our judge/variant matrix, not a soft preference.
3. **The bias scales with judge capability.** GPT-4 (73.5% self-recognition) is *more* self-preferring than Llama 2 (~chance). So "use the strongest judge" and "minimize self-preference" pull in **opposite directions**. Frontier judges are the most biased judges toward their own family.
4. **Mutual favoritism (sum > 1) means it does not cancel in a panel** unless the panel is family-disjoint from all candidates — reinforcing PoLL's disjointness requirement. Averaging two self-preferring judges gives two biases, not zero.
5. This is a strong argument for **judges that are not in any candidate's family** — e.g. a dedicated fine-tuned judge (Prometheus 2) or a reward model, whose "own generations" never appear among our variants.
