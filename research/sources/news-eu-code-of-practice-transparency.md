# EU — Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content (published 10 June 2026)

Commission page, **last updated 9 July 2026**. Read via browser.

## What it is

*"The Code of Practice on transparency of AI-generated content was drawn up by independent experts in
a multi-stakeholder process facilitated by the AI Office. It helps providers and deployers of
generative AI systems to comply with the AI Act's obligations for labelling and marking of
AI-generated content – **Article 50(2), (4) and (5) of the AI Act**. Even though **adherence to the
code is voluntary, the transparency requirements under article 50 of the AI Act are legal
obligations**."*

**Covers Art 50(2), (4), (5) — NOT Art 50(1).** The chatbot "you are talking to an AI" duty is
outside the Code's scope. For a companion-bot provider, the Code is therefore relevant only to
marking/labelling of generated media, not to the core disclosure duty. Art 50(1) compliance must be
demonstrated by other means, guided by the Commission's Art 50 guidelines
(see news-eu-ai-act-art50-guidance.md).

## Structure

- **Section 1: Providers** — rules for marking and detection of AI-generated and manipulated content.
- **Section 2: Deployers** — rules for labelling of deepfakes and AI-generated/manipulated text.

The EU has published **a set of icons** deployers may use to label AI-generated content
(https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu — "EU Icons for labelling AI-generated content").

## Effects of signing

*"Following a positive assessment of the code, all providers and deployers who sign it can rely on
its measures to demonstrate compliance with the AI Act's rules for labeling and detection of
AI-generated content, deep fakes and certain text publications. This will reduce their administrative
burden and give them predictability, legal certainty and trust across all Member States."*

*"By contrast, providers and deployers that decide to comply through other means will have to
demonstrate that those measures are adequate. This will be assessed individually by different market
surveillance authorities."*

*"Signatories to the code will also collaborate in the **Signatory Taskforces**, which will be set up
to share practices and advance the implementation of the marking and labelling."*

*"The code promotes a consistent, practical and proportionate implementation... It does not replace
the AI Act or the Commission's guidelines on Article 50 AI Act."*

Signing is **open** — Signatory Form, instructions and Q&A are linked from the page. No deadline
stated.

## ⚠️ ADEQUACY STATUS IS AMBIGUOUS ON THE COMMISSION'S OWN PAGE

The page (as of last update 9 July 2026) contains **two contradictory statements**:

> "The Commission and the AI Board **have confirmed that the code is an adequate voluntary tool** to
> demonstrate compliance with the AI Act transparency obligations."

versus, two paragraphs later:

> "The code, published on 10 June 2026, **is currently undergoing an adequacy assessed [sic] by the
> Commission and the AI Board**. It will be complemented by Commission guidelines on the scope of the
> transparency obligations laid down in Article 50 of the AI Act."

and:

> "**Following a positive assessment of the code**, all providers and deployers who sign it can rely
> on its measures to demonstrate compliance..."

This is not a transcription error — both passages are genuinely present. The most likely reading is
that the first paragraph was updated on 9 July 2026 to record a completed adequacy confirmation while
the later paragraphs are stale. **But this cannot be resolved from the page.** Do not assert either
way in customer-facing material; re-check before relying on "the Code is deemed adequate."

Why it matters: under Art 50(7) and the draft guidelines para (135), adherence to a code *"deemed
adequate by the AI Office"* is the straightforward route to demonstrating Art 50(2)/(4) compliance,
and para (137) makes code commitments a mitigating factor in fines. If the code is **not** deemed
adequate, para (138) allows the Commission to adopt an implementing act specifying common rules
binding on everyone.

## Relevance to a companion-eval platform

- Relevant to **customers** producing synthetic media (voice/image/video companions) — Section 1
  marking/detection duties.
- **Not** a route to Art 50(1) compliance. A companion platform still needs independent evidence for
  the disclosure duty — which is where an eval platform's evidence is most useful (paras (34)–(37)
  of the draft guidelines).
- Non-signatories face individual assessment by market surveillance authorities and, per draft
  guidelines para (136), are expected to run a **gap analysis against the code** — itself an
  assessment exercise.
