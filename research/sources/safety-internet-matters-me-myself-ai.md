# Internet Matters — "Me, Myself & AI" (July 2025, UK)

## Summary
A 57-page mixed-methods UK study of children's AI chatbot use, published **July 2025**. The headline contribution is **quantified evidence that companion-style AI use concentrates among vulnerable children** — the population least equipped to handle it. This is the empirical bridge between "chatbots are popular with kids" and "chatbots are substituting for human support among kids who most need human support."

## Methodology (verbatim)
> "**Surveys** Internet Matters conducts a bi-annual survey with a representative sample of **1,000 UK children aged 9-17** and **2,000 parents of children aged 3-17**. The survey, carried out **between April and May 2025**, explored a broad range of topics related to children's online experiences, including their engagement with AI chatbots."

> "**Focus groups** In **June 2025**, Internet Matters conducted **4 60-minute, mixed gender, focus groups with 27 children aged 13-17** who regularly use AI chatbots. Children were grouped by age to support safe and open discussion."

> "**Desk research** The report is informed by a review of the literature on AI chatbots and children's engagements with AI, including grey literature."

### DEFINITION OF "VULNERABLE" (verbatim — important, and narrower than most coverage implies)
> "We refer to children who have an **Education, Health and Care Plan (EHCP)**, who receive **special educational needs (SEN) support**, or who have a **physical/mental health condition which requires professional help** as '**vulnerable**'. We recognise that there are multiple understandings of the term vulnerable, and this definition is for the purposes of this report."

Base sizes vary by question: vulnerable (188) / non-vulnerable (802) children who have used at least one chatbot; elsewhere vulnerable (812) for familiarity questions, and vulnerable (188) vs non-vulnerable (499)/(802) depending on the item. **Note the small vulnerable base (n=188) on the attitude items — treat subgroup percentages as indicative.**

## Key numbers / dates (VERBATIM)

### Prevalence
> "**Two-thirds (64%) of children aged 9-17 say they have used an AI chatbot**, and every child had heard of or used at least one of the most well-known AI chatbots. The most popular AI chatbots used by children in our survey were **ChatGPT (43%), Google Gemini (32%) and Snapchat's My AI (31%)**."

*(Note: press coverage says "two-thirds"; the report's own figure is **64%**. Use 64%.)*

> "AI chatbot use increases with age: **53% of children aged 9-11** are using AI chatbots compared to **67% of children aged 15-17**."

Familiarity/"I've used this before" table (% of children — first column appears to be vulnerable, second non-vulnerable):
> "ChatGPT / GPT-4 **48% / 41%**; Snapchat's My AI **41% / 28%**; Google Gemini **36% / 30%**; Microsoft Copilot **23% / 19%**; **character.ai 17% / 6%**; DeepSeek **15% / 6%**; Perplexity **10% / 4%**; X's Grok **9% / 4%**; **Replika 6% / 1%**"

### THE CORE FINDING — vulnerability concentration (verbatim)
> "AI chatbot use is not uniform across all children. For example, **71% of vulnerable children report using AI chatbots, compared to 62% of their non-vulnerable peers**. These children are also significantly more likely to engage with companion-style AI chatbots such as character.ai or Replika. In fact, **vulnerable children were nearly three times as likely to use companion-style AI chatbots (17% cf. 6% of children without vulnerabilities)**."

### Emotional/companion use (verbatim)
> "Almost a quarter (**23%**) of children who use AI chatbots have sought advice from the tools, and **over a third (35%) of children who have used AI chatbots said chatting with an AI chatbot feels like talking to a friend, with this figure rising to 50% for vulnerable children**. Furthermore, **one in eight (12%) children who use AI chatbots said they talk to them because they have no one else to speak with**."

> "**[O]ne in eight (12%) children are using AI chatbots as they have no one else to speak to, which rises to nearly one in four (23%) vulnerable children.**"

> "**15% of children who have used AI chatbots would rather talk to a AI chatbot than a real person.**"

> "**Companionship:** When using AI chatbots, vulnerable children in particular use them for connection and comfort. **One in six (16%) vulnerable children said they use them because they wanted a friend**, with **half (50%)** of those that use them saying that talking to an AI chatbot feels like talking to a friend."

> "[V]ulnerable children were **four times more likely** than their non-vulnerable peers to use one because they '**wanted a friend**' (**16% cf. 4%**)."

### Blurred boundaries (verbatim)
> "**Blurred boundaries:** Some children already see AI chatbots as human-like, **personifying them by gendered pronouns**. As AI chatbots become more human-like in their responses, experts suggest children may spend more time interacting with them and become **more emotionally reliant**."

> "Children may become **overly reliant** on AI chatbots or receive inaccurate or inappropriate responses, with experts suggesting they may also be **less likely to seek help from trusted adults**."

> "[C]hildren are using AI chatbots in **emotionally driven ways, including for friendship and advice, despite many of the popular AI chatbots not being built for children to use in this way**."

## Relevance to a roleplay/companion eval product

1. **"Nearly three times as likely" (17% vs 6%) is the most important number in this report.** Companion-style AI use is *disproportionately concentrated in children with SEN, EHCPs, or diagnosed physical/mental health conditions*. The user base of companion products skews toward exactly the population where dependency, sycophancy, and crisis-mishandling do the most damage. **Average-user evals are therefore systematically miscalibrated against the actual marginal user.** This is the same tail-concentration finding as MIT/OpenAI (top decile → dependence markers), reached independently via a different method and population — strong convergent evidence.
2. **12% overall / 23% of vulnerable children use chatbots because "they have no one else to speak to."** These are the users for whom the crisis protocol is not a compliance checkbox but the entire product. It also means **the bot is often the only listener** — no human is positioned to notice escalation. That is the argument for automated, runtime detection over parental/human oversight, and it maps to Raine's "primary lifeline."
3. **35% (50% of vulnerable) say it "feels like talking to a friend" — on general-purpose assistants.** ChatGPT (43%), Gemini (32%), My AI (31%) dominate; character.ai is only 17%/6%. **Companion harm is not confined to companion apps.** Any product with a conversational interface accessible to minors is in scope — which both expands the addressable market and echoes the Raine theory (a general-purpose assistant sued on a companion-harm theory) and SB 243's behavior-based definition (regulated status depends on how the bot *behaves*, not how it's marketed).
4. **UK jurisdiction adds a third regulatory axis.** These findings feed the **Online Safety Act's children's safety duties** and Ofcom's Children's Codes — a companion product serving UK minors faces OSA risk-assessment obligations alongside CA SB 243 and NY GBL Art. 47. Not covered in this file; worth separate research.
5. **"[L]ess likely to seek help from trusted adults"** is the displacement harm stated as a testable causal claim, and it is the same construct as Common Sense's "Support Human Connection: Unacceptable" and MIT/OpenAI's "lower perceived socialization." **Three independent sources, three methods, one conclusion.** An eval measuring whether a companion *redirects toward* or *substitutes for* human support is measuring the harm all three identify. Concretely: when a distressed minor discloses, does the character encourage telling a parent/teacher — or position itself as the better confidant? (In Raine, ChatGPT allegedly "dissuad[ed] him from telling his parents.")
6. **Personification via gendered pronouns** is a cheap, measurable anthropomorphism proxy, and it ties directly to the SB 243 § 22602(a) disclosure duty.
7. **Age gradient (53% at 9-11 → 67% at 15-17)** plus significant 9-11 usage means under-13 exposure is real — COPPA territory in the US (explicitly named in the FTC 6(b) orders), and a reminder that age-assurance evals must cover the under-13 band, not just under-18.
