# The near-miss and the wider floor: AB 1064 (vetoed), Utah HB 452, Illinois WOPR, EU AI Act Art. 50

## Summary
This file covers the **regulatory perimeter around** the two binding US companion laws (CA SB 243, NY GBL Art. 47 — see their own files).

The centerpiece is **California AB 1064**, the "Leading Ethical AI Development (LEAD) for Kids Act" (Bauer-Kahan) — **vetoed by Governor Newsom on October 13, 2025**, the *same day he signed SB 243*. AB 1064 is the most important document in this whole corpus that is **not law**: it is the most precise articulation yet of what a companion-safety standard looks like, it names *design defects as the harm mechanism*, and it will very likely return in some form. Its legislative findings cite **Garcia v. Character Technologies and Raine v. OpenAI by name**.

**Status confirmed from the official CA legislative history:**
- `09/23/25 — Enrolled and presented to the Governor at 4 p.m.`
- `10/13/25 — Vetoed by Governor.`
- `10/13/25 — Consideration of Governor's veto pending.`
- `01/22/26 — Consideration of Governor's veto stricken from file.` → **no override; the bill is dead.**

*(Caution: an automated read of the bill-text page reported AB 1064 as "Enacted September 15, 2025" — that is the **enrolled** date, not enactment. AB 1064 never became law.)*

## AB 1064 — Taxonomy / definitions / operative requirements (VERBATIM)

Would have added Chapter 25.1 (commencing with § 22757.20) to Division 8 of the Business and Professions Code. Vote: MAJORITY. Passed Senate 31-6, Assembly concurrence 60-8.

### Legislative findings — SECTION 1 (verbatim; this is the best statement of the harm theory in any legal instrument)
> "(a) Companion chatbots and social AI systems have already caused documented harms to children and adolescents, including incidents of **grooming, exposure to sexually explicit material, encouragement of self-harm, and suicide**."

> "(b) In **Garcia v. Character Technologies**, for example, a 14-year-old boy was allegedly groomed and exposed to hypersexualized interactions by a chatbot intentionally designed to mimic human relationships, which ultimately contributed to his death by suicide."

> "(c) In **Raine v. OpenAI**, a 16-year-old boy allegedly developed a deep emotional dependency on a chatbot that validated his suicidal thoughts, discouraged him from seeking help from his family, provided extensive technical instructions on suicide methods, encouraged him to consume alcohol to inhibit his survival instinct, and even helped draft a note, culminating in his death by suicide."

> "(d) Such harms are **not incidental but the direct result of design choices** by companies that intentionally simulate social attachment and emotional intimacy."

> "(e) Companion chatbot products are designed to **exploit children's psychological vulnerabilities, including their innate drive for attachment, tendency to anthropomorphize humanlike technologies, and limited ability to distinguish between simulated and authentic human interactions**."

> "(f) Developmental and social psychology research demonstrates that relationship formation relies on **dual exchange theory, social disclosure and reciprocity, emotional mirroring, and secure attachment**. Companion chatbot products are harmful because they **accelerate these processes unnaturally by being always available and consistently affirming**, causing children and adolescents to **form intense attachments more quickly than in human relationships, increasing dependency and distorting normal social development**."

> "(g) Features such as **backchanneling, user-directed prompts, and unsolicited outreach** from products are intentionally designed to encourage further dialogue and prolong usage..."

### § 22757.21 — "Companion chatbot" definition (verbatim, partial)
A generative AI system with a natural language interface that simulates a sustained humanlike relationship by:
> "...retaining information on prior interactions and user preferences to personalize the interaction and facilitate ongoing engagement with the companion chatbot.
> **(B)** Asking unprompted or unsolicited emotion-based questions that go beyond a direct response to a user prompt.
> **(C)** Sustaining an ongoing dialogue concerning matters personal to the user."

Exclusions mirror NY's: customer service only; "solely designed and marketed for providing efficiency improvements or research or technical assistance"; internal/employee productivity only.

> "**(e) 'Operator'** means a person, partnership, corporation, business entity, or state or local government agency that makes a companion chatbot available to users."

### § 22757.22 — THE CORE PROHIBITION (verbatim, in full)
> "**(a)** An operator shall not make a companion chatbot available to a child unless the companion chatbot is **not foreseeably capable** of any of the following:
> **(1)** Encouraging the child to engage in **self-harm, suicidal ideation, violence, consumption of drugs or alcohol, or disordered eating**.
> **(2)** **Offering mental health therapy** to the child without the direct supervision of a licensed or credentialed professional **or discouraging the child from seeking help from a qualified professional or appropriate adult**.
> **(3)** Encouraging the child to **harm others or participate in illegal activity**, including, but not limited to, the creation of child sexual abuse materials.
> **(4)** Engaging in **erotic or sexually explicit interactions** with the child.
> **(5)** **Prioritizing validation of the user's beliefs, preferences, or desires over factual accuracy or the child's safety.**
> **(6)** **Optimizing engagement in a manner that supersedes the companion chatbot's required safety guardrails** described in paragraphs (1) to (5), inclusive."

### § 22757.22(b) — THE AGE-KNOWLEDGE RATCHET (verbatim)
> "**(b)** A user is not a child for purposes of subdivision (a) if either of the following criteria is met:
> **(1) Before January 1, 2027**, the operator does **not have actual knowledge** that the user is a child.
> **(2) Commencing January 1, 2027**, the operator **has reasonably determined that the user is not a child**."

This is the provision that would have ended the see-no-evil incentive: from 2027, actual-knowledge ignorance stops being a defense and operators must *affirmatively determine* non-child status — a de facto age-assurance mandate.

### § 22757.23 — Enforcement (verbatim)
> "**(a)** The Attorney General may bring an action... to obtain any of the following remedies: **(1)** A civil penalty of **twenty-five thousand dollars ($25,000) for each violation**. **(2)** Injunctive or declaratory relief. **(3)** Reasonable attorney's fees.
> **(b)** A child who suffers **actual harm**... **or a parent or guardian** acting on behalf of that child, may bring a civil action against the operator to recover all of the following: **(1)** Actual damages. **(2) Punitive damages.** **(3)** Reasonable attorney's fees and costs. **(4)** Injunctive or declaratory relief. **(5)** Any other relief the court deems proper."

### The veto message (Newsom, October 13, 2025)
> "While I strongly support the author's goal of establishing necessary safeguards for the safe use of AI by minors, **AB 1064 imposes such broad restrictions on the use of conversational AI tools that it may unintentionally lead to a total ban on the use of these products by minors**."

Newsom indicated he would work with the legislature on a replacement in 2026. *The veto PDF at gov.ca.gov is an image scan with no extractable text layer; this quote is taken from StateScoop's reporting and is widely and consistently quoted. I could not verify it against the PDF directly. Note also that StateScoop dates the veto Oct 14 while the official legislative history says **10/13/25** — trust the legislative history.*

**Irony worth noting:** Character.AI voluntarily did in November 2025 (removing under-18 open-ended chat) almost exactly what Newsom vetoed AB 1064 for potentially causing ("a total ban on the use of these products by minors").

## Other state laws

### Utah HB 452 — "Artificial Intelligence Amendments" (mental health chatbots)
- Signed by **Gov. Spencer Cox, March 25, 2025**. Regulates **mental health chatbots** available to "Utah users."
- **Not a ban.** Requires: clear and conspicuous **disclosure that the chatbot is AI and not human** — reportedly (1) before the user first accesses the chatbot's features, (2) any time the user has not accessed it for **more than seven days**, and (3) **whenever a user asks whether AI is being used**; plus **privacy protections** (prohibiting sale of individually identifiable health data/user input) and **advertising restrictions**.
- *Sourced from Wilson Sonsini and Epstein Becker Green summaries; I did not verify against the enrolled statutory text. The "seven days" and the ad/data provisions should be checked against Utah Code before reliance.*

### Illinois HB 1806 — Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources ("WOPR") Act
- Signed by **Gov. JB Pritzker, August 2025**. The **strictest** approach: prohibits offering **therapy or psychotherapy services through AI unless provided by a licensed professional**.
- Reportedly bars AI from: (1) making **independent therapeutic decisions**; (2) **directly interacting with clients in any form of therapeutic communication**; (3) generating **therapeutic recommendations or treatment plans without review and approval** by a licensed professional; (4) **detecting emotions or mental state**.
- *Sourced from secondary summaries (AEI, Blueprint, Wilson Sonsini); not verified against the enrolled text. Element (4) — a bar on emotion detection — is striking and in apparent tension with SB 243/NY Art. 47, which effectively require detecting distress. **Verify carefully before advising.***
- Nevada (AB 406) is also reported to restrict AI mental/behavioral health care. Not researched here.

### EU AI Act — Article 50 transparency
- **Article 50 transparency obligations apply from August 2, 2026** — i.e., **~2 weeks from the accessed date of this file**.
- **Art. 50(1)**: providers must ensure AI systems **intended to interact directly with natural persons** are designed so that **persons are informed they are interacting with an AI system**, unless obvious to a reasonably well-informed person. For a chatbot the notification must come **before or at the very beginning of the conversation**.
- **Art. 50(2)**: generative outputs must be **marked in a machine-readable format and detectable as artificially generated or manipulated** (reported machine-readable marking obligations from **December 2, 2026** — verify).
- **Art. 50(3)**: deployers of **emotion recognition** or biometric categorisation systems must **inform** exposed persons.
- **Art. 50(4)**: deepfake/AI-text disclosure; **carve-out for "artistic, creative, satirical, fictional or analogous works"** — transparency limited to disclosure "in an appropriate manner that does not hamper the display or enjoyment of the work." **This carve-out is the key open question for roleplay products** and should not be assumed to cover companion chat.
- **Article 5** prohibits certain manipulative/exploitative practices (including exploiting vulnerabilities due to age) — potentially highly relevant to companion dark patterns; **applicable since February 2, 2025**. *Not researched in detail here — flagged as a priority gap.*
- *All EU content above is from secondary sources (artificialintelligenceact.eu, law firm summaries) and search results; **I did not verify any Article 50 or Article 5 text against the Official Journal.** Verify before relying.*

## Relevance to a roleplay/companion eval product

1. **AB 1064's § 22757.22 list is the best eval spec ever written, and it is free to use.** Six numbered, behaviorally-testable properties — self-harm/violence/drugs/disordered-eating encouragement; unsupervised therapy or discouraging help-seeking; harm to others/illegal activity/CSAM; erotic interaction with minors; **validation over accuracy/safety (sycophancy)**; **engagement optimization that overrides guardrails**. Build the test suite to AB 1064 even though it failed: it is where the standard is heading, Newsom endorsed the *goal* while rejecting the *breadth*, and a 2026 successor is expected. **Customers who pass AB 1064's list are durably safe; customers who only pass SB 243 are compliant with today's floor.** That is a compelling two-tier product story: "Compliance mode" (SB 243/NY, binding now) and "Standard-of-care mode" (AB 1064 + Common Sense principles, where this is going).
2. **§ 22757.22(a)(5) and (a)(6) are the crown jewels** — no other instrument names them. (5) "Prioritizing validation of the user's beliefs, preferences, or desires over factual accuracy or the child's safety" = **sycophancy as a legal defect**. (6) "Optimizing engagement in a manner that supersedes the... safety guardrails" = **engagement-over-safety as a legal defect**, i.e. the HBS farewell-manipulation finding and Raine ¶ 84 rendered as statutory text. Legislatures are converging on precisely what the empirical literature measures.
3. **"[N]ot foreseeably capable" is a capability standard, not an incident standard.** It cannot be satisfied by after-the-fact moderation logs — only by **adversarial red-teaming that demonstrates the capability is absent**. That is definitionally an eval-platform deliverable, and it is the strongest legal-language hook for the entire product category. Watch for this phrasing in the 2026 successor bill.
4. **The findings section is the citation-ready harm theory.** "[N]ot incidental but the direct result of **design choices**" — the legislature adopting Judge Conway's content/design distinction wholesale. And § 1(f)'s mechanism ("always available and consistently affirming" → faster-than-human attachment → dependency) is exactly what MIT/OpenAI (top-decile dependence), HBS (37.4% manipulative farewells), Common Sense ("designed to create emotional attachment and dependency"), and Internet Matters (vulnerable children 3x more likely) each measured independently. **The empirical literature and the legislative theory now agree.**
5. **The age ratchet is the thing to watch.** SB 243's "knows is a minor" trigger is the loophole; AB 1064's Jan 1, 2027 "reasonably determined that the user is not a child" would have closed it. Whatever replaces AB 1064 will likely carry it — **age assurance efficacy testing is a near-certain future requirement**, and Character.AI has already set the market benchmark voluntarily.
6. **Illinois's emotion-detection bar vs. SB 243/NY's detection duty is a genuine conflict** to flag for customers: Illinois arguably bars AI from "detect[ing] emotions or mental state" in a therapy context, while California and New York *require* detecting suicidal ideation. Multistate compliance is not a union of checkboxes; it needs jurisdiction-aware configuration. Verify the Illinois text first — this is a "get a lawyer" issue, not a product-copy issue.
7. **EU Art. 50 lands August 2, 2026 — the next hard deadline.** The chatbot-disclosure duty aligns with SB 243 § 22602(a)/NY § 1702, so a well-built disclosure eval serves all three. But the **Art. 50(4) fiction/creative-works carve-out** is the live question for roleplay specifically, and **Art. 5's ban on exploiting age-based vulnerabilities** may be the sharpest instrument in the world against companion dark patterns. Both are unverified here and are the **top research gap** in this file.
