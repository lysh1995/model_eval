# Banks & Li — PRISMA scoping review of how machine companionship is measured (N=71 works)

**Authors:** Jaime Banks, Zhixin Li
**Status:** **Peer-reviewed**, *Journal of Computer-Mediated Communication*, vol. 31(2), published **2026-02-23**.

**READ THIS ONE FIRST IF YOU READ ONLY ONE.** It is the single most relevant new publication to our platform's core problem, because its subject *is* measurement — not effects.

## Abstract (verbatim)

> "The notion of machine companions has long been embedded in socio-technological imaginaries. Recent advances in AI have moved those media musings into believable sociality manifested in interfaces, robotic bodies, and devices. Those machines are often referred to colloquially as 'companions,' yet there is little careful engagement of machine companionship (MC) as a formal concept or measured variable. This PRISMA-guided scoping review systematically samples, surveys, and synthesizes current scholarly works on MC (N = 71; 2017–2025)."

## Method

- **PRISMA-guided scoping review**
- **N = 71 scholarly works**, **2017–2025**

## The core finding — measurement fragmentation

- Most frequently assessed constructs: **intimacy, attachment, trust**
- But approaches are **"highly varied"**, with **more than 50 distinct measured variables** across 71 works
- **Most measures adapt human-relationship scales without machine-specific validation**

## Why this matters for the eval platform — this is the strategic finding

1. **It independently confirms the gap our platform exists to fill.** A peer-reviewed PRISMA review of the entire field concludes there is no agreed operationalization of machine companionship and >50 competing variables. **The field has no measurement standard.** That is both the risk (we can't just adopt a consensus instrument, because there isn't one) and the opportunity (a well-constructed instrument could become the standard).
2. **"Most measures adapt human-relationship scales without machine-specific validation" is a direct critique of most of the instruments in this batch.** It is precisely why **HAABI** (arXiv:2605.29484) matters — HAABI was built inductively *from companion users* rather than adapted from interpersonal attachment theory, and its abstract makes exactly this argument ("Existing measures often adapt interpersonal frameworks..."). Banks & Li's review is independent, peer-reviewed corroboration that HAABI's critique of the field is correct.
   - Conversely, this is a **mark against the AI Attachment Scale**, which *is* derived from attachment theory — though it at least conducted machine-specific construct validation across 5 studies.
3. **It supports a deliberate choice on our part**: we should not pick a scale off the shelf and call it validated. We should pick a *construct set* (substitution / separation anxiety / loss of control), assemble items from the best-validated sources for each, and **run our own validation**. Banks & Li's review is the citation justifying that decision to stakeholders who ask "why not just use an existing scale?"
4. The 2017–2025 window means it **predates HAABI, AIED, and AIDep-22** (all 2026). The fragmentation it documents has, if anything, gotten worse since — four new scales landed in the six months after its search window closed. **Nobody has compared them to each other.** A head-to-head psychometric comparison of AI Attachment Scale vs. HAABI vs. AIDep-22 vs. AIED does not exist and would be a genuine contribution.

## Limitations

- Scoping review, not a meta-analysis — it maps the literature, it does not pool effects or rank instruments by quality.
- Search window closes 2025; misses the 2026 scale crop entirely.
- Covers "machine companionship" broadly — includes robotic/embodied companions, not only LLM chatbots.
