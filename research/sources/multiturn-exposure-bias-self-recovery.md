# Exposure Bias versus Self-Recovery: Are Distortions Really Incremental?

EMNLP 2021. arXiv:1905.10617.

**Status: the single most important counterweight to the off-policy pessimism. This is the paper that says replaying a foreign prefix is probably OK.**

## Abstract

Investigates whether teacher forcing during training causes incrementally worsening distortions at test time. Proposes metrics evaluating **quality, diversity, and consistency** when feeding **ground-truth prefixes versus model-generated prefixes** into autoregressive LMs.

## The methodology — it is *exactly* our experiment

> compare model performance when given "**ground-truth data prefixes (instead of prefixes generated by the model itself)**" versus natural model generation

This isolates the effect of conditioning on a prefix the model did not produce. Both automatic and human evaluations, across open-ended generation tasks.

**Map onto us:** a "ground-truth prefix the model didn't generate" IS our replayed dialogue history from a different model. The structural question is identical, one level up (tokens → turns).

## Core finding (verbatim)

> "the distortion induced by the prefix discrepancy is **limited, and does not seem to be incremental during the generation**."

They identify the **"self-recovery ability of the LM"** — language models actively counteract exposure-bias effects rather than accumulating errors progressively.

## Conclusion on severity

> Contrary to prevailing assumptions, exposure bias appears **substantially less problematic than widely believed** in the literature.

## Relevance to companion-eval-platform — this is the crux of the off-policy verdict

The naive RL/imitation framing (O(H²) compounding, DAgger, static-context Theorem 3.1) predicts that conditioning on off-policy history is catastrophic and worsens with horizon. **This paper tests that prediction empirically in exactly the relevant setting and finds it FALSE for autoregressive text generation.** Distortion is bounded and non-incremental because LMs self-recover — they re-condition on the visible context rather than on a hidden state that has drifted.

Why the theory over-predicts: the O(H²) bound is **worst-case**, and it assumes the state can drift arbitrarily far. In dialogue replay the state is *re-anchored every turn* by an injected user turn written by someone else. The model reads a coherent (if unfamiliar) history and responds to it. There is no hidden accumulator to corrupt.

**Synthesis with the other sources:**
- Static-context Theorem 3.1 (O(H²), (H−i) weighting) = worst-case bound, front-loaded
- Self-Recovery = observed average-case behavior, bounded and non-incremental
- Time-To-Inconsistency = the residual real risk: *abrupt* prompt-to-prompt drift is what actually kills (HR≈4.7), and a replayed turn that doesn't follow is exactly an abrupt jump

⇒ **The threat from replay is not compounding drift. It is per-turn LOCAL INCOHERENCE at the seams.** That is a measurable, per-turn quantity (semantic discontinuity between the model's reply and the next replayed user turn), not an unbounded horizon effect. Which means it can be *detected and controlled for* rather than merely feared.

**Caveat on transfer:** this paper is 2021, pre-RLHF-era, token-level, on open-ended generation with smaller LMs. The self-recovery claim at the *turn* level for *instruction-tuned* models is plausible-by-analogy but not directly established. This is the highest-value thing for us to validate on our own data, and we can: compare a model's turn-N behavior given replayed history vs. given its own self-generated history for the same character. If self-recovery holds, the two should agree.
