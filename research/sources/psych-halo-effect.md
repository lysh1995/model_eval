# Thorndike (1920), "A Constant Error in Psychological Ratings", *Journal of Applied Psychology* 4, 25–29

The paper that **named the halo effect**. Full text extracted (5pp). Directly relevant to the team's
"trait entanglement / crosstalk" concern: **this is the founding demonstration that human raters
cannot rate traits independently either** — and it is a *methods* paper about rating instruments,
which is exactly what a character-sheet rubric is.

## The opening claim, verbatim (p. 25)

> "In a study made in 1915 of employees of two large industrial corporations, it appeared that the
> estimates of the same man in a number of different traits such as intelligence, industry,
> technical skill, reliability, etc., etc, were **very highly correlated and very evenly
> correlated**. It consequently appeared probable that **those giving the ratings were unable to
> analyze out these different aspects of the person's nature and achievement and rate each in
> independence of the others**. Their ratings were apparently affected by **a marked tendency to think
> of the person in general as rather good or rather inferior and to color the judgments of the
> qualities by this general feeling**. This same constant error toward **suffusing ratings of special
> features with a halo belonging to the individual as a whole** appeared in the ratings of officers
> made by their superiors in the army."

**"Suffusing ratings of special features with a halo belonging to the individual as a whole"** — that
is the origin of the term, and it is a statement about *the impossibility of independent trait
judgment*, not about generosity or leniency.

## The aviation cadets — the headline numbers (p. 25)

The Scott rating plan required four independently-estimated dimensions: **Physical Qualities,
Intelligence, Leadership, Personal Qualities (Character)**.

> "**The instructions very emphatically required each of these four to be estimated independently of
> the others** ... Yet the correlations of the Intelligence rating with the ratings for Physique,
> Leadership and Character made by a very conscientious officer in the case of **137 aviation cadets**
> whose work he, as flight commander, supervised, **were .51, .58 and .64 respectively**."

> "**These are all higher than reality, plus the attenuation due to erroneous judgments, could well
> give**, especially within the restricted range of the commissioned-officer group. **They are also too
> much alike.** In reality Intelligence and Character or Intelligence and Leadership should give about
> three times as close a correlation as Intelligence and Physique."

Note the structure of the argument: the correlations are damning **not just because they are high,
but because they are *flat*** — intelligence↔physique (.51) should be far weaker than
intelligence↔leadership (.58), and it isn't. **Evenness is the signature of halo.**

The instructions themselves were emphatic (quoted in the paper, "Points for Special Attention", #9):

> "**In making or using any section of the scale, consider only the qualification it covers, totally
> disregarding all the others**"

**Explicit instructions to rate independently did not work.** This is the crucial precedent for the
team.

## Replication across raters (p. 27)

> "The same effect appears in the ratings given by other officers. **The correlations are too high and
> too even.** For example, for the three raters next studied the average correlation for **physique
> with intelligence is .31; for physique with leadership, .39; and for physique with character,
> .28**."

## The flying-ability comparison — a quantified halo (p. 27)

> "It is known from abundant evidence that technical ability as a flyer is a rather highly
> specialized quality. Considering the restricted range of the aviation cadets, **the correlation
> between general ability for officer work and technical ability as a flyer could hardly be above
> .40**, without any attenuation. **As attenuated by the imperfections of the rater's knowledge of
> both, it could hardly be above .25.** Yet the correlations for the eight raters studied in this
> respect are **.74, .85, .52, .91, .63, .72, .47 and .53, an average of .67**. **Obviously a halo of
> general merit is extended to influence the rating for the special ability, or vice versa.**"

**Observed .67 against a theoretical ceiling of .25.** This is the cleanest quantification in the
paper: the halo inflated an entangled correlation to ~2.7× its defensible maximum.

## The teachers (Knight, Teachers College) — 129 teachers (pp. 27–28)

Ratings were **official, used to determine salaries and promotions**:

> "**General merit as a teacher has correlations of .68 with intellect, .79 with power in discipline,
> and .63 with voice. It is clear that the rating of a teacher's voice must have been influenced by
> the general impression of her ability. Voice correlates .50 with 'Interest in Community Affairs,'
> and .63 with intelligence!**"

## Ruling out attenuation — the decisive test (p. 28)

Thorndike anticipated the obvious objection (maybe traits really are correlated, and unreliability
is masking it) and killed it:

> "In the cases so far the correlations are a resultant of (1) the real facts, (2) the constant error
> of the 'halo,' as we may call it, and (3) the reverse error of attenuation due to chance
> inaccuracies in the ratings. **In certain further work by Mr. Knight the correlations are freed from
> the last influence**, by being based on the composite rating of two groups... **The self-correlations
> of the ratings by one such a group with ratings for the same trait by the other group are over
> .90.** The correlations for general ability as a teacher with intellect and with ability to
> discipline are about **.95 and .80!** The correlation of intelligence and ability to discipline is
> about **.80!** **The correlations of a standard test of intelligence with general ability as a
> teacher and with ability to discipline are, for the individuals in question, not over .3.**"

**This is the killer contrast.** When you measure intelligence with an *objective test* instead of a
*rating*, its correlation with teaching ability drops from **.95 → ≤.30**. The entanglement lives in
**the rater**, not in the person. Reliability was over .90 — so this is not noise, it is **highly
reliable, systematic entanglement**.

## The conclusion, verbatim (pp. 28–29)

> "The writer has become convinced that **even a very capable foreman, employer, teacher, or
> department head is unable to treat an individual as a compound of separate qualities and to assign
> a magnitude to each of these in independence of the others.** The magnitude of the constant error
> of the halo, as we have called it, also seems **surprisingly large**, though we lack objective
> criteria by which to determine its exact size."

**Thorndike's proposed fix** (p. 29) — note this is a *design* prescription, and it is precisely
implementable in an LLM eval harness:

> "As a consequence **science seems to demand that, in all work on ratings for qualities the observer
> should report the evidence, not a rating, and the rating should be given on the evidence to each
> quality separately without knowledge of the evidence concerning any other quality in the same
> individual.**"

Two separable requirements: **(a) separate evidence from rating**, and **(b) blind each trait's
rating to the other traits' evidence.**

## Implications for the team's "character consistency" metric and "trait entanglement / crosstalk"

1. **The halo is in the measuring instrument, not only the measured object.** If the team's character
   consistency scores come from **an LLM judge reading a transcript against a character sheet**, that
   judge is a rater — and Thorndike's finding is that raters *cannot* decompose a person into
   independent qualities even when explicitly and emphatically instructed to. **Some fraction of the
   observed "crosstalk" when the team perturbs "shy" is very likely halo in the judge, not
   entanglement in the model.** These are different bugs with different fixes, and the current design
   cannot distinguish them.

2. **This is a confound that must be ruled out before "crosstalk" is called a model defect.**
   Cheap, direct test, straight from Thorndike's own logic:
   - Score each trait with **a separate judge call that sees only the evidence relevant to that
     trait** (Thorndike's prescription (b)), vs. the current all-traits-at-once call.
   - If the trait intercorrelations **collapse** under per-trait blinding → the crosstalk was in the
     judge. If they **persist** → it is in the model (and see below — it may then be realism).
   - Thorndike's own version of this test: rating-based intelligence↔teaching = **.95**;
     test-based intelligence↔teaching = **≤.30**. **The instrument was producing ~3× the true
     signal.** Expect an effect of that order.

3. **"Too high and too even" is a diagnostic the team can literally compute.** Thorndike's tell was
   not magnitude alone but **flatness** — plausibly-related and plausibly-unrelated trait pairs
   correlating equally. Compute the full trait × trait correlation matrix of the judge's scores. If
   "shy↔anxious" ≈ "shy↔verbose" ≈ "shy↔honest", that is a halo signature and the scores are not
   measuring what the rubric says.

4. **On whether entanglement is defect or realism — Thorndike cuts a specific way.** He demonstrates
   entanglement in *ratings*, and explicitly attributes it to the rater ("the correlations of a
   standard test of intelligence ... are not over .3"). So Thorndike **alone** does not license
   "entanglement = realism." What he establishes is narrower and still very useful:
   **any evaluation of a character that runs through a holistic human-or-LLM judgment will exhibit
   entanglement as a matter of course** — so a metric that *demands* independent trait movement is
   demanding something that has never been observed in a rating instrument in 100+ years. The case
   that entanglement is *substantively* real (in the person, not the rater) rests on the metatrait
   literature — see `psych-metatraits-trait-intercorrelation.md`. **Both are true at once, and the
   team needs to separate them:** part halo (fixable, in the judge), part real covariance (not a
   defect).

5. **The reverse risk — halo will also inflate the consistency metric itself.** If a judge forms a
   global impression that "this model is playing the character well," it will suffuse the per-turn
   consistency ratings with that impression, making the metric **artificially smooth and
   artificially high**, and blind to exactly the localized breaks the team wants to catch. Halo is
   not only a source of false crosstalk alarms; it is a source of **false all-clears**.

6. **Actionable design rules from a 1920 paper:**
   - Judge each trait in a separate call, blinded to other traits' evidence.
   - Require the judge to **quote the evidence (turn spans) before scoring** — "report the evidence,
     not a rating."
   - Randomize trait order; never present the character sheet's traits in a fixed sequence.
   - Never let the judge see a global "is this in character?" score before per-trait scores.
   - Report the trait × trait correlation matrix as a **standing instrument-health check**, with
     "too high and too even" as the failure signature.
