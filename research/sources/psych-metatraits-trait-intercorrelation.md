# Trait interdependence: are the Big Five actually orthogonal in practice?

**Directly targets the team's "entanglement / crosstalk" assumption**: that an author can perturb one
trait and expect only that trait to move.

**Short answer: no, the Big Five are not orthogonal in practice. But *why* they are correlated is
genuinely contested, and the honest verdict is "partly real, partly rater artifact." That two-part
answer is more useful to the team than either extreme, because it maps onto two different bugs with
two different fixes.**

## The basic fact: the scales are correlated

> "**scales measuring the Big Five factors ... tended to show a consistent pattern of
> intercorrelations**" (Digman 1997, as cited in "Substance and Artifact")

> "Researchers questioned the fundamentality of the Big Five traits, as **there was strong evidence
> suggesting that they are not orthogonal. In fact, the scales used to measure the Big Five are not
> orthogonal in practice.**"

Crucially, on where the orthogonality *impression* comes from:

> "**Evidence for the Big Five is not predicated on orthogonality** and several prominent researchers
> have been proponents of **oblique rotations**, including Cattell, Norman and occasionally Goldberg."

**The apparent independence of the Big Five is substantially a consequence of the *analytic choice*
to use orthogonal (varimax) rotation** — a convention, not a finding. Rotate obliquely, and the
factors correlate. **Orthogonality was assumed into the model, then read back out of it.**

## The actual intercorrelation values

From **Hopwood et al. (2011)** — a **GFP-skeptical** paper, so these are conservative estimates
(joint EFA across multiple inventories):

> "the inter-factor correlations for the five factor model **were not substantial using most rules of
> thumb** (N and E: **−.23**; N and A: .03; N and C: **−.23**; N and O: −.03; E and A: .05; E and C:
> .06; **E and O: .39**; A and C: −.02; A and O: −.16; C and O: .02)"

Second joint EFA (five inventories):

> "the inter-factor correlations for the five factor model were **modest** (**E and O: .22**; E and N:
> −.14; E and A: .03; E and C: .07; O and N: −.17; **O and A: −.20**; O and C: −.05; N and A: .07;
> **N and C: −.17**; A and C: −.01)"

Sample sizes: N = 433; N = 946 (joint EFA, three inventories); N = 956 (five inventories); N = 556
(CFA).

**Note what even the skeptics find: E–O = .39 and .22; N–E = −.23; N–C = −.23.** These are *not zero*.
The skeptical position is "modest," not "absent." **Nobody in this literature defends
orthogonality.**

## The metatraits: Stability and Plasticity

Digman (1997) named them **α** and **β**; DeYoung et al. (2002) relabeled them:

- **Stability (α)** = shared variance of **Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and low Neuroticism**
  > α (Stability) loads on: "**low Neuroticism (N) and high Agreeableness (A) and Conscientiousness
  > (C)**"
- **Plasticity (β)** = shared variance of **Extraversion and Openness/Intellect**
  > β (Plasticity) defined by: "**Extraversion (E) and Openness (O)**"

Proposed biology (DeYoung): "**Stability is associated with individual differences in serotonergic
functioning and Plasticity with individual differences in dopaminergic functioning.**"

The two metatraits are themselves "expected to be weakly correlated with one another."

Validity evidence — the metatraits *predict things the Big Five don't*: in DeYoung et al. (2002),
"**Stability positively predicted conformity and Plasticity negatively predicted conformity** across
university and community samples." Also "Metatraits of the Big Five **differentially predict
engagement and restraint of behavior**" (2009), and higher-order factors "predict **exploration and
threat in life stories**."

**If the correlations were pure noise, they could not predict outcomes. They do. Some of it is
real.**

## The strongest evidence for SUBSTANCE — DeYoung (2006), multi-informant

The key study, because it addresses the obvious objection (that correlations are one rater's halo):

> "In a large community sample (**N = 490**), **the Big Five were not orthogonal when modeled as latent
> variables representing the shared variance of reports from 4 different informants.** The standard
> higher-order factor structure was present in latent space: **Neuroticism (reversed), Agreeableness,
> and Conscientiousness formed one factor, labeled Stability, and Extraversion and Openness/Intellect
> formed a second factor, labeled Plasticity.**"

**Four independent informants per target.** A single rater's halo cannot survive that aggregation —
if the correlations were only halo, they would vanish in the shared-variance latent space. **They
didn't.** This is the best single argument that trait covariance is real and not merely rater bias.

DeYoung also found the *rater-quality* moderator, which is highly relevant to LLM-judge design:

> "single-adjective rating instruments are likely to **yield lower interrater agreement** than phrase
> rating instruments and ... **lower interrater agreement is associated with weaker correlations among
> the Big Five and a less coherent higher-order factor structure**"

**Worse measurement → *weaker* apparent entanglement.** Counterintuitive and important: a noisy judge
*under*-detects crosstalk. So if the team's judge is noisy and *still* shows crosstalk, the true
crosstalk is **larger** than measured, not smaller.

## The strongest evidence for ARTIFACT

**Biesanz & West (2004)** — compared self-reports, peer ratings, and parental ratings:

> "concluded that **'observed correlations among Big Five traits are the product of informant-specific
> effects'**; that is, **different domains were correlated within each data source but were unrelated
> across sources.**"

**GFP saturation drops when you cross inventories** (meta-analytic, **370 independent samples,
155,781 individuals**):

> "**GFP saturation was reduced substantially when based on between inventory data (26%) compared to
> within inventory data (50%).** These results indicate that **the GFP functions as a response artifact
> that may be reduced by administering scales from different inventories.**"

**Half the general factor evaporates when you change instruments.** That half was method, not person.

**"Substance and Artifact in the Higher-Order Factors of the Big Five"** — behavior-genetic tests:
- Study 1: **1,207 MZ and 698 DZ twin pairs** from Japan (n = 646), Canada (n = 453), Germany
  (n = 806). Study 2: 532 adults, 180 adolescents. Study 3: 1,525 individuals from 838 twin pairs.
- Path coefficients (Study 1 full model): **αg = .30; βg = .37**.
- Cross-observer correlations (Study 2): **".38 to .65 (Mdn = .55)"**.
- **The verdict is mixed and leans artifact**: "**artifact models outperformed substance models by
  root-mean-square error of approximation criteria**"; "**Full models combining both were slightly
  better still.**"

Hopwood et al. (2011) name the mechanism, and it is **Thorndike's**:

> "**response tendencies such as socially desirable responding and halo effects contribute to general
> factors**"

← This is the direct link to `psych-halo-effect.md`. **Part of measured trait entanglement is the
halo effect, 90 years on, still in the instrument.**

## van der Linden et al. (2010) — the meta-analysis, and the balanced verdict

- **K = 212 studies, total N = 144,117.**
- Found support for **Stability and Plasticity (α and β)** and a **GFP at the highest hierarchical
  level**. *JRP*, 44, 315–327.
- (Contested: "No support for a general factor of personality in a reanalysis of Van der Linden et
  al. (2010)" exists as a published rebuttal.)

Van der Linden's own position is the balanced one, and it is the one to carry forward:

> "**even though the general factor may reflect some level of artifactual variance, the GFP mainly
> represents a substantive factor causing correlations between personality traits and, thereby,
> challenging the assumption of independence of the Big Five.**"

> "Critiques can be roughly categorized into **a measurement artifact view and a statistical artifact
> view.** The idea that the GFP reflects **social effectiveness** is currently the leading substantive
> interpretation."

## Honest summary of the evidence

| Claim | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Big Five scales are intercorrelated in practice | **Uncontested.** Even skeptics find E–O ≈ .22–.39, N–E ≈ −.23, N–C ≈ −.23. |
| Orthogonality is a modeling convention (varimax), not a finding | **Well supported.** "Evidence for the Big Five is not predicated on orthogonality." |
| The correlations are *entirely* rater/method artifact | **Refuted.** DeYoung (2006) 4-informant latent modeling; metatraits predict external criteria. |
| The correlations are *entirely* substantive | **Refuted.** GFP saturation 50% → 26% across inventories; artifact models fit better on RMSEA. |
| **The correlations are part real, part artifact** | **This is the defensible reading**, and "full models combining both were slightly better still." |

## Implications for the team's "character consistency" metric and "trait entanglement / crosstalk"

1. **The premise that traits are independently composable is false as stated — and it is false in
   humans, not just in models.** "Perturb *shy*, expect only shyness to change" presumes orthogonal
   trait axes. **Human trait ratings have never been orthogonal.** The team is holding their model to
   a standard that no human personality dataset in a century has met. **Some crosstalk is mandatory
   for realism**; a model with a perfectly diagonal perturbation matrix would be *unlike any measured
   human sample*.

2. **But "entanglement = realism" is an over-claim, and the team should not be handed it.** They asked
   to be refuted where wrong; intellectual honesty runs both ways. **Roughly half the general factor
   is method artifact** (saturation 50% within-inventory → 26% between-inventory). So the correct
   position is: **crosstalk is expected, but the *observed amount* is inflated by the measuring
   instrument, and the *pattern* is what determines whether it is realistic.**

3. **Concrete: the expected crosstalk has a *shape*, and that shape is testable.** This is the single
   most actionable item in this file. Human trait covariance is not uniform — it is
   **structured by the metatraits**:
   - **Within Stability** (A, C, low N): perturbing one **should** move the others. Perturb "shy"
     toward high N → **expect** A and C to move. **This is realism.**
   - **Within Plasticity** (E, O): perturbing E **should** move O (E–O is the *largest* observed
     intercorrelation, **.22–.39**).
   - **Across metatraits** (e.g. C → O = .02; A → C = −.01): correlations are ~**zero**. Perturbing
     Conscientiousness should **not** move Openness.
   **So the team has a falsifiable prediction, not a vibe.** Build the model's trait × trait
   perturbation matrix and compare it against the human matrix (Hopwood's values above are a usable
   reference).
   - Model crosstalk **matches the metatrait structure** → **realism.** Stop calling it a defect.
   - Model crosstalk is **flat** — everything moves everything equally → **halo/artifact.**
     Thorndike's "too high and too even" signature. Fix the judge.
   - Model crosstalk is **structured but wrong** (C→O large, A→C zero) → **genuine defect.** Fix the
     model.
   **The current framework collapses all three into one "entanglement" number and can diagnose
   none of them.**

4. **Rule out the judge before blaming the model — and note the direction of bias.** Hopwood et al.
   name "**halo effects**" as a contributor to general factors. The team's judge is a rater. Run the
   Thorndike protocol from `psych-halo-effect.md` (per-trait blinded scoring) and the
   multi-inventory analogue: **score the same transcript with two differently-worded rubrics.** If
   crosstalk drops by roughly half, that half was instrument (the 50%→26% result predicts almost
   exactly this). **What survives is the model's actual trait structure.**
   Then apply DeYoung's moderator in the *right direction*: "lower interrater agreement is associated
   with **weaker** correlations among the Big Five." **A noisy judge under-reports crosstalk.** So
   surviving crosstalk measured by a noisy judge is a **lower bound.**

5. **Use multiple informants — DeYoung (2006) is a directly portable design.** Four informants,
   latent-variable modeling of shared variance. The eval analogue: **N independent judges (different
   models/prompts), model the shared variance, and analyze crosstalk in that latent space.** This is
   the only design in the literature that cleanly separates real trait covariance from rater halo,
   and the team can implement it — they are already running multiple judges for other metrics.

6. **The rotation problem undercuts the question itself.** The Big Five's independence is largely a
   varimax artifact; HEXACO re-rotates A and N such that quick temper changes trait membership
   entirely (see `psych-big-five-hexaco.md`). So **"did perturbing *shy* move only shyness?" is not
   basis-independent.** A model may represent character in a basis that is a *rotation* of the
   sheet's axes — in which case single-axis perturbation *necessarily* produces multi-axis motion in
   the sheet's coordinates, with **no defect anywhere**. Before treating crosstalk as a bug, the team
   should ask whether their sheet's axes and the model's internal axes are the same basis. **They
   almost certainly are not.**

7. **What this does NOT excuse.** Nothing here licenses arbitrary trait bleed. **Character-sheet
   traits are not Big Five traits** — a sheet may specify "afraid of dogs" and "loves opera," which
   have no metatrait relationship and *should* be independent. **The realism defense applies only to
   trait pairs with a documented human covariance structure.** If perturbing "shy" moves "loves
   opera," that is a defect and no amount of GFP literature rescues it. **The right question is
   always: is this specific crosstalk pair one where humans covary?**
