# Fleeson (2001), *JPSP* 80(6), 1011–1027

"Toward a Structure- and Process-Integrated View of Personality: Traits as Density Distributions of
States." **Full text extracted (17pp).** Page numbers are the journal's printed pages.

**This is the most quantitatively devastating source for a naive consistency metric.** Mischel's
argument was about *correlations*; Fleeson's is about *distributions*, and it is worse for the team's
framework, because it shows the amount of within-person variation directly rather than inferring it.

## Abstract, verbatim (p. 1011)

> "Three experience-sampling studies explored the distributions of Big-Five-relevant states
> (behavior) across 2 to 3 weeks of everyday life. **Within-person variability was high, such that the
> typical individual regularly and routinely manifested nearly all levels of all traits in his or her
> everyday behavior.** Second, **individual differences in central tendencies of behavioral
> distributions were almost perfectly stable.** Third, **amount of behavioral variability (and skew and
> kurtosis) were revealed as stable individual differences.** Finally, amount of within-person
> variability in extraversion was shown to reflect individual differences in reactivity to
> extraversion-relevant situational cues. Thus, decontextualized and noncontingent Big-Five content
> is highly useful for descriptions of individuals' density distributions as wholes. Simultaneously,
> **contextualized and contingent personality units (e.g., conditional traits, goals) are needed for
> describing the considerable within-person variation.**"

**Note the two-part conclusion**: Big Five content describes *the distribution as a whole*;
if-then/conditional units are required for *the variation within it*. **Both are needed. Neither
alone is a person.**

## Method — Study 1 (p. 1014)

- **46 students** at a small southeastern university.
- **"Five times per day for 13 days, participants described how they had been acting and feeling
  during the previous hour."** Reports on a regular schedule, every 3 hrs (noon, 3pm, 6pm, 9pm,
  midnight), 1–2 min each, on Palm Pilots.
- **"Of the possible 65 reports per participant, the mean was 49.6 reports (76%), and the median was
  53.5 reports (82.3%), with a range of 13–63 reports."**
- States rated with adjectives on **7-point scales**.
- **Within-person variability is defined as "the standard deviation across the resulting large number
  of states (i.e., how much the states belonging to one person differ from each other)."**

## THE NUMBERS — within-person SDs (Figure 3 caption, p. 1016)

> "**The larger standard deviations (for Extraversion, 1.08; for Conscientiousness, 0.97) show that
> variability cannot be much larger on a 7-point scale while maintaining normality and that
> individuals must largely overlap with each other in their behavior.** Conditional and contextual
> personality units are needed to describe this variation."

**Read that carefully: within-person SD is at the mathematical ceiling.** On a 7-point scale, an
SD of ~1.08 around a mean of ~4 is about as variable as a normal distribution can be without running
off the ends of the scale. **The typical person is nearly as variable as it is arithmetically
possible to be.**

Full within-person parameters from **Table 1** (p. 1017):

| | Extraversion | Agreeableness | Conscientiousness | Emotional Stability |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-state stability** | **.29** | **.48** | **.28** | **.36** |
| Location (mean) M | 3.99 | 4.97 | 4.72 | 5.31 |
| Location SD (between-person) | 0.75 | 0.74 | 0.66 | 0.68 |
| **Location Stability** | **.90** | **.94** | **.87** | **.90** |
| **Size (within-person SD) M** | **1.08** | **0.77** | **0.97** | — |
| Size SD | 0.24 | 0.23 | 0.27 | — |
| **Size Stability** | **.59** | **.58** | **.67** | — |
| Shape (skew) M | −0.16 | −0.34 | −0.28 | — |
| Shape (skew) Stability | .41 | .58 | .53 | — |
| Shape (kurtosis) Stability | .37 | .28 | .36 | — |

**Three numbers to burn in:**
- **Single-state stability: .28–.48** — one moment predicts another moment barely at all.
- **Location (mean) stability: .87–.94** — the *mean* is almost perfectly stable.
- **Within-person SD (1.08) > between-person SD (0.75)** for Extraversion. **A person differs from
  themselves more than people differ from each other.**

## THE KILLER FINDING — within-person ≥ between-person variability (p. 1016)

> "F tests on differences between two variances revealed that **within-person variance in Extraversion
> was greater than between-person variance in Extraversion questionnaire scores, F(41,43) = 1.90, p <
> .05, and greater than between-person variance in Extraversion behavioral averages, F(41, 45) =
> 2.06, p < .05;** that **within-person variation in Conscientiousness was greater than between-person
> variance in Conscientiousness behavioral averages, F(41, 45) = 2.15, p < .01;** and that
> within-person variation did not differ from between-person variation for any other trait. **In sum,
> individuals differ from themselves over time at least as much as they differ from each other at the
> average level, and more so for the traits of Extraversion and Conscientiousness.**"

**"Individuals differ from themselves over time at least as much as they differ from each other."**

This is the sentence. If the amount a single person varies across two weeks is **as large as the
amount that separates different people**, then **any metric that scores within-person variation as
deviation is scoring the normal human range as defect.** The team's metric would flag a real person's
Tuesday-vs-Thursday as a character break of the same magnitude as swapping them for a different
human being.

And the summary (p. 1016):

> "The width of these distributions makes clear that **the average individual routinely and regularly
> manifests all levels of Extraversion, Conscientiousness, and Intellect and most levels of
> Agreeableness and Emotional Stability in his or her daily behavior.** In sum, **one individual's
> behavior varies from hour to hour over a 2-week period close to the maximum extreme possible,
> almost as much as affect varies from hour to hour and at least as much as individuals differ from
> each other. Such variability is near the high end of expected possibilities.**"

Also (p. 1016): **traits are about as variable as *affect*** — "trait standard deviations are almost
equal to affect standard deviations, and some are larger... **Extraversion was more variable than
Negative Affect**." Extraversion — a *trait* — fluctuates hour to hour **more than mood does**.

## What IS stable: the MEAN, not the act (pp. 1017–1018)

The claim, stated as a falsifiable prediction (p. 1016):

> "The central claim is that **the individual differences that show regularity are the distributions of
> states, as opposed to single states or to only one parameter of the distributions.** If this claim is
> correct, then (a) **single states are not highly correlated** across independent assessments, (b)
> **locations of the distributions (means) are highly correlated** across assessments, and (c) at least
> one other distributional parameter is also highly correlated across assessments."

**All three confirmed.**

**Single states** (two randomly selected reports, ≥6 reports apart, averaged over 20 trials) — p. 1017:

> "**These correlations are slightly higher than the traditional .30 limit but are low enough to
> indicate that levels of single states do not exhibit sufficient regularity to be the focus of
> personality descriptions.**"

(The values: .29, .48, .28, .36 — i.e. **Mischel's .30 reproduced from the inside**, in
experience-sampling data, 33 years later.)

**Means** (split-half, 10 trials) — p. 1017:

> "**These correlations approach 1.00, showing that the location of an individual's distribution is
> highly predictable. For example, the .90 correlation for Extraversion means that an individual's
> average level of Extraversion in half the data was nearly identical to his or her average level of
> Extraversion in the other half of the data.**"

> "(Stepping up the single-state correlations with the **Spearman-Brown prophecy formula predicted
> values similar to these empirically observed values**.)"

— **directly confirming Epstein.** See `psych-epstein-aggregation.md`.

**Crucially, aggregation does not manufacture stability** (p. 1018) — Fleeson pre-empts the obvious
objection:

> "(**the low correlations for kurtosis also point out that aggregation does not artificially create
> stability—only when the characteristic is a stable aspect of the individual does aggregation
> produce large correlations**)"

## Variability itself is a stable trait (p. 1018)

> "**The size of an individual's distribution was clearly a reliable characteristic of the individual**,
> although not as reliable as was the location."

Size (SD) stability: **.59, .58, .67**. **How much you vary is itself a stable individual
difference** — comparable in magnitude to the Wediko if-then profile stabilities (.41–.48). This is
the deepest cut: **variability is not the absence of a trait; variability is a trait.**

And the mechanism (abstract): "**amount of within-person variability in extraversion was shown to
reflect individual differences in reactivity to extraversion-relevant situational cues**" — i.e. **the
SD is generated by if-then reactivity.** This is Fleeson's bridge to CAPS.

## Whole Trait Theory — the synthesis (Fleeson & Jayawickreme, 2015)

Fleeson, W., & Jayawickreme, E. (2015). "Whole Trait Theory." *Journal of Research in Personality*,
56, 82–92. — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0092656614001111

The theory splits a trait into two halves that must both be specified:
- **Descriptive side** — the trait as a **density distribution of states** (mean, SD, skew,
  kurtosis). This is the Big Five content.
- **Explanatory side** — the **mechanisms** producing differential reaction to situations. This is
  the social-cognitive / CAPS machinery.

> "adding an explanatory account to the Big 5 creates two parts to traits—an explanatory part and a
> descriptive part—which are recognized as **separate entities that are joined into whole traits**."

The core prescription: "personality researchers should **modify models of traits to include mechanisms
of differential reaction to situations**."

**A character sheet as the team currently uses it specifies only a point, not even a distribution —
neither half of a whole trait.**

## Implications for the team's "character consistency" metric

1. **The metric's implicit model of a trait is a point value. The evidence says a trait is a
   distribution — and a wide one.** "Shy = 0.8" cannot be checked against a turn, because a shy person
   in real data occupies **nearly the entire 1–7 range** of shyness-relevant behavior across two
   weeks. Scoring turn-level deviation from a point value means **scoring the normal width of a human
   distribution as defect on virtually every turn**. The metric will fire constantly, and the firings
   carry no information.

2. **"Same person in turn 3 and turn 97" has a measured answer in humans, and it is r ≈ .28–.48.**
   That is Fleeson's single-state stability — two moments from the same person's real life, sampled
   hours or days apart. **Two random turns from a genuinely consistent human correlate at ~.3.** If the
   team's model scores much above that on turn-to-turn invariance, it is **less variable than a
   human**, not more faithful to the character.

3. **What to measure instead — concretely, the four distribution parameters.** Replace "deviation from
   the character sheet" with **distribution recovery**:
   - **Mean (location)** — does the character's *average* level on each trait match the sheet?
     **Human benchmark for stability: .87–.94.** ← *this is the real "character consistency" metric,
     and it is the one that behaves.*
   - **SD (size)** — does the character vary the *right amount*? Human benchmark: **within-person SD
     ≈ 1.08 (Extraversion), 0.97 (Conscientiousness), 0.77 (Agreeableness)** on a 7-point scale.
     Stability of SD as an individual difference: **.58–.67**. **A model whose SD ≈ 0 is broken**,
     and the current metric would award it a perfect score.
   - **Skew / kurtosis** — shape. Stability .28–.58. Lower priority.
   - Then: **turn 3 vs turn 97 becomes "mean of the first block vs mean of the last block,"** which is
     a real drift test with a real benchmark (~.90).

4. **This yields a two-sided metric, which is the key structural fix.** The current metric is
   one-sided: variation is always bad, so the optimum is a frozen character. Fleeson's parameters
   give **both a floor and a ceiling**: too little variance is as diagnosable as too much. **"Persona
   flattening" and "persona drift" become two failure modes on one axis**, detectable with the same
   instrument. The team almost certainly already sees flattening in their models and currently has no
   metric that can express it — this is that metric.

5. **The strongest single line to bring to the team:** *"individuals differ from themselves over time
   at least as much as they differ from each other at the average level"* (p. 1016). A metric that
   penalizes within-character variation is penalizing an effect **the same size as the entire
   between-person personality signal.** It is not a small correction; it is the largest effect in the
   data.

6. **On trait entanglement / crosstalk — Fleeson gives the right test.** The SD of a trait is
   *generated by* "reactivity to trait-relevant situational cues" (abstract). So traits are **not
   independent knobs**; they are **distributions produced by a shared reactive system responding to
   shared situations.** If a situation is relevant to shyness *and* to warmth, then perturbing "shy"
   *should* move warmth, because both distributions are generated from the same cue-reactivity
   machinery. **The clean, decisive test the team can run:** perturb "shy" and ask **which parameter
   moved** on the other traits.
   - Only the **means** of unrelated traits shifted → **that is genuine crosstalk**, a real defect
     (the sheet's location was corrupted).
   - The **SDs / reactivity profiles** shifted on situationally-related traits → **that is Whole Trait
     Theory working as specified.** Realism.
   The current metric collapses these into one "entanglement" number and therefore cannot tell a
   corrupted specification from a functioning personality system.

7. **Both halves of a whole trait must be specified, or the eval is under-determined.** WTT: descriptive
   (distribution) + explanatory (if-then mechanism). The team's character sheet supplies **neither** —
   it supplies a point estimate. **A character sheet should specify: (a) a target mean per trait,
   (b) a target SD per trait, and (c) if-then contingencies** ("when rejected, withdraws"). Only then
   is there anything well-defined to score against, and only then does "deviation" mean something.
