# Mischel & Shoda (1995), *Psychological Review* 102(2), 246–268

**This is the central lead for the "character consistency" metric.** Full text extracted from the
Columbia PDF (23pp). Page numbers below are the journal's printed page numbers where visible.

## The thesis, verbatim (abstract, p. 246)

> "A theory was proposed to reconcile paradoxical findings on the invariance of personality and the
> variability of behavior across situations. For this purpose, individuals were assumed to differ in
> (a) the accessibility of cognitive-affective mediating units (such as encodings, expectancies and
> beliefs, affects, and goals) and (b) the organization of relationships through which these units
> interact with each other and with psychological features of situations. **The theory accounts for
> individual differences in predictable patterns of variability across situations (e.g., if A. then
> she X, but if B then she Y), as well as for overall average levels of behavior, as essential
> expressions or behavioral signatures of the same underlying personality system.**"

Note the **`if A, then she X; if B, then she Y`** formulation appears in the abstract itself.

## The setup: variability is not the enemy of personality (p. 246)

> "The construct of personality rests on the assumption that individuals are characterized by
> distinctive qualities that are relatively invariant across situations and over time. In a century
> of personality research, however, abundant evidence has documented that individual differences in
> social behaviors tend to be surprisingly variable across different situations. **Although this
> finding has been interpreted as evidence against the utility of the personality construct, we show
> that it need not be and, on the contrary, that this variability reflects some of the essence of
> personality coherence.**"

> "These findings invite a new conception of personality in which such patterns of variability are
> seen **not as mere 'error' but also as reflecting essential expressions of the same underlying
> stable personality system** that produces the individual's characteristic average levels of
> behavior."

## THE KEY CLAIM: variability is signal, not noise

This is the passage the team needs. Mischel & Shoda set it up as an empirical test with a **null
prediction** (pp. 249–250):

> "Note that if personality is conceptualized in terms of behavioral dispositions, the individual's
> variation in behavior across situations reflects differences among situations in people's typical
> levels of behavior (e.g., more cheerful at parties than at funerals), as well as the effects of
> measurement noise or random fluctuation. Therefore, when the data are standardized and rescaled
> relative to the typical level of behaviors expected in each situation, removing the main effects
> of situations, **the remaining variation in an individual's behavior across situations should
> simply be 'noise.' Then, the mean stability of the intraindividual pattern of variation should be
> zero. On the other hand, if the observed situation-behavior profile reflects enduring qualities of
> the individual, it should show some significant stability despite the noise.**"

And the framing of the question (p. 249):

> "From the perspective of a process conception of personality one must ask: Are the individual's
> distinctive if...then..., situation-behavior relations within a particular domain of social
> behavior stable and meaningful? ... **If the observed variability is simply 'error,' it needs to be
> removed; if it is potentially stable and meaningful, it may contain important clues about the
> underlying personality system, reflecting something of the essence of personality coherence and
> the system that produces it.**"

The answer was: **not zero.** The variability is signal.

> "Most important for the analysis of the nature of personality invariance and its behavioral
> expressions, process models seem to suggest that clues about the person's underlying qualities—the
> construals and goals, the motives and passions, that drive the individual—**may be seen in when and
> where a type of behavior is manifested, not only in its overall frequency.** If so, the patterns of
> situation-behavior relationships shown by a person might be **a possible key to individuality and
> personality coherence, rather than an error source to be eliminated systematically.**" (p. 248)

## The indictment of averaging (p. 252) — read this one twice

> "In sum, the finding of meaningful stable situation-behavior profiles indicates that there are
> characteristic intraindividual patterns in how individuals relate to different psychological
> conditions and that these patterns form a sort of **behavioral signature that reflects personality
> coherence** (Shoda et al., 1994). **In retrospect, what seems remarkable is not so much that this
> type of behavioral signature of personality exists, but rather that it continues to be treated as
> error and eliminated by simply averaging behavior over diverse situations. Although such
> aggregation is widely seen as the way to capture personality, it actually removes data that may
> alert us to the person's most distinctive qualities and to his or her unique intraindividual
> patterning of social behavior.**"

> "It cannot be dismissed as error and simply aggregated away without losing something of the
> essence of personality." (p. 252)

## The Wediko summer camp study — the numbers

Design (pp. 248–249):

- Residential summer camp for children, **ages 7 to 13**.
- Citations: Shoda (1990); Shoda, Mischel, & Wright (1989, 1993a, 1993b, 1994); Wright & Mischel
  (1987, 1988).
- **"participants were closely but unobtrusively observed in the course of the 6-week summer, with
  an average of 167 hours of observation per participant."**
- Situations were defined by **psychological features**, not nominal place/activity. Two constituent
  features emerged from cluster analysis: **valence (positive vs. negative)** and **type of person
  (adult counselor vs. child peer)**.
- **Five interpersonal situations**: three negative — "peer teased, provoked, or threatened";
  "adult warned the child"; "adult gave the child time out" — and two positive — "peer initiated
  positive social contact"; "adult praised the child verbally."
- Behaviors coded: verbal aggression, physical aggression, withdrawal, friendly/prosocial behavior,
  compliance, whining, prosocial talk.

**Critical methodological move — standardize within situation** (p. 249):

> "The frequencies of behavior were first converted to standardized scores within each situation to
> indicate the level of an individual's behavior in a situation relative to the normative levels in
> that situation. **Each person's situation-behavior profile reflects how his or her pattern of
> variability across situations deviates from the normative pattern for the sample.**"

> "**Standardization removes all the main effects of situations, so that the remaining
> intraindividual variance in the profile reflects the unique way in which the individual's behavior
> varies across the situations, above and beyond what is expected from the differences in the
> normative levels of behavior across situations.**"

### Profile stability coefficients (across all five situations), p. 250

> "The mean stability coefficients were **.47 (p < .01) for verbal aggression, .41 (p < .01) for
> compliance, .28 (p < .01) for whining, and .19 (p < .05) for prosocial talk** (Shoda et al.,
> 1994). Thus, overall, if...then..., situation-behavior profiles were significantly stable over the
> course of the summer."

### Profile stability *within the three negative situations only* (subtler discrimination), p. 250

> "Even though all three situations were negative in valence, and therefore the differences among
> them were subtler than among the five situations collectively, **the mean profile stability
> coefficient was essentially as high as the stability of the profiles over all five situations.
> Specifically, they were .48 (p < .01) for verbal aggression, .32 (p < .05) for physical
> aggression, .45 (p < .01) for compliance, .08 (p > .05) for whining, and .20 (p > .05) for
> prosocial talk** (Shoda et al., 1994)."

> "**It should be clear that these significant manifestations of behavioral coherence are obscured in
> the usual analyses of cross-situational consistency or by aggregating behaviors over different
> situations.**"

### Individual exemplars (Figure 2, from Shoda, Mischel & Wright 1994, Fig. 1, p. 678)

- **Child #9 — profile stability r = 0.89.** "Child 9 was more verbally aggressive than others
  (standardized score above 2.0 on average) when warned by an adult, but showed less aggression than
  others on average when approached positively by a peer."
- **Child #28 — profile stability r = 0.49.** "Child 28 was most verbally aggressive when approached
  positively by a peer, but not particularly aggressive when warned by an adult."

Two children, **opposite if-then profiles**. Aggregate over situations and they may look identical
on "aggressiveness." The *profile* is the personality.

### The null-hypothesis kill shot (p. 250)

> "Recall that the conception of personality as behavioral dispositions implies that intraindividual
> variations in a type of behavior across situations (after the main effects of situations are
> removed by standardization) reflect only intrinsic unpredictability or measurement error. **If that
> assumption were correct, the stability of the intraindividual pattern of variation should on
> average be zero. The overall findings are obviously inconsistent with this prediction** and
> indicate that the situation-behavior profiles reflect a statistically significant, stable facet of
> individual differences in social behavior observed as it unfolds in vivo in everyday social
> situations."

### **The trade-off, stated explicitly** (p. 250) — most important sentence for the team

> "Furthermore, as shown elsewhere (Shoda, 1990), **the degree that an individual is characterized by
> stable patterns of situation-behavior relations is negatively related to the level of overall
> cross-situational consistency that can be expected.**"

**Read that again.** Having a *strong, stable if-then signature* mathematically *lowers* your
cross-situational consistency score. The two metrics are in tension **by construction**. A model
with a vivid, stable, context-discriminating character will score *worse* on naive cross-situational
consistency than a flat model that does the same thing everywhere.

## Resolving the "classic personality paradox" — Mischel & Peake reanalysis (pp. 250–252)

The paradox (Bem & Allen, 1974): intuition says people are consistent; data says they are not.

Carleton College field study (Mischel & Peake, 1982): **63 college students**, conscientiousness
observed in vivo across situations (classroom, dormitory, library) over a semester.

> "As the first set of two columns of Figure 3 show, and as Mischel and Peake (1982) reported,
> **those who perceived themselves as consistent (the first light column) did not show greater
> overall cross-situational consistency than those who did not.** The second set of columns provides
> clear support for the hypothesis of coherence in terms of situation-behavior profiles: **For
> individuals who perceived themselves as consistent, the average situation-behavior profile
> stability correlation was near .5, whereas it was trivial for those who saw themselves as
> inconsistent.**"

> "Thus, the self-perception of consistency seems to be linked to stability in the situation-behavior
> profiles... **If so, the intuition of consistency is neither paradoxical nor illusory: it is based
> on behavioral consistency but not the sort for which the field was searching for so many years.**"

**This is the single most transferable finding for a roleplay eval.** What makes an observer say
"that's the same person" is **profile stability**, NOT cross-situational invariance. Cross-situational
consistency did *not* distinguish the people who read as consistent. Profile stability did (~.5 vs
~0). If the team wants a metric that tracks the human judgment "this character felt like the same
person," the literature says: **measure profile stability, not invariance.**

## The meteorology analogy (p. 247)

> "No doubt overall climatic trends are useful to know, allowing, for example, the accurate
> prediction that in general, San Francisco is cooler and has higher precipitation than Los Angeles
> ... **If meteorologists were to focus only on the aggregate climatic trends however, they would
> constrain their understanding of the atmospheric processes that are responsible for the changing
> weather patterns and forgo the goal of more accurate specific prediction of weather.**"

## The CAPS architecture (why entanglement is the mechanism, not a bug)

The mediating units are **encodings, expectancies and beliefs, affects, goals and values,
competencies and self-regulatory plans**. The theory's core is not the units but **their
organization**:

> "individuals were assumed to differ in (a) the accessibility of cognitive-affective mediating
> units ... and (b) **the organization of relationships through which these units interact with each
> other** and with psychological features of situations." (abstract)

**The personality IS the network of relationships between units.** In CAPS, traits are not separable
modules — a situation activates a *network*, and activation spreads. Perturbing one unit necessarily
propagates. See `psych-metatraits-trait-intercorrelation.md`.

### The simulation (p. 259) — CAPS reproduces signatures from a network

Mischel & Shoda ran a connectionist simulation of **100 hypothetical individuals** across **15
situations**:

> "The model implemented in the computer program produced stable patterns of intraindividual
> variability in behavior for most of the 100 simulated individuals. ... **The average of these
> stability coefficients (computed using Fisher's r-to-z transformation) was 0.66, and 90 of the 100
> hypothetical individuals had stability coefficients higher than 0.20.** (Of course, the exact size
> of the coefficients, but not the sign, depends on the amount of intrinsic unpredictability of
> behavior modeled in the simulation and thus can be increased or decreased readily.)"

Note: **an "individual" here is a set of network activation weights** — i.e. a character sheet as a
weight matrix, not a trait list. Stable if-then signatures **emerge** from the network without being
specified as invariance constraints. This is a direct precedent: the right way to specify a
character is as a *relational network over situation features*, and the right way to evaluate it is
by the **stability of the emergent profile**.

## Caveat the team must respect: situation features must be salient to the character (p. 250)

> "**However, if situation units are defined in terms of features salient for the researcher but
> trivial for, or irrelevant to, the individuals studied, one cannot expect their behaviors to vary
> meaningfully across them, and the resulting pattern of behavior variation therefore would be
> unstable and meaningless.** To discover the potentially predictable patterns of behavior variability
> that characterize individuals, a first step is to identify those features of situations that are
> meaningful to them and that engage their important psychological qualities (e.g., personal
> constructs and goals)."

The camp study **derived** its situation taxonomy empirically (asking children and staff to describe
each other, then cluster-analyzing the situational modifiers like "when someone teases him about his
glasses"). It did not impose one a priori. **A team building an if-then eval must derive the
situation features per character** — from the character sheet's own psychology (what this character
is sensitive to) — not from a generic scenario grid.

## Implications for the team's "character consistency" metric

1. **The metric as specified is measuring the construct the field abandoned in 1968 and formally
   replaced in 1995.** "Same person in turn 3 and turn 97 across many different contexts" is
   *cross-situational consistency*. Mischel & Shoda's whole point is that this is the wrong
   invariant. Deviation-from-sheet-across-contexts is not a defect measure; it is a **realism
   measure with the sign flipped**.

2. **The metric is anti-correlated with the thing they actually want.** Shoda (1990), quoted above:
   "the degree that an individual is characterized by stable patterns of situation-behavior
   relations is **negatively related** to the level of overall cross-situational consistency that can
   be expected." Optimizing the current metric actively selects **against** characters with vivid
   if-then structure. It rewards a character who is uniformly shy at a funeral, a rave, and a job
   interview — which is not a shy person, it is a **broken** one.

3. **The Mischel & Peake reanalysis is the team's validity target.** Perceived consistency tracked
   **profile stability (~.5)**, not cross-situational consistency (**no difference**). If the eval's
   north star is "users feel this is the same person," the metric must be **profile stability**.
   Their current metric is empirically demonstrated *not* to track the human judgment they care
   about.

4. **Concrete replacement — the ipsatized profile-stability design.** Port the Wediko method
   directly:
   - Define **K psychological situation features** relevant to *this* character (derive from the
     sheet: what does this character encode as threat, rejection, invitation, authority?).
   - Run the character through many contexts; score behavior on the sheet's trait dimensions.
   - **Standardize each behavior score within situation type, across the population of characters**
     (this is the crucial step — it removes situation main effects, so "everyone is warmer when
     praised" doesn't count as character signal).
   - Split the session into two non-overlapping halves (Wediko used two independent time samples:
     turns 1–48 vs 49–97 is the natural analogue).
   - **Correlate each character's ipsatized profile across the two halves. That correlation is the
     metric.** Human benchmark: **.47** (verbal aggression), **.41** (compliance) — and the paper
     treats **> .20** as the meaningful floor (90/100 simulated individuals cleared it).
   - Report the *mean* across characters after Fisher r-to-z, as they did.

5. **A model that scores r ≈ .89 on profile stability (Child #9) is not "inconsistent" — it is the
   most strongly characterized entity in the sample.** Under the team's current metric, Child #9
   (aggression z > 2.0 when warned by an adult, z < 0 when approached by a peer) would be flagged
   as a massive character-sheet deviation. He is the paper's *exemplar of personality coherence*.
   **The metric would fire its loudest alarm on its best case.** That is the refutation in one line.

6. **On trait entanglement / crosstalk — CAPS says this IS the mechanism of personality.** The
   theory's defining claim is that personality resides in "the organization of relationships through
   which these units interact **with each other**." A system where perturbing "shy" leaves everything
   else untouched is not a personality — it is a lookup table. **Independent composability of traits
   is the null model CAPS was written to reject.** The 100-individual simulation shows stable
   signatures *emerging from* an interacting weight network; you could not get signatures out of an
   orthogonal one. If the team's model shows crosstalk when "shy" is perturbed, the CAPS-correct
   question is not "how do we eliminate it?" but **"is the crosstalk pattern stable and
   character-appropriate?"** Stable, sheet-consistent crosstalk = a working personality system.
   Random, sheet-inconsistent crosstalk = a real defect. **The current metric cannot tell these two
   apart**, which is its deepest failure.

7. **Turn 3 vs turn 97 is a legitimate question — but only for the *mean*, not the act.** Nothing in
   CAPS says drift over time is fine. It says *variation across situations* is not drift. The team
   should decompose: variance across **situations** (signal — keep, and check its stability) vs
   variance across **time within situation type** (candidate drift — this is where turn 3 vs turn 97
   belongs). Their current metric confounds these two into one number. See
   `psych-fleeson-whole-trait-density.md` for the state/mean decomposition that makes this precise.
