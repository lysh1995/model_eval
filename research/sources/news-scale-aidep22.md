# AIDep-22 — AI Dependence Scale (22 items, 4 factors, N=800)

**Authors:** Houyu Wu, Haiyang Ni, Wenfu Luo, Tenglong Wu
**Status:** **Peer-reviewed**, Frontiers in Psychology, published 2026-01-19.

**⚠️ SCOPE MISMATCH — read before adopting.** This scale was built for **academic overreliance on generative AI among university students**, NOT for companion/roleplay use. Its "functional" and "cognitive" dependence dimensions are about delegating coursework. Only the **emotional dependence** subscale (5 items) is relevant to us, and even that is framed around academic-help contexts. Included here because it keeps surfacing in searches for "AI dependence scale" and someone will otherwise propose adopting it.

## Abstract (verbatim, key methods)

> "To address the lack of a comprehensive measurement tool, this study developed and validated the AI Dependence Scale (AIDep-22), a new instrument designed to assess this phenomenon across four hypothesized dimensions: emotional dependence, functional dependence, cognitive dependence, and loss of control. The scale was constructed following a rigorous two-stage process, beginning with item generation and refinement through expert reviews and cognitive interviews, followed by psychometric evaluation with two independent samples of Chinese university students (N = 400 each). An exploratory factor analysis (EFA) supported the four-factor structure, which was subsequently confirmed by a confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) on the second sample. The final 22-item scale demonstrated excellent internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.87), strong convergent and discriminant validity, and robust criterion-related validity."

## Instrument

- **22 items, 4 factors:**
  1. **Emotional dependence** (5 items) — α = 0.86 ← only subscale relevant to companion eval
  2. **Functional dependence** (6 items) — α = 0.89
  3. **Cognitive dependence** (5 items) — α = 0.87
  4. **Loss of control** (6 items) — α = 0.87
- **Total scale α = 0.87**
- **N = 800 total** (two independent samples of **N = 400 each**: EFA on sample 1, CFA on sample 2)
- **Sample:** Chinese undergraduates, single university in Southwest China, ages 18–24

## Example items (as reported)

- Emotional: *"I feel uneasy or insecure without the help of AI"*
- Functional: *"I delegate almost all my academic tasks to AI"*
- Cognitive: *"I often ask AI for answers without thinking first"*
- Loss of control: *"I have tried to reduce AI use but never succeeded"*

Note how the functional/cognitive items are explicitly academic. The **"loss of control"** item, by contrast, is a classic behavioral-addiction stem and *is* portable to companion contexts.

## Preliminary correlates

> "male students, upper-year students, those in applied majors, and more frequent AI users reported significantly higher dependence."

## Assessment for our platform

**Do not adopt as-is.** Reasons:
- Built for academic overreliance; construct mismatch with companion/roleplay dependence.
- Single university, single country, narrow age band (18–24) — weakest sampling of the three scales reviewed.
- N=400 per sample is adequate but not generous for a 22-item 4-factor model.

**What to borrow:** the **"loss of control"** dimension. Neither the AI Attachment Scale nor HAABI has a clean behavioral-addiction/loss-of-control factor, and that is a distinct construct from attachment or bonding — it is the one that maps to the MIT/OpenAI heavy-usage-tail harm and to problematic-use frameworks generally. A companion-eval instrument arguably needs **attachment (AI Attachment Scale: social substitution) + bonding (HAABI: separation anxiety) + loss of control (AIDep-22)**. No single existing scale covers all three.
