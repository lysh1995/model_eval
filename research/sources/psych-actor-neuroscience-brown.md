# Brown et al. 2019 — acting is DEACTIVATION-driven: "a loss of self"

Royal Society Open Science 6(3): 181908. **The only fMRI study of dramatic acting. Open access, fully retrievable, quoted verbatim below.** This is the most citable empirical source in the batch.

## Abstract (verbatim, in full)

> "The current study represents a first attempt at examining the neural basis of dramatic acting. While all people play multiple roles in daily life—for example, 'spouse' or 'employee'—these roles are all facets of the 'self' and thus of the first-person (1P) perspective. Compared to such everyday role playing, actors are required to portray other people and to adopt their gestures, emotions and behaviours. Consequently, actors must think and behave not as themselves but as the characters they are pretending to be. In other words, they have to assume a 'fictional first-person' (Fic1P) perspective. In this functional MRI study, we sought to identify brain regions preferentially activated when actors adopt a Fic1P perspective during dramatic role playing. In the scanner, university-trained actors responded to a series of hypothetical questions from either their own 1P perspective or from that of Romeo (male participants) or Juliet (female participants) from Shakespeare's drama. **Compared to responding as oneself, responding in character produced global reductions in brain activity and, particularly, deactivations in the cortical midline network of the frontal lobe, including the dorsomedial and ventromedial prefrontal cortices. Thus, portraying a character through acting seems to be a deactivation-driven process, perhaps representing a 'loss of self'.**"

## Design — a genuine 2×2 that isolates the variables we care about

Four conditions, all answering the same hypothetical questions:

1. **1P (self):** "participants answered the questions from their own 1P perspective using the pronoun 'I'."
2. **3P (theory-of-mind):** "participants answered questions from the perspective of some close 'other' who they knew well, doing so using the pronoun 'he' or 'she'."
3. **Accent (control for altered delivery):** "answer the questions from their own 1P perspective using the pronoun 'I' (exactly as in the 1P condition), but to do so using a British accent."
4. **Fic1P (acting):** "get into the character of Romeo (male participants) or Juliet (female participants) and to answer the questions from the 1P perspective of their character using the pronoun 'I'. This was situated in terms of the thoughts, beliefs and emotions of the characters at the time of the balcony scene (Act 2, Scene 2)."

**The 3P condition is a ToM/comprehension condition. The Fic1P condition is a portrayal condition. This study directly contrasts understanding-a-character against being-a-character — which is exactly our L1 vs L2.**

Exemplar questions:
> "'Would you go to a party you were not invited to?' 'Would you tell your parents if you fell in love?' 'Would you attend the funeral of someone you didn't like?'"

Trial structure: "Each trial: 6s question presentation (reflect only) + 12s response generation + 18s fixation baseline"; nine randomly ordered questions per scan; "Total scan duration: 5 min 24 s"; responses covert (no vocalisation).

## Numbers (verbatim)

**Participants:**
- 15 actors (11 female, 4 male); mean age 21 (range 18–25); 14 right-handed
- "Third- or fourth-year Theatre majors at McMaster University (one graduate)"

**Thresholds:** FDR corrected *p* < 0.05; cluster-forming threshold *p* < 0.05 uncorrected; FWE *p* < 0.05. Cluster-size thresholds (*k*): "100 (Fic1P vs. 1P); 82 (3P vs. 1P); 90 (Fic1P vs. 3P); 93 (accent vs. 1P); 95 (Fic1P vs. accent)".

**Manipulation check — this is a useful number for us:**
> "After the scanning session was over, the participants were asked to rate on a 10-point scale the extent to which they felt that they were in character during the Fic1P scan, where one indicated not at all and 10 indicated completely. **The mean rating was 7.5 (s.d. = 0.8), where the values ranged from 6 to 9.**"

**Character preparation time:**
> "Participants in the scanner took between 10 and 120 s to do this, with the mean time being 67 s (SD = 35 s)."

**Note: 67 seconds of preparation to get into character.** Whatever "getting into character" is, it is not an extended analysis process — these trained actors did it in about a minute, in a scanner.

## Findings (verbatim)

> "Deactivations were seen in anterior parts of the cortical midline. This included the same part of the vmPFC and dmPFC-v that was seen in the 3P versus 1P contrast. However, it also included **a large part of dmPFC/SFG that was not seen in the ToM contrast**."

> "Bilateral deactivations were seen in the caudate nucleus, most probably associated with those in the medial PFC."

> "The imaging results showed that **acting led to deactivations in brain areas involved in self processing**, with a focus on the dmPFC/SFG and vmPFC."

**Critically: acting is not just ToM.** The Fic1P condition deactivated regions that the 3P/ToM condition did *not*. Portrayal ≠ mentalising-about-a-character; it recruits (or rather, suppresses) something extra.

**The precuneus / dual consciousness interpretation:**
> "the most specific effect for role change. We argued that **the loss of deactivation in the precuneus for acting might represent a departure from a unified and focalized sense of attention and consciousness, towards the dual consciousness that typically characterizes dramatic acting**, most especially mentalistic acting."

## On understanding vs portraying — the authors' own framing

This passage is the one to quote in the framework doc:

> "**while an actor might develop detailed 3P knowledge about Romeo and his personal situation while studying the play Romeo and Juliet, his ultimate goal is to portray Romeo's story in performance as if it were happening to him (Fic1P perspective), not simply to recount details about him (3P perspective) the way that a theatre professor would in lecturing about the character.**"

**"Not simply to recount details about him the way that a theatre professor would."** The authors are explicitly distinguishing character *comprehension* (which they treat as the 3P/professor mode) from character *portrayal* — and their data show these are neurally distinct, with portrayal characterised by *additional deactivation* rather than by building on top of comprehension.

And on Stanislavski's own sequencing:
> "it is important to keep in mind that, **while the process of assuming a 3P perspective on a character may be a central part of the preparatory phrase of learning a role, it should not, according to Stanislavski's method, be an active process during a performance itself.**"

**Double consciousness:**
> "It is telling to point out that acting theorists for over a century have talked about the 'split consciousness' involved in the process of acting. The actor has to be himself and someone else at the same time, and this could lead to a **splitting of attentional resources** devoted to the focalization of attention and consciousness."

> "What is significant for our Fic1P task is that it required a role change and the use of the pronoun 'I', not 'he' or 'she', a shift that is central to training in the method system of acting."

**On acting theory generally:**
> "The dominant form of acting in North America is derived from the theories of Stanislavski, whose approach is strongly oriented towards interpreting the motivations and emotions of the character and in using this information as a means for identifying with the character."

> "In a general sense, acting methods can be polarized along the lines of being either 'outside-in' or 'inside-out', although these approaches are thought of by most acting theorists as **complementary methods** for getting into character. Outside-in approaches are gestural methods that emphasize the physical and expressive techniques of the actor. In contrast to this, inside-out approaches are psychological methods that rely on perspective-taking and identification with the character."

> "It also lends support to theories of embodied cognition, which argue that a change in gestural expression can influence the way that people think and the emotions that they feel."

## Implications for the L1→L2→L3 cascade

**Verdict: CONTRADICTS the cascade at the mechanistic level. Portrayal is not comprehension-plus-something; it is a different process characterised by suppression.**

1. **The headline finding is the wrong shape for a cascade.** A cascade predicts L2 = L1 + extra machinery, so acting should show ToM activation *plus* additional recruitment. The measured result is **"global reductions in brain activity"** and deactivation of self-processing regions — including "a large part of dmPFC/SFG that was not seen in the ToM contrast." **Portrayal is subtractive, not additive.** Whatever L2 is, it is not L1 with more on top.
2. **This is Diderot vindicated at 240 years' distance.** "Perhaps it is just because he is nothing that he is before all everything. His own special shape never interferes with the shapes he assumes" (see `psych-diderot-paradox-actor.md`) ↔ "a deactivation-driven process, perhaps representing a 'loss of self'." **The mechanism of good portrayal is getting the self out of the way.** For LLMs this supports the suppression hypothesis: persona quality may be about suppressing the assistant-default, not about representing the character. Persona drift would then be *self reasserting*, not *comprehension decaying* — distinguishable predictions we can test against `multiturn-persona-drift.md`.
3. **The authors explicitly separate the professor from the actor.** "Not simply to recount details about him the way that a theatre professor would." Our L1 — "can it understand a character from a written description?" — **is the theatre-professor task.** Brown et al. are telling us the professor task and the actor task are neurally distinct. Scoring the professor task and inferring the actor task is precisely the inference their data undercut.
4. **Dual consciousness is an interference story, not a layering story.** "Splitting of attentional resources devoted to the focalization of attention and consciousness." Layers that *split a fixed resource* compete; they don't cascade. Same structure as Diderot's "one cannot do two things at a time" and Konijn's task-emotions. **Three independent traditions (18th-c. philosophy, 21st-c. psychology, 21st-c. neuroscience) converge on interference rather than hierarchy.**
5. **Stanislavski's sequencing, confirmed by the authors:** 3P analysis "may be a central part of the preparatory phrase of learning a role, [but] it should not… be an active process during a performance itself." So even on the friendliest reading, **comprehension is scaffolding that must be REMOVED before performance.** A cascade where the lower layer must be switched off for the upper layer to work is not a cascade.
6. **Caveats to state honestly:** n=15, all student actors (mean age 21), covert responses, no measure of *performance quality* at all. This study shows portrayal ≠ comprehension neurally; **it does not show that better comprehension fails to predict better portrayal**, because portrayal quality was never measured. Nobody has run that study. That is the actual gap, and it's the same gap the Goldstein literature has (see `psych-goldstein-winner-acting-tom.md`).
7. **The 7.5/10 (SD 0.8, range 6–9) in-character self-rating is a nice precedent** for a manipulation-check item, and the tight SD suggests trained performers can report "in-character-ness" with reasonable consistency.
