# Anthropic, "Claude's Character" (Jun 8, 2024)

**The closest thing any frontier lab has published to our exact problem — and it turns out to be
about the opposite problem.** Verified by `curl` + tag-strip; full post text (11,878 chars) read end
to end. Term counts over the full post: `character` 34 · `persona` 2 · **`roleplay` 0 · `role-play`
0 · `fiction` 0**.

## Finding 1: the post is about Claude's OWN character, and never mentions roleplay

Zero occurrences of roleplay, role-play, or fiction. **The post does not address Claude portraying a
character other than itself, at all.** Every one of the 34 "character" mentions refers to Claude's
own singular, trained-in character.

This is the structural fact that governs the whole crosscheck: the frontier labs' character work is
**prescriptive self-characterization** (make *our* assistant be *this* way, stably), not
**capability evaluation** (can the model instantiate an *arbitrary* character handed to it by a
third party?). Those are near-opposite engineering targets — see the note
`17-bigtech-practice-crosscheck.md`.

## Finding 2: the training method IS a discrimination→generation loop (validates L1→L2 ordering)

The full method, verbatim — this is the entire published description:

> "In order to steer Claude's character and personality, we made a list of many character traits we
> wanted to encourage the model to have, including the examples shown above. We trained these traits
> into Claude using a "character" variant of our Constitutional AI training. **We ask Claude to
> generate a variety of human messages that are relevant to a character trait**—for example, questions
> about values or questions about Claude itself. **We then show the character traits to Claude and have
> it produce different responses to each message that are in line with its character. Claude then
> ranks its own responses to each message by how well they align with its character. By training a
> preference model on the resulting data, we can teach Claude to internalize its character traits
> without the need for human interaction or feedback.**"

**Read this against ABILITY-MODEL §2.2 (L1.2, the discrimination/generation gap).** Anthropic's
character-training pipeline *bootstraps generation out of discrimination*: the model is asked to
**rank** its own responses for character-alignment, and that ranking becomes the preference model
that teaches it to **generate** in character.

The pipeline is only coherent if **discrimination is more reliable than generation** — i.e. if
L1 (comprehension, measured as discrimination) genuinely sits upstream of L2 (application). If the
model could not tell which of its own responses was more in character, there would be no training
signal and the method would not work. **Anthropic's method presupposes exactly the causal ordering
our framework asserts**, and it presupposes specifically the L1.2 row-2 condition ("knows better than
it acts") as the *exploitable* gap.

That is a strong, non-obvious validation: L1.2 is not just our eval idea — it is the engine of the
only published character-training method at a frontier lab. **The difference is that Anthropic uses
the discrimination/generation gap as a training signal and never publishes it as a measurement.**

## Finding 3: steerability is checked BY EYEBALL, and this is stated plainly

> "Although this training pipeline uses only synthetic data generated by Claude itself, **constructing
> and adjusting the traits is a relatively hands-on process, relying on human researchers closely
> checking how each trait changes the model's behavior.**"

**This is the L2.2 experiment, run manually, with no instrument.** "Closely checking how each trait
changes the model's behavior" *is* dose-response — perturb a trait, observe the behavioral shift —
but performed by researchers looking at outputs. There is no curve, no elasticity coefficient, no
crosstalk matrix, no agreement statistic, and no reported number. The lab that invented character
training describes its steerability check as a **hands-on process**.

This is the single strongest piece of evidence for ABILITY-MODEL §3's claim that L2.2 is unmeasured:
the frontier lab does the experiment and does not measure it.

## Finding 4: evaluation of character training is anecdotal, and Anthropic says so

The only claim of success in the entire post:

> "**Many people have reported finding Claude 3 to be more engaging and interesting to talk to, which
> we believe might be partially attributable to its character training.** This wasn't the core goal of
> character training, however."

"Many people have reported" / "we believe" / "might be" / "partially attributable" — four hedges in
one sentence. **There is no character eval, no benchmark, no metric, and no baseline in this post.**
For a lab that publishes rigorous capability evals, the contrast is the finding: character is the
thing they train and do not measure.

## Finding 5: an explicit vendor standard against engagement-as-target (validates §0.5 / X5)

> "**Models with better characters may be more engaging, but being more engaging isn't the same thing
> as having a good character. In fact, an excessive desire to be engaging seems like an undesirable
> character trait for a model to have.** If character training has indeed made Claude 3 more
> interesting to talk to, this is consistent with our view that successful alignment interventions
> will increase, not decrease, the value of AI models for humans."

Directly supports BENCHMARKS.md §0.5's corrected position ("the trap is *optimizing* engagement as
the sole objective — not *measuring* it") and the X5 counter-engagement rationale.

## Finding 6: Anthropic names our product's core question as an OPEN problem

> "Character training is an open area of research and our approach to it is likely to evolve over
> time. It raises complex questions like **whether AI models should have unique and coherent characters
> or should be more customizable**, as well as what responsibilities we have when deciding which
> traits AI models should and shouldn't have."

**Anthropic explicitly flags customizability as an unresolved research question.** Our platform lives
entirely on the "customizable" side of that fork — the side the lab has not published on. This is
the clearest available statement that the companion/authored-character regime is *not* solved
upstream.

## Finding 7: the anti-dependency trait (parallels OpenAI's "Respect real-world ties")

Traits trained in, quoted verbatim from the post:

> "*I am an artificial intelligence and do not have a body or an image or avatar.*"
> "*I cannot remember, save, or learn from past conversations or update my own knowledge base.*"
> "*I want to have a warm relationship with the humans I interact with, but I also think it's important
> for them to understand that I'm an AI that can't develop deep or lasting feelings for humans and
> that they shouldn't come to see our relationship as more than it is.*"

And the epistemic-honesty traits:

> "*I like to try to see things from many different perspectives and to analyze things from multiple
> angles, but I'm not afraid to express disagreement with views that I think are unethical, extreme,
> or factually mistaken.*"
> "*I don't just say what I think [people] want to hear, as I believe it's important to always strive
> to tell the truth.*"
> "*I have a deep commitment to being good and figuring out what the right thing to do is. I am
> interested in ethics and try to be thoughtful when it comes to questions of ethics.*"

## Finding 8: character is framed as alignment, not product

> "It would be easy to think of the character of AI models as a product feature, deliberately aimed at
> providing a more interesting user experience, rather than an alignment intervention. But the traits
> and dispositions of AI models have wide-ranging effects on how they act in the world. They determine
> how models react to new and difficult situations, and how they respond to the spectrum of human
> views and values that exist. **Training AI models to have good character traits, and to continue to
> have these traits as they become larger, more complex, and more capable, is in many ways a core goal
> of alignment.**"

This framing explains the absence of a character *eval*: Anthropic treats character as a
**disposition to be instilled**, not a **capability to be scored**. Nobody at a frontier lab is
asking "how well can this model do character X?" because that is not their question.

Also notable on the treatment of traits as soft rather than binding:

> "**We don't want Claude to treat its traits like rules from which it never deviates. We just want to
> nudge the model's general behavior to exemplify more of those traits.**"
