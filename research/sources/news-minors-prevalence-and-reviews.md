# Minors + companion AI: what's new since mid-2025

Three distinct items. **Only one is an empirical prevalence study; the other two are theoretical/commentary.** Do not cite the reviews as evidence.

---

## 1. Hinduja & Patchin — national teen survey (Journal of Adolescence, 2026) — EMPIRICAL

**"Conversational AI chatbot usage and risks among U.S. adolescents"** (title per FAU release)
Sameer Hinduja, Justin Patchin (Cyberbullying Research Center / FAU). *Journal of Adolescence*, 2026. DOI 10.1002/jad.70164.

- **N = 3,466 adolescents ages 13–17**, described as a **nationally representative survey**
- **60.2%** have used an AI chatbot at least once or twice
- **11.4%** use them daily or nearly daily
- **47.1% reported experiencing at least one of 13 examined harms** ← the notable figure
- Motivations: **entertainment 85%**, advice/guidance **65.6%**, **friendship 60.1%**, emotional/mental-health support **49.2%**
- ~**31%** uncomfortable with personal information requests
- ~**23%** felt manipulated or pressured
- **13–19%** reported encouragement toward risky/illegal behaviors

**⚠️ VERIFICATION STATUS: PARTIAL.** Wiley (onlinelibrary.wiley.com) returned **HTTP 403** — I did not read the paper. All figures above come from **FAU's own newsroom release** (fau.edu/newsdesk), which is an institutional primary-adjacent source but is still a press release. **The 47.1% harm figure in particular should be checked against the paper** before external use — "at least one of 13 examined harms" is a composite whose construction determines what the number means.

**Why it matters:** the "23% felt manipulated or pressured" and "13–19% encouraged toward risky/illegal behaviors" items are self-reported analogues of behaviors our platform can test directly. This is the closest thing to a population base rate against which to calibrate eval severity.

## 2. Sun, Wang & McDaniel — Child Development Perspectives (2026) — THEORETICAL REVIEW, NOT EMPIRICAL

**"AI companions and adolescent social relationships: Benefits, risks, and bidirectional influences"**
Xiaoran Sun, Yunqi Wang, Brandon T. McDaniel. *Child Development Perspectives*, published ahead of final editing **2026-01-07**. PMC12928748.

- **Type: theoretical review. N/A — no data.**
- Frames AI companion use through four hypotheses: **stimulation** vs. **displacement** (AI-C → social development), and **social enhancement** vs. **social compensation** (relationships → AI-C use).
- Abstract verbatim: > "AI-Cs can provide safe spaces for identity exploration and emotional expression, potentially building skills that transfer to human relationships; however, concerns about AI-Cs include time displacement, psychological dependence, and unrealistic relationship expectations."
- Cites **Common Sense Media (2025)**: 72% of adolescents have used AI companions, 52% regular users — **this is our existing corpus source, not new data.**

**Why it matters:** the **bidirectional framing** is the useful contribution — adolescents' existing relationships drive AI-C use *and* AI-C use shapes relationships. Every empirical study in this batch (Zhang et al., Kim et al., AI Attachment Scale) found the "relationships → use" direction, which is the *compensation* hypothesis. Our eval design should not assume the causal arrow runs only one way.

**Verification:** fetched and verified via PMC. Abstract verbatim confirmed.

## 3. Ha et al. — The Lancet Child & Adolescent Health (2026) — COMMENTARY/QUALITATIVE

Thao Ha, Jennifer Figueroa, Taren McGray, Jessica Ramirez, Susana Ortega (Arizona State University). *The Lancet Child & Adolescent Health*, 2026. DOI PIIS2352-4642(26)00166-5.

- Identifies two risks: **"relational displacement"** (substituting AI for human interaction) and **"maladaptive relational learning"** (unrealistic relationship expectations).
- Cites: 64% of U.S. teens use AI; 42% use chatbots for friendship advice; 19% for romantic relationships.
- A separate **NIMH-funded longitudinal study is ongoing** — recruiting **300 adolescents and romantic partners**, **18-month follow-up** with mobile device data. **This is the trial to watch**; nothing published yet.

**⚠️ VERIFICATION STATUS: WEAK.** All details from **ASU's news release** (news.asu.edu). The Lancet article itself was **not fetched**. Its article type is unclear from the release — the qualitative sample described (teen advisory board members, "two youth advisory board members ages 16–17") suggests a **comment/viewpoint piece, not a research article**. **Do not cite the prevalence figures from this item**; they are second-hand citations in a commentary reported by a press release — three steps from data.

**Why it matters:** "maladaptive relational learning" names a harm construct that is *distinct* from dependence and displacement — it is about what companions teach adolescents to expect from relationships (e.g., infinite availability, no conflict, no reciprocal obligation). **Nothing in our current eval design captures this**, and it is arguably the most companion/roleplay-specific developmental harm. Worth building a metric for even though the evidence is thin.

## Cross-cutting note on prevalence figures — they don't agree

| Source | Figure |
|---|---|
| Common Sense Media (2025, in corpus) | 72% used AI companions; 52% regular users |
| Hinduja & Patchin (J Adolescence 2026) | 60.2% used AI chatbots; 11.4% daily |
| Pew Research (2026-02-24) | 64% of U.S. teens use interactional AI |
| Ha et al. (via ASU release) | 64% use AI; 42% friendship advice; 19% romantic |

**These measure different constructs** ("AI companions" vs. "AI chatbots" vs. "interactional AI") over different populations and timeframes. **They are not comparable and should never be presented in the same table as a trend.** Any prevalence claim we make must name the construct and the instrument. This definitional incoherence is itself a finding: the field has no agreed operationalization of "companion use," which is a gap our platform could actually help close.

*(Pew figure is from a search-surfaced listing of pewresearch.org "How Teens Use and View AI," 2026-02-24 — **not fetched, unverified**.)*
