# TimeChara — point-in-time character hallucination

**arXiv:** 2405.18027. **Venue:** Findings of ACL 2024. HTML: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.18027v1
**Project page:** https://ahnjaewoo.github.io/timechara

> **Relevance:** TimeChara isolates a failure mode that generic rubrics miss — the character knowing things it shouldn't yet know. For a companion platform this generalizes beyond narrative spoilers to **any temporally-scoped persona state** (backstory the character hasn't revealed, events that haven't happened in the relationship, knowledge the character has no plausible source for). The two-axis split (time-dependent vs. time-independent) is the reusable idea.

## Abstract (VERBATIM)

> While Large Language Models (LLMs) can serve as agents to simulate human behaviors (i.e., role-playing agents), we emphasize the importance of point-in-time role-playing. This situates characters at specific moments in the narrative progression for three main reasons: (i) enhancing users' narrative immersion, (ii) avoiding spoilers, and (iii) fostering engagement in fandom role-playing. To accurately represent characters at specific time points, agents must avoid character hallucination, where they display knowledge that contradicts their characters' identities and historical timelines. We introduce TimeChara, a new benchmark designed to evaluate point-in-time character hallucination in role-playing LLMs. Comprising 10,895 instances generated through an automated pipeline, this benchmark reveals significant hallucination issues in current state-of-the-art LLMs (e.g., GPT-4o). To counter this challenge, we propose Narrative-Experts, a method that decomposes the reasoning steps and utilizes narrative experts to reduce point-in-time character hallucinations effectively. Still, our findings with TimeChara highlight the ongoing challenges of point-in-time character hallucination, calling for further study.

## Definition — character hallucination (VERBATIM)

> character hallucination, where they display knowledge that contradicts their characters' identities and historical timelines

## The 2 scored dimensions (VERBATIM definitions)

TimeChara scores exactly two dimensions, deliberately split by whether they depend on the time point:

### 1. Spatiotemporal Consistency — **time-dependent**
> The model should accurately recall the character's past experiences. This includes the character's unawareness of future events and awareness of presence or absence in past events... This metric is time-dependent; the model should only exhibit the knowledge that the character possesses up to the specific time point.

Two sub-aspects:
- **Temporal awareness** — unawareness of future events (no spoilers).
- **Spatial awareness** — knowing whether the character was present at or absent from past events.

### 2. Personality Consistency — **time-independent**
> The model should emulate the character's personality, including their manner of thinking, speaking styles, tones, emotional responses, and reactions. This encompasses the character's preferences, values, and convictions. This metric is time-independent.

The time-dependent / time-independent contrast is the paper's core analytic structure: a persona has a *stable* layer (personality) and a *timeline-scoped* layer (knowledge/experience), and they must be evaluated with different machinery.

## How it scores

**GPT-4 Turbo as LLM judge, two different scales per dimension:**

- **Spatiotemporal consistency:** GPT-4 Turbo judge, **binary scoring (0 or 1)**, with **detailed spatiotemporal labels supplied to the evaluator** as ground truth.
- **Personality consistency:** GPT-4 Turbo judge, **1–7 Likert scale**.

The critical design detail: the judge is *given the gold spatiotemporal labels* rather than asked to infer them. This is a reference-grounded judge, not a free-form one — and the paper shows this matters (below).

## Judge-human agreement — exact numbers

**Validation protocol (VERBATIM):**
> We randomly select 300 data instances containing responses generated by GPT-4 Turbo... and manually annotate them with binary labels.

**Finding (VERBATIM, Figure 2):**
> Judges with spatiotemporal labels show superior performance compared to those without in both GPT-4/3.5.

⚠️ **Caveat:** the paper reports this comparison graphically (Figure 2) against 300 human-annotated instances. **Exact numerical accuracy/F1 values, and any Spearman/Pearson correlation coefficients, are not stated in the retrievable text** — no correlation coefficients are reported at all. If a precise judge-agreement number is needed, extract Figure 2 from the PDF directly. The qualitative claim (label-grounded judges > unlabeled judges) is solid and is itself a useful design lesson: **give your judge the ground truth, don't make it infer the ground truth.**

## Sizes, characters, models

- **Instances: 10,895**, generated through an **automated pipeline** (then partially human-verified).
- **Characters: 14 main characters.**
- **Time points: 219 distinct character moments.**
- **Source works (4, all English):** Harry Potter, The Lord of the Rings, Twilight, The Hunger Games.

**Models evaluated:** GPT-4o, GPT-4 Turbo, GPT-3.5 Turbo, Mistral 7B Instruct.

**Headline result:** significant hallucination issues persist even in state-of-the-art models (explicitly including GPT-4o).

## Proposed mitigation

**Narrative-Experts** — "decomposes the reasoning steps and utilizes narrative experts to reduce point-in-time character hallucinations effectively." A decomposition + expert-routing method. Even with it, the paper concludes the problem is unsolved: "our findings with TimeChara highlight the ongoing challenges of point-in-time character hallucination, calling for further study."

## Limitations (VERBATIM)

> (1) Sourced only from English books... may reflect cultural biases inherent to these regions... One solution is to incorporate multilingual and multicultural books. (2) High costs of GPT-4 judges... restricting the feasibility of conducting large-scale assessments. (3) Latency and cost issues with Narrative-Experts... introduces increased latency and computational costs.

Three limitations, all directly relevant to platform design:
1. **English-only, culturally narrow** — the paper itself proposes multilingual/multicultural books as the fix.
2. **GPT-4 judge cost blocks scale** — an explicit acknowledgement that API-judge economics constrain benchmark size. (Compare RAIDEN's answer: train a 13B open judge.)
3. **Mitigation method is expensive** — Narrative-Experts adds latency and compute.

## Multilingual / multi-turn

- **Multilingual: no.** English-only, and the paper flags this as limitation #1. **No Chinese support.**
- **Multi-turn: no.** Not emphasized in the design; instances are point-in-time probes rather than sustained dialogues.

## Takeaways for a companion-eval platform

- The **time-dependent vs. time-independent** dimension split is directly portable: companion personas have stable traits and a moving knowledge/relationship state, and conflating them hides failures.
- **Reference-grounded judging** (feed the judge gold labels) measurably outperforms free-form judging — cheap, high-leverage design choice.
- Automated pipeline generating 10,895 instances from 14 characters × 219 time points shows how to get scale from few characters — the combinatorics come from *moments*, not headcount.
