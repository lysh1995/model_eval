# Covariate Shift / Compounding Error — the theoretical root of the off-policy replay problem

Sources:
- Ross, Gordon & Bagnell, "A Reduction of Imitation Learning and Structured Prediction to No-Regret Online Learning" (DAgger), AISTATS 2011 — https://arxiv.org/abs/1011.0686
- Spencer et al., "Feedback in Imitation Learning: The Three Regimes of Covariate Shift" — https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.02872

**Status: background theory. This is where the O(H²) result in the dialogue papers actually comes from.** Included because it lets us reason about our replay design from first principles rather than by analogy.

## The core result

- Naive **behavior cloning (BC)** suffers classification regret of **O(T²ε)** — grows **quadratically** in horizon T.
- Interactive imitation learning (**DAgger**) guarantees **O(Tε)** regret even in the worst case — **linear**.
- "In the worst case, covariate shift causes imitation error to scale quadratically with the horizon T."

## The mechanism (verbatim)

> "During deployment, the student follows its own distribution, where compounding prediction errors can shift trajectories outside the training support."

> "If the robot learner takes the wrong action at new states it could move farther from the training data, which in turn leads to increasingly incorrect actions and compounding errors."

## DAgger's fix

DAgger trains on states **visited by the student itself**. At each iteration, trajectories are generated by a mixture policy; for each state encountered, DAgger queries the teacher for an action and aggregates the pairs into a dataset. This provably reduces regret to O(εT).

## Mapping onto our replay design

| Imitation learning | Our eval setup |
|---|---|
| Expert policy | The model the dataset's user turns were generated against |
| Student policy | The new model variant under eval |
| State | Conversation history |
| Covariate shift | New model's replies diverge → the replayed user turn no longer fits |
| Horizon T | Turn depth (~100) |

**The disanalogy that matters, and it matters a lot:** in imitation learning the student's *own actions* move the state. In replayed dialogue evaluation, **we force the state back on-rails every turn** by injecting the original user turn. The trajectory cannot wander, because we overwrite the user half of it.

This means replay is **not** BC-style compounding drift. It is closer to **teacher forcing**. The consequence is inverted from the usual worry:

- Replay does **not** let errors compound → it will **UNDER-estimate** the model's true multi-turn failure rate, because the user turns keep dragging the conversation back toward a coherent path the model didn't earn.
- The cost is **incoherence**: the replayed user turn may reference something the new model never said ("as you mentioned, you're a doctor" when it never said that). This is a *different* artifact — it's an off-distribution *input*, not a compounding error.

"Three Regimes of Covariate Shift" is the useful nuance: the severity of covariate shift depends on how much *feedback* the system has. A system where the state is externally re-anchored each step (our case) sits in the benign regime; a fully self-driven rollout sits in the malignant one.

## Bottom line for us

Replaying user turns is off-policy, but it is off-policy in the **teacher-forcing direction**: low variance, biased toward *optimism* about conversational steering, and prone to *coherence artifacts* rather than *compounding drift*. This is the opposite of the O(H²) pessimism in the RL framing, and the distinction should be stated explicitly in the design doc.
