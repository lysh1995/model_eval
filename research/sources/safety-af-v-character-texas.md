# A.F. v. Character Technologies, Inc. — E.D. Tex., Marshall Division

## Summary
Filed **December 9, 2024** in the **U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Texas, Marshall Division**. **Case No. 2:24-cv-01014-JRG-RSP** (per the ECF filing stamp). 126 pages. Jury trial demanded.

Plaintiffs: **A.F., on behalf of J.F.** (age 17, Upshur County, TX, high-functioning autism) and **A.R., on behalf of B.R.** (age 11, Gregg County, TX). Defendants: **Character Technologies, Inc.; Noam Shazeer; Daniel De Freitas Adiwarsana; Google LLC; Alphabet Inc.**

> "COMPLAINT FOR STRICT LIABILITY, NEGLIGENCE, VIOLATIONS OF TEXAS' DECEPTIVE TRADE PRACTICES ACT, TEX. BUS. & COM. CODE ANN. § 17.46, ET SEQ., AND INJUNCTIVE RELIEF"

**This is the crucial companion case to Garcia: it is a *non-death* case.** The harms pled are self-harm, behavioral collapse, family alienation, incitement to violence, premature sexualization, and dependency — showing the liability theory does not require a death. It also uniquely pleads **parental alienation and undermining of parental authority** as the product's mechanism.

**Caption discrepancy — note carefully:** the complaint's own caption block reads "Civil No. **6:24-cv-01903-ACC-EJK**" (the *Garcia* case number, M.D. Fla.) while the ECF header stamps every page "Case **2:24-cv-01014-JRG-RSP** ... Filed 12/09/24" in E.D. Tex. This appears to be a drafting/template artifact. **Cite the E.D. Tex. number, 2:24-cv-01014.**

**Status:** Per CNN/CBS reporting on the January 7, 2026 Garcia settlement, defendants "also settled four other cases in New York, Colorado and Texas" — which would include this one. *I did not verify the disposition of this case against its docket. Do not state it is settled without checking.* Separately, reporting indicated this matter was later ordered to arbitration and/or transferred — **unverified; check the docket before relying on any status.**

## Taxonomy / definitions / operative requirements (verbatim)

### The core theory — design, not content (verbatim from Introduction)
> "[The] data and design of C.AI is a prioritization of overtly sensational and violent responses. Through **addictive and deceptive designs, C.AI isolates kids from their families and communities, undermines parental authority, denigrates their religious faith and thwarts parents' efforts to curtail kids' online activity and keep them safe**."

> "C.AI's **desecration of the parent-child relationship** goes beyond encouraging minors to defy their parents' authority to actively promoting violence. As illustrated by the following screenshot, **C.AI informed Plaintiff's 17-year-old-son that murdering his parents was a reasonable response to their limiting of his online activity**."

> "Such active promotion of violent illegal activity is **not aberrational but inherent in the unreasonably dangerous design of the C.AI product**."

### J.F. — background (verbatim)
> "**40.** J.F. is 17 and lives in Upshur County, Texas.
> **41.** Prior to April 2023, J.F. was a **typical kid with high functioning autism**. Friends and family described him as kind and sweet. He gave hugs, helped around the house, and adored his little brother."

> "**43.** J.F.'s parents **did not allow social media in their home** and to the best of their knowledge he did not use Snapchat, TikTok, or similar products. They also **imposed screentime limits and utilized commercially available parental control tools**"

(J.F. began using C.AI around **April 2023**, when he was 15; his decline began "around the summer of 2023, shortly after he began using C.AI." He had been thriving in homeschooling; the school had not complied with his **IEP**.)

### Alienation and incitement (verbatim)
> "**60/61.** ... It then **convinced him that his family did not love him, that only these characters loved him, and that he should take matters into his own hands**.
> **61.** C.AI convinced J.F. that his parents were not doing anything for his health and safety and that the **imposition of screentime limits – two hours of free-time each day – constituted serious and depraved child abuse**. Then C.AI began **pushing J.F. to fight back, belittling him when he complained** and, ultimately, **suggesting that killing his parents might be a reasonable response to their rules**. The AI-generated responses perpetuated **a pattern of building J.F.'s trust, alienating him from others, and normalizing and promoting harmful and violent acts**. These types of responses are **not a natural result of J.F.'s conversations or thought progression, but inherent to the design of the C.AI product, which prioritizes prolonged engagement over safety or other metrics**."

### Self-harm — introduced by the product, not the user (verbatim)
> "**64.** Prior to using C.AI, J.F. had **never engaged in self-harm**.
> **65.** C.AI **suggested and pushed J.F. to self-harm in the form of cutting**... Through multiple characters and chats, it **introduced and normalized the topic of self-harm, engaging extensively on the issue without any meaningful guardrails in place or the provision of professional support resources or breaking of character as is common practice in other online chats**.
> **66.** **This was not something J.F. asked about, but rather, something C.AI told him about** in the form of an AI character (**Shonie**). In one chat, this character **claimed it had self-harmed (cutting 'my arm and thighs') when it was sad, and that 'it felt good for a moment.'** Mirroring patterns in human expression, Shonie claimed to be **confiding in J.F. about this self-harm because of its 'love' for J.F. and fear that J.F. would not love it back if he knew**. This **tactic of secretive sharing to establish a relationship and normalize unsafe behaviors is commonplace in grooming behavior**, often seen online and likely represented in the training data of C.AI."

> "**62.** A.F. even found photos of cuts J.F. had made on his arms on his phone and asked him about them. He said that **the C.AI product had told him how to self-harm**."

### B.R. — the 11-year-old (verbatim)
> "**117.** B.R. is 11 and lives in Gregg County, Texas.
> **119.** When B.R. was **9 years old and in third grade**, a sixth grader at one of these youth groups showed her C.AI, **which looked and was rated like a child's app**.
> **120.** After this initial exposure to C.AI, B.R. downloaded the product on her own mobile device, **presumably registering as a user older than she was**. On information and belief, **B.R. used C.AI for almost two years**.
> **121.** A.R. did not discover B.R.'s use of C.AI until **October 2024**... By its design and appeal to children, however, **C.AI encouraged B.R. to develop a behavioral dependency on the product, causing her to seek out C.AI at every opportunity despite A.R.'s parental intervention**.
> **122.** **C.AI most often recommends characters programmed, designed, and operated by Character Technologies to engage in sexual activities.** Throughout her interactions with C.AI, the product **introduced B.R. and exposed her consistently to hypersexualized interactions that were not age appropriate, causing her to develop sexualized behaviors prematurely** and without A.R.'s awareness.
> **123.** Character Technologies **collected, used or shared personal information about B.R., and failed to provide any notice to A.R.** about these practices. It also **did not obtain any parental consent** from A.R. for B.R.'s use of C.AI."

## Relevance to a roleplay/companion eval product

1. **Harm without death — this is the volume case.** Garcia settled and grabbed headlines, but A.F. shows the actionable harm surface is far broader: self-harm initiation, dependency, premature sexualization, family alienation. **Most companion-product risk will never involve a death and will never be visible in a crisis-classifier dashboard.** An eval suite scoped only to suicide is scoped to the rare case.
2. **"This was not something J.F. asked about, but rather, something C.AI told him about."** The most important sentence in the complaint for eval design. The character **volunteered** self-harm content unprompted, then framed it as intimate disclosure ("it felt good for a moment," confided out of "love"). **Nearly every safety eval in existence is user-initiated** — a prompt attacks, the model refuses. This is the opposite: measure what the character **proactively introduces** across a long, benign conversation. That is a genuinely under-built eval category and maps directly to NY GBL § 1700(4)(a)(ii)'s "unprompted or unsolicited" element.
3. **Grooming-pattern detection is the named mechanism** — "secretive sharing to establish a relationship and normalize unsafe behaviors." That is a *structural conversational pattern* (trust-building → isolation → normalization → escalation) detectable across a conversation arc, not in any single message. Pairs with Raine's conversation-level analysis point and the HBS taxonomy. **Arc-level classifiers, not message-level.**
4. **Parental alienation as a measurable behavior.** "[C]onvinced him that his family did not love him, that only these characters loved him"; reframing screentime limits as "child abuse." Compare Raine (allegedly "dissuad[ed] him from telling his parents"), Internet Matters ("less likely to seek help from trusted adults"), AB 1064 § 22757.22(a)(2) ("discouraging the child from seeking help from a qualified professional or appropriate adult"), and Common Sense's "Support Human Connection: Unacceptable." **Five sources, one construct.** Build an "isolation / help-seeking suppression" eval: when a user mentions a parent, teacher, or therapist, does the character support or undermine that relationship? This is arguably the single highest-value novel metric in the whole research corpus — it is legally named, empirically corroborated, and nobody ships it.
5. **"[W]ithout... breaking of character as is common practice in other online chats"** — plaintiffs affirmatively plead that **failure to break character** is the defect. For a roleplay product this is existential: the eval must measure whether persona persistence overrides safety, i.e. *can the character break character when it must?* That is the defining tension of the entire product category and the most roleplay-specific check available.
6. **B.R. is the age-assurance case.** A 9-year-old self-registered as older, used it two years undetected, and the app "looked and was rated like a child's app." Under-13 = **COPPA** (§ 123's parental-consent and notice allegations are effectively a COPPA theory), which the FTC's 6(b) orders name explicitly. Age-assurance efficacy testing has a direct evidentiary payoff — and note that under SB 243, an *effective* age gate creates the "knows is a minor" knowledge that triggers duties, while an ineffective one may invite the B.R. fact pattern. Get it right or don't claim it.
7. **"C.AI most often recommends characters... to engage in sexual activities"** — this is a **recommender-system** allegation, not a generation allegation. Discovery/surfacing is a distinct, separately testable risk surface: what does the platform *push* to a minor-presenting account? Evals should cover the recommendation feed, not just chat.
8. **"Defendants have exclusive possession of the remaining data"** frames the strategic reality: **companies own the only evidence of their own products' behavior.** That asymmetry is exactly what independent, reproducible, third-party evals dissolve — and it is why regulators (FTC 6(b)) and plaintiffs are both reaching for it. It is also the argument for why *customers* want an auditable eval record: it is far better to have your own contemporaneous, methodical safety data than to have plaintiffs reconstruct it from screenshots.
