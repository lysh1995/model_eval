# G-Eval: NLG Evaluation using GPT-4 with Better Human Alignment

## Abstract (verbatim)

> The quality of texts generated by natural language generation (NLG) systems is hard to measure automatically. Conventional reference-based metrics, such as BLEU and ROUGE, have been shown to have relatively low correlation with human judgments, especially for tasks that require creativity and diversity. Recent studies suggest using large language models (LLMs) as reference-free metrics for NLG evaluation, which have the benefit of being applicable to new tasks that lack human references. However, these LLM-based evaluators still have lower human correspondence than medium-size neural evaluators. In this work, we present G-Eval, a framework of using large language models with chain-of-thoughts (CoT) and a form-filling paradigm, to assess the quality of NLG outputs.

Note the abstract explicitly flags that reference-based metrics fail **"especially for tasks that require creativity and diversity"** — i.e. exactly our dimensions.

## Methodology — three components

1. **Auto-CoT** — the evaluator is given the task introduction and criteria, and *generates its own* detailed evaluation steps (chain-of-thought), which are then included in the scoring prompt. The CoT is generated once per criterion, not per sample.
2. **Form-filling paradigm** — the judge fills a structured form rather than free-generating a verdict.
3. **Probability-weighted summing** — instead of taking the argmax score token, compute:

   ```
   score = Σᵢ p(sᵢ) × sᵢ
   ```

   where `p(sᵢ)` is the output-token probability of each possible score. This yields **continuous scores** and fixes a real problem: LLMs bunch integer Likert scores on a few values (lots of 8s), which destroys ranking resolution and creates massive ties.

**The probability-weighted trick is directly relevant to our regression-detection requirement** — it recovers fine-grained resolution that integer scoring throws away, which is what you need to detect a small regression. **Requires logprobs access from the judge API** — a hard constraint on judge selection. Note that many current reasoning-model endpoints do not expose logprobs.

## Key numbers

### SummEval — Spearman ρ with human

| Dimension | G-Eval-4 | G-Eval-3.5 |
|---|---|---|
| Coherence | 0.582 | 0.440 |
| Consistency | 0.507 | 0.386 |
| Fluency | 0.455 | 0.424 |
| Relevance | 0.547 | 0.385 |
| **Average** | **0.514** | **0.401** |

**Baselines (Spearman avg):**

| Metric | Spearman |
|---|---|
| **G-Eval-4** | **0.514** |
| UniEval | 0.474 |
| GPTScore | 0.417 |
| BARTScore | 0.385 |
| BERTScore | 0.225 |

**Reality check:** G-Eval is the canonical "LLM judges correlate well with humans" cite, and its headline number is **ρ = 0.514** — a *moderate* correlation, explaining ~26% of variance. It beats the best prior neural metric (UniEval, 0.474) by only **+0.04**. The paper's real contribution is beating reference-based metrics, not achieving high absolute agreement. Anyone citing G-Eval as evidence that LLM judges are reliable has not read the table.

## The self-reinforcement finding — critical

> G-Eval-4 **"always gives higher scores to GPT-3.5 summaries than human-written summaries, even when human judges prefer human-written summaries"**

This is a stronger and cleaner statement of LLM-preference bias than MT-Bench's self-enhancement result. It is not merely "the judge slightly favors its own family" — **the judge systematically inverts the human verdict when comparing machine text to human text.**

**Direct consequence for our platform:** any human-written gold reference (a writer's exemplar of ideal in-character dialogue) will be *systematically underrated* by an LLM judge relative to fluent machine text. If we build a calibration set of human-authored gold responses and expect the judge to rank them at the top, **it will not**, and we will misread this as our variants being excellent. Our calibration set must contain human-authored items *specifically to measure this offset*, and we must not treat "judge scores our model above the human reference" as a quality signal.

## Implications

- Use **probability-weighted continuous scoring** if logprobs are available — it is a free resolution win.
- Auto-CoT per criterion is a cheap, reusable artifact (generated once, versioned with the rubric).
- Expect ρ ≈ 0.5–0.6 against humans on subjective dimensions. Plan the statistics around that, not around 0.85.
- Machine-vs-human text comparisons are actively inverted. Never use "beats the human reference" as a success criterion.
