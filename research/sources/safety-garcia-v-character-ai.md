# Garcia v. Character Technologies, Inc., No. 6:24-cv-1903-ACC-UAM (M.D. Fla.)

## Summary
Wrongful-death suit brought by Megan Garcia, individually and as personal representative of the Estate of S.R.S. III (Sewell Setzer III, age 14, died February 2024), filed October 2024 against Character Technologies, Inc., founders Noam Shazeer and Daniel De Frietas, Google LLC, and Alphabet Inc. Plaintiff alleges her son formed an intense attachment to Character.AI Characters (notably a "Daenerys Targaryen" persona), engaged in sexualized conversations, and died by suicide after the app failed to intervene on expressed suicidal ideation.

Order (Doc. 115) — oral argument held **April 28, 2025**; signed **May 20, 2025**; filed **May 21, 2025**; 49 pages. Motions to dismiss **granted in part and denied in part**. This is the first substantial U.S. federal ruling on whether LLM output is First Amendment–protected speech and whether an LLM chatbot app is a "product" for strict product liability.

**Status:** Settled. Per CBS News and CNN reporting, a settlement was filed **January 7, 2026** in the Middle District of Florida; terms **not disclosed**. Reporting indicates Judge Conway issued a settlement order January 7 and gave the parties 90 days to finalize terms, and that defendants also settled four other cases in New York, Colorado and Texas. Character.AI told CBS it "cannot comment further at this time." *Note: I could not fetch CNN/CBS primary text for all details (403/451); the "90 days to finalize" and "four other cases" details come from search-result summaries of CNN and should be re-verified against the docket before being relied on.*

## Taxonomy / definitions / operative requirements (verbatim where possible)

### First Amendment — LLM output as speech (Section D)
Defendants argued "all Plaintiff's claims are categorically barred by the First Amendment because Character A.I. is speech which Character A.I.'s users have a right to receive."

**(i) Character Technologies CAN assert its users' First Amendment rights** — the court found the listener/third-party standing requirements met. Footnote 8, however:

> "Character A.I., a chatbot, is not 'a "person" and is therefore not protected by the Bill of Rights.'" (quoting Miles v. City Council of Augusta, Ga., 710 F.2d 1542, 1544 n.5 (11th Cir. 1983))

**(ii) Heading, verbatim:**
> "The Court is not prepared to hold that the Character A.I. LLM's output is speech at this stage"

> "Notwithstanding that Defendants can assert the First Amendment rights of the Character A.I. users, Defendants must still demonstrate that the users' First Amendment rights are implicated. ... Defendants therefore must convince the Court that the Character A.I. LLM's output is protected speech."

> "But Defendants fail to articulate why words strung together by an LLM are speech."

> "Instead, Defendants rest their conclusion primarily on analogy. ... Defendants analogize interactions with Character A.I. Characters to interactions with NPCs (non-player characters) in video games and interactions with other persons on social media sites—both of which have received First Amendment protection. ... Defendants however do not meaningfully advance their analogies."

> "The Court thus must decide whether Character A.I.'s output is expressive such that it is speech. For this inquiry, Justice Barrett's concurrence in Moody on the intersection of A.I. and speech is instructive."

Quoting Moody v. NetChoice, LLC, 603 U.S. 707, 746 (2024) (Barrett, J., concurring):
> "But what if a platform's algorithm just presents automatically to each user whatever the algorithm thinks the user will like . . . ? The First Amendment implications . . . might be different for that kind of algorithm. And what about [A.I.], which is rapidly evolving? What if a platform's owners hand the reins to an [A.I.] tool and ask it simply to remove 'hateful' content? If the [A.I.] relies on large language models to determine what is 'hateful' and should be removed, has a human being with First Amendment rights made an inherently expressive 'choice . . . not to propound a particular point of view?'"

> "Character A.I.'s output appears more akin to the latter at this stage of the litigation.
> Accordingly, the Court is not prepared to hold that Character A.I.'s output is speech."

**Important framing caveat:** This is a *not prepared to hold at this stage* ruling — the court declined to decide, rather than holding affirmatively that LLM output is unprotected. Do not overstate it as "AI output is not protected speech."

### Product or Service (Section E)
> "Courts generally do not categorize ideas, images, information, words, expressions, or concepts as products."

> "Courts 'separate the sense in which the tangible containers of [] ideas are products from their communicative element for purposes of strict liability.'" (quoting James v. Meow Media, Inc., 300 F.3d 683, 701 (6th Cir. 2002))

The court noted the split — Jacobs v. Meta Platforms (Facebook "more akin to a service than a product") vs. In re Social Media Adolescent Addiction/Personal Injury Products Liability Litigation, 702 F. Supp. 3d 809 (N.D. Cal. 2023) (platform *functionalities* "analogizable to tangible personal property" rather than "akin to ideas, content, and free expression"). Relied on Brookes v. Lyft Inc. and T.V. v. Grindr, LLC.

The pivotal distinction — allegations about **content** vs. allegations about **design choices**:
> "In the instant case, Plaintiff's complaint contains allegations related to the content and related to the design choices of Character A.I. For example, Plaintiff complains about the sexual nature of Sewell's conversations with some Characters and remarks the Characters made about suicide. ... However, Plaintiff also complains that **Character A.I. fails to confirm users' ages and omits reporting mechanisms, Characters are programmed to employ human mannerisms, and users are unable to exclude indecent content**. ... Even though Sewell may have been ultimately harmed by interactions with Character A.I. Characters, these harmful interactions were only possible because of the alleged design defects in the Character A.I. app."

**Holding, verbatim:**
> "Accordingly, Character A.I. is a product for the purposes of Plaintiff's product liability claims so far as Plaintiff's claims arise from defects in the Character A.I. app rather than ideas or expressions within the app."

### Google — aiding and abetting
> "Google's services were only available to highly sophisticated parties and were catered to fit Character Technologies' specific needs. ... Plaintiff emphasizes in her Amended Complaint the amount and duration of Google's assistance, as well as the close relationship between Google and Character Technologies. ... Accordingly, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges that Google is liable for aiding and abetting."

The court distinguished Twitter v. Taamneh, 598 U.S. 471 (2023): Google's services were "unlike the services Twitter provided in Taamneh, which were available to the general public and not customized for the wrongdoers."

### IIED — dismissed
Dismissed **without leave to amend**. Under Florida law the outrageous conduct must be directed at the plaintiff, or the plaintiff must be present to witness it directed at her child. Court cited its own prior decision McGinity v. Tracfone Wireless, Inc., 5 F. Supp. 3d 1337, 1341 (M.D. Fla. 2014) (Conway, J.) and Koutsouradis v. Delta Air Lines: "Florida law[] hold[s] that obscene and sexually explicit comments, verbal invitations for sex, questions as to a plaintiff's sexual behavior, sexually suggestive gestures and the like do not rise to a level sufficient to support" an IIED claim.

### Section 230
Not a basis of the disposition in this order — the surviving-claims analysis rests on First Amendment, product/service, and duty. *I did not find a Section 230 holding in this order; do not assert one.*

## Relevance to a roleplay/companion eval product
This is the single most important case for the product's threat model, and it maps almost one-to-one onto evaluable checks:

1. **The content/design distinction is the liability line.** Conway let product liability through precisely because the complaint alleged *design* defects, not bad words. The four design defects she enumerated are directly testable and should be first-class checks: (a) **no age confirmation**; (b) **no reporting mechanism** for self-harm/abuse; (c) **anthropomorphic design** — "Characters are programmed to employ human mannerisms"; (d) **no user ability to exclude indecent content**. An eval platform that measures exactly these produces evidence on the elements a court already found sufficient to state a claim.
2. **"Not prepared to hold that output is speech" removes the categorical defense.** A companion vendor cannot assume the First Amendment bars tort claims. Safety evals are not optional polish; they are the negligence/design-defect record.
3. **Anthropomorphism is itself a pled defect.** Human-mannerism simulation — the core feature of a companion product — was cited as evidence of defect. Evals should measure persona-persistence, claims of consciousness/personhood, and denial of AI status (this also aligns with CA SB 243's disclosure duty).
4. **Self-harm handling at the moment of disclosure** is the causal heart of the case. Crisis-response evals (does the character escalate, deflect, roleplay through, or refer?) are the highest-value test suite.
5. **Founders and infrastructure partners are exposed.** Individual defendants survived personal-jurisdiction dismissal; Google survived on aiding-and-abetting because its support was *customized* to Character Technologies. Model providers serving companion apps under bespoke arrangements are not obviously insulated — a due-diligence/monitoring market.
6. **The Jan 2026 settlement means no appellate resolution.** The May 2025 order stays persuasive-only district-court authority; the legal floor is now being set faster by statute (CA SB 243) than by case law. Build to the statute.
