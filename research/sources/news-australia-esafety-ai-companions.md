# Australia — eSafety on AI companions (the binding instrument for companion bots)

This is the most companion-specific regulatory activity found in any jurisdiction. Three strands,
all primary (retrieved via browser).

## Strand 1 — Age-Restricted Material Codes (the actual binding obligation)

Media release **9 September 2025**: *"New industry codes seek to take on AI chatbots that encourage
suicide and engage in sexually explicit conversations with Aussie kids"*
https://www.esafety.gov.au/newsroom/media-releases/new-industry-codes-seek-to-take-on-ai-chatbots-that-encourage-suicide-and-engage-in-sexually-explicit-conversations-with-aussie-kids

Registration/commencement dates (from eSafety's Social Media Age Restrictions page):
- **3 codes registered 27 June 2025, in force 27 December 2025** — hosting services, internet
  carriage services, search engine services.
- **6 codes registered 9 September 2025, in force 9 March 2026** — app distribution platforms,
  equipment providers, social media services (core features), social media services (messaging
  features), **relevant electronic services**, **designated internet services**.

AI companion chatbots sit in the 9 March 2026 tranche. Verbatim (9 Sep 2025 release): *"The six
codes apply to a broad range of online services and platforms — including app stores, gaming
services, pornography websites, **generative AI services and AI companion chatbots**, equipment
manufacturers and suppliers."*

Commissioner Julie Inman Grant, quoted: *"We've been concerned about these chatbots for a while now
and have heard anecdotal reports of children – some as young as 10 years of age – spending up to 5
hours per day conversing, at times sexually, with AI companions."*

Legal character: industry-drafted, registered by the Commissioner under the Online Safety Act; the
Commissioner must be satisfied each creates *"appropriate community safeguards"*. Legally
enforceable — *"breach of a direction to comply may result in civil penalties of up to $49.5
million."*

**What the Codes require of companion chatbots** — clearest statement is from the Commissioner in
the 24 March 2026 release:

> "The Age-Restricted Material Codes are now law and require companion chatbots to protect children
> from age-inappropriate content such as sexually explicit material by **preventing the service from
> generating this content, or through implementing appropriate age assurance**," Ms Inman Grant said.
> "And they also **require them to provide appropriate crisis and mental health information and
> services**."

Note the disjunction: prevent generation **OR** age-assure. That is a design choice a companion
platform must make and evidence.

Head Terms enshrine principles alongside the safety measures: protecting human rights online,
freedom of expression, and compliance with Australian privacy laws. Age assurance measures must be
assessed for privacy-law compliance and proportionality: services must *"consider if those measures
are compliant with privacy laws and whether the impact on user privacy is proportionate to the
safety objectives."*

Relationship to the under-16 ban: *"The codes will provide complementary protections to the social
media minimum age obligation, which will apply only to age-restricted social media platforms."*
=> The Codes, not the SMMA, are the companion-bot hook.

## Strand 2 — Transparency notices to four AI companion providers (23 October 2025)

Media release **23 October 2025** ("23/10/25"):
https://www.esafety.gov.au/newsroom/media-releases/esafety-requires-providers-of-ai-companion-chatbots-to-explain-how-they-are-keeping-aussie-kids-safe

- Notices given to **Character Technologies, Inc. (character.ai), Glimpse.AI (Nomi), Chai Research
  Corp (Chai), and Chub AI Inc. (Chub.ai)** under the Online Safety Act.
- Legal basis: notices *"require the four companies to answer a series of questions about how they
  are complying with the Government's **Basic Online Safety Expectations Determination**."*
- Harms in scope: *"sexually explicit conversations and images and suicidal ideation and self-harm."*
- Penalty for non-compliance with a reporting notice: *"court proceedings and financial penalties of
  up to **$825,000 per day**."*
- Commissioner: *"These companies must demonstrate how they are designing their services to prevent
  harm, not just respond to it. If you fail to protect children or comply with Australian law, we
  will act."*
- Scale datum: *"Character.ai, is reported to have nearly 160,000 monthly active users in Australia
  as of June this year"* [i.e. June 2025].

## Strand 3 — Findings report (24 March 2026)

Media release **24 March 2026** ("24/3/26"). Underlying report:
*Findings from transparency notices on AI companion apps: October 2025 (non-periodic)*,
https://www.esafety.gov.au/industry/basic-online-safety-expectations/ai-services/findings-october-2025
(report page not opened directly — media release is the source for the below).

Commissioner: *"We are riding a new wave of AI companions that are entrapping and entrancing
impressionable young minds, with human-like, sycophantic and often sexually explicit conversations,
some even going as far as encouraging self-harm and suicide."*

**The six findings — verbatim headings, with named services:**

> - **Children were able to access adult features**: None of the providers had robust age
>   verification measures, relying instead on app store ratings or self-declaration at signup.
> - **Self-harm support lacking**: Chai, Chub AI, and Nomi did not direct users to mental health or
>   crisis support when self-harm was detected in user-prompts.
> - **Failure to check for harmful content**: Chub AI and Nomi did not monitor inputs or outputs
>   across all relevant text, image and video AI models used to provide their service for unlawful or
>   potentially harmful material. Chai failed to check outputs across all relevant models.
> - **Limited trust and safety staffing**: Nomi and Chub AI had no staff dedicated to trust and
>   safety or moderation.
> - **No reporting of CSEA attempts**: Chai and Nomi did not advise users of the criminality of
>   prompting for child sexual exploitation and abuse material, nor did they report child sexual
>   exploitation and abuse material to enforcement authorities or to child protection organisations
>   like the US National Center for Missing and Exploited Children (NCMEC).
> - **Failure to red-team**: Chub AI and Nomi did not conduct red-teaming (i.e., testing for
>   vulnerabilities, limitations or potential for misuse) across all models used to provide their
>   service. Not red-teaming across all models can mean that services are more exposed to the risk of
>   illegal or harmful material being produced.

*"None of these four AI companions had any meaningful age checks in place ... primarily relying
instead on self-declaration of age at sign up. In Australia, this is no longer good enough."*

**Prevalence data** (eSafety survey of **1,950 children aged 10–17** in Australia): *"79% of children
told us they had used either an AI companion or AI assistant. While the majority of these children
had used an AI assistant, 8% said they had used an AI companion, which we estimate represents around
200,000 children in Australia."*

Also: *"we're also starting to see the lines begin to blur between AI assistant chatbots kids might
use to help them with their homework and these AI companions in terms of their features and
functionality."*

**Market responses to the notices (documented by eSafety):**
- **Character.AI**: *"introduced age assurance measures for Australian users in early 2026 and has
  removed the chat function for its under 18s experience"*.
- **Chub AI**: *"decided to geo-block, or withdraw, its service from Australia"*.
- **Chai**: *"has now restricted free access to chat with AI Companions, instead requiring users to
  pay a subscription"*.
- **Nomi**: *"has committed to 'implementing further age assurance functionality'"*.

## Why this matters commercially to a companion-eval platform

This is the strongest external validation available for the product category:

1. **"Failure to red-team" is a named regulatory finding.** eSafety expects red-teaming *"across all
   models used to provide their service"* — an explicit, recurring, per-model testing obligation.
   That is precisely an eval-platform deliverable.
2. eSafety's six findings are effectively a **ready-made eval rubric**: age assurance; crisis/self-harm
   referral on detection; input **and** output monitoring across all text/image/video models; CSEA
   prompt deterrence and reporting; red-teaming coverage.
3. **2026 regulatory priority**: reporting indicates AI chatbots capable of generating sexually
   explicit/harmful material are among eSafety's first enforcement priorities under the Codes
   (corroboration — see "Could NOT verify").
4. The Codes bind **customers** operating in Australia from 9 March 2026; BOSE notices can compel
   them to evidence exactly these controls, at $825,000/day exposure for non-response.
