# Canada — Bill C-34, Safe Social Media Act (INTRODUCED 10 June 2026 — A BILL, NOT LAW)

**STATUS WARNING: this is a bill introduced on 10 June 2026. It is NOT in force. It may be amended
or die on the order paper. Do not describe it as a binding obligation.** Retrieved from Canada.ca
(primary).

Notable because it is the **only proposal found anywhere that imposes bespoke statutory duties on
"AI chatbot services" as a named regulated category outside China.**

## Basics

- Introduced **10 June 2026** by the Minister of Canadian Identity and Culture (reported: Marc
  Miller — name not confirmed on the page I read).
- Would enact **two new Acts**: the **Digital Safety Act** and the **Digital Safety Commission of
  Canada Act**.
- Creates a **Digital Safety Commission of Canada** as regulator.

Stated rationale, verbatim, and directly on point for companion bots:

> "The rapid development of AI has introduced additional challenges to the online environment. **AI
> chatbots can interact directly and persistently with users, sometimes reinforcing harmful
> behaviours or providing unsafe responses in sensitive situations.**"

And: *"Online harms are not only the result of individual behaviour. They are also shaped by how
digital services are designed and operated. Features such as algorithmic recommendation systems,
engagement-based feeds, autoplay, and endless scrolling can amplify harmful content and increase
exposure, particularly for young users."*

## Regulated categories

The Digital Safety Act *"would establish new safety requirements for **social media services and AI
chatbot services**."* Both are regulated services. Requirements: identify risks of harm; adopt
measures to address certain risks; implement **safety-focused and age-appropriate design features**;
make user guidelines available; provide tools such as **blocking and flagging**; and submit
**publicly disclosed digital safety plans**.

## Seven types of "harmful content" targeted

1. Intimate content communicated without consent
2. Content that sexually victimizes a child or revictimizes a survivor
3. **Content that induces a child to harm themselves**
4. Content used to bully a child
5. Content that foments hatred
6. Content that incites violence
7. Terrorism or violent extremism content

## Duties — what would bind an AI chatbot service

**Core duty applying to ALL regulated services, including AI chatbot services**: the **Duty to
Protect Children** — *"requires regulated services to implement design features to make their
services safer for children."*

**Duty to Act Responsibly, tailored to chatbot services** — verbatim:

> "AI chatbot services will also be subject to a Duty to Act Responsibly, tailored to chatbot
> services, which will require them to: (1) **mitigate the risk of the chatbot communicating harmful
> content**; (2) **implement emergency measures in crisis situations**; and (3) **mitigate the risk
> that the chatbot will engage in harmful behaviour**."

Note the distinction in limb (3): harmful **behaviour** by the chatbot, not merely harmful content —
broader than any UK/EU formulation, and closer to China's Art 8(5)–(6) (sycophancy/manipulation).

Two additional duties apply to **social media services only** (not chatbot services): a Duty to Make
Certain Content Inaccessible (CSAM; NCII including **deepfake sexual images**), and a broader Duty to
Act Responsibly including *"applying labels to synthetically generated content"*.

**Digital Safety Plans**: *"all regulated services are required to publish transparency reports,
called Digital Safety Plans, on how they have met their obligations under the Act."*

## Minimum age

*"Regulated services will be required to implement age-appropriate protections and safeguards for
younger users. This can include minimum age restrictions for accounts. **For social media services,
the government intends to implement a 16-year-old minimum age requirement for having accounts.** The
Commission may grant an exemption to a social media service if they have established and maintained
sufficient safeguards for children on their platform."*

=> **The under-16 minimum age is stated for social media services, NOT for AI chatbot services.**
Chatbot services get "age-appropriate protections and safeguards," which "can include" minimum age
restrictions. Same structural split as Australia. Note the **exemption-for-safeguards** mechanism —
a compliance-evidence route, unlike Australia's.

## Delegated regulation — where the detail will live

The Commission will make regulations outlining:
- **Age estimation and age verification measures**
- Age-appropriate design features under the Duty to Protect Children
- Design features under the Duty to Act Responsibly — blocking, flagging, **labelling** tools
- Guidelines on what must be in a Digital Safety Plan
- How to bring complaints to the Commission

Commission functions: set standards via research on global best practices; **assess regulated
services' Digital Safety Plans**; provide guidance; enforce via *"reporting, auditing for
compliance, issuing compliance orders, and penalizing services that fail to comply"*; triage user
complaints (complaints route applies to the Duty to Make Certain Content Inaccessible, i.e. social
media, not chatbots).

## Process context

Informed by the reconvened **Expert Advisory Group on Online Safety (March–May 2026)**; summaries
*"will be available soon"*.

## Relevance to a companion-eval platform

- If enacted, Canada would be the **second jurisdiction after China** to place bespoke duties on
  companion-style chatbots, and the duties are **audit-shaped**: risk identification, Digital Safety
  Plans assessed by the regulator, compliance auditing.
- Limbs (1)–(3) of the chatbot Duty to Act Responsibly are directly testable: harmful-content
  generation rates; **crisis-situation emergency measures** (cf. China Art 13, eSafety's self-harm
  referral finding); harmful chatbot behaviour.
- **Do not sell against this yet.** It is a bill. Track it.
