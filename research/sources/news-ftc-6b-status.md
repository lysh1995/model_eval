## ANSWER TO THE BRIEF'S QUESTION: **No findings published.**

The task asked: "FTC 6(b) companion-bot inquiry — any findings published?"

**No.** As of **2026-07-16**, no staff report and no findings have been released. Confirmed by
searching for a 2026 report specifically; nothing exists. The landing page still describes orders,
not results.

**Why, and when to expect one:**
> "the outcome of the Section 6(b) process is usually a **staff report**, but in practice, it can
> take considerable time – **measured often in years** – for agency staff to obtain and review the
> requested information, develop a report, and get FTC approval to release the report to the
> public."

⚠️ **Plan on no FTC report before 2027 at the earliest.** Do not build a roadmap milestone around it.

## What we know ✅

- **Orders issued 2025-09-11** under **Section 6(b)** authority (wide-ranging study, **no specific
  law-enforcement purpose**).
- **Seven recipients:** **Alphabet/Google, Character Technologies, Instagram, Meta, OpenAI, Snap,
  xAI**.
- **Information demanded** (per FTC's own framing): product **advertising**, **safety practices**,
  **monetization**, **usage and engagement**, **character design and approval**, **testing and
  monitoring for negative impacts**, **age-based access restrictions**, **complaint handling**, and
  **compliance with company rules and terms**.
- Core question: what steps companies took "to **evaluate the safety** of their chatbots when
  acting as companions, to limit the products' use by and potential negative effects on children
  and teens, and to apprise users and parents of the risks."
- ⚠️ A 6(b) study "won't create enforceable standards on its own."

## Why this matters for us

### 1. The order is a **de facto eval spec written by the regulator**
Read the demanded categories as product requirements: *measure, test, and monitor negative impacts
on children and teens; document character design and approval; document age-based restrictions.*
**Seven companies had to answer this in writing, under compulsion, in late 2025.** Whatever they
produced is the incumbent standard of care — and the ones who had nothing to say learned that
building it is now cheaper than explaining its absence. **"Character design and approval" is a
notable inclusion**: the FTC treats the *character-creation pipeline* (who authored the persona,
who approved it, against what criteria) as a safety surface. Our corpus has **no**
character-provenance/approval dimension. That is a gap.

### 2. Don't wait for it
The absence of findings for 10 months, with years plausible, means **the FTC report is not a
market catalyst.** The catalysts already fired: state statutes (16 enacted), a state AG suit
(Florida), a settled docket (Character.AI), and app-store age mandates (Texas/SCOTUS). Note 07
lists the 6(b) inquiry among live pressures — accurate, but it is the **slowest** of them and
should be de-prioritized relative to the 2027-01-01 state effective-date cliff.
