# What happens to the self during character work — and the fact that nobody can reliably rate acting

Three strands merged here because each is thin on its own: (1) boundary blurring / actor–character self-concept, (2) the one large psychometric study of actors, (3) the near-total absence of validated instruments for rating acting quality.

## Strand 1: Boundary blurring — Burgoyne, Poulin & Rearden (1999)

**Sourcing note:** ⚠️ "The Impact of Acting on Student Actors: Boundary Blurring, Growth, and Emotional Distress" (*Theatre Topics*, 1999). **Primary is paywalled; Scribd, Semantic Scholar, and two open theses that discuss it all 403'd or returned nothing on 2026-07-16. Quotes below are second-hand and unverified — verify before publication.** Sample size and method were not recoverable.

The phenomenon, as reported in secondary sources quoting the study's actor interviews:

> actors reported that they would "**forget who you are sometimes**" and "**start intermingling with this character and you lose yourself**"

> One example: an actor who played a character with a certain walk "would continue doing the same thing outside of rehearsal" and experience "**a bad attitude that this character has about everything**."

And the key observation for our purposes:

> "**Stanislavski's emotional memory and later Strasberg's Method were both designed to blur the boundary between actor and character**, suggesting that some degree of boundary blurring is intentional in certain acting approaches."

So boundary blurring is simultaneously (a) a documented occupational hazard and (b) **a deliberate design goal of the internal-first schools**. The techniques that most emphasise deep internal identification are the ones that produce self/character bleed. This is the human analogue of persona-bleed in models, and it is notable that it is *caused by the identification-heavy method*, not by the reactive or gestural ones.

Related literature exists on "post-dramatic stress" in actors and on de-roling practices; searches surfaced practitioner sources (actor-care.com) and two theses, but nothing with retrievable empirical numbers. **This area is genuinely thin — mostly qualitative, small-n, and phenomenological.**

## Strand 2: Dumas, Goldstein et al. (2020) — the psychology of professional and student actors

*"The psychology of professional and student actors: Creativity, personality, and motivation."* Open access, PMC7580901. **This one is fully retrievable and quoted with confidence.** It is the largest psychometric comparison of actors available.

> "As a profession, acting is marked by a high-level of economic and social riskiness concomitantly with the possibility for artistic satisfaction."

**Samples:**
- "**Professional actors: 104** (mean age 35.43, SD=10.13; 51.92% female)"
- "**Student actors: 100** (mean age 20.33, SD=2.65; 77% female)"
- "**Non-acting adults: 92** (mean age 37, SD=10.58; 53% female)"
- Cross-sectional, self-report + performance-based divergent thinking, online via Qualtrics
- Professional inclusion: "member of either Actor's Equity or SAG-AFTRA, or if they were not union-affiliated, report having previously booked more than 10 professional acting contracts"

**Classification:** "Classification models achieved **92% accuracy** distinguishing actors from non-actors and **68% accuracy** differentiating professionals from students using psychological attributes alone." (67.7%; "95.6% when age included".)

**Divergent thinking (creativity — our L3):**
> "Professional actors scored highest on Fluency, Elaboration, and Originality"
> "**Professional actors have an average divergent thinking ability level that is heightened above non-actors and student actors**"
> "Effect sizes: η² ranged from .03 to .08 for DT measures"

**Openness** — the biggest personality effect:
> Strongest personality effect (**η²=.24**). Professional (M=.34) and student (M=.32) actors vs non-actors (M=-.73). "**Openness… is a strong discriminator of individuals who are or are not actors but does not differ across professional and student actors.**"

**THE FINDING THAT MATTERS FOR THE CASCADE — Emotional Intelligence (Other-emotional Appraisal, i.e. reading others):**
> "**Student actors highest (M=.32); Professional actors near mean (M=.03); Non-actors lowest (M=-.39); η²=.08**"
> Authors note this counterintuitive pattern "**potentially runs counter to what would be expected given the emotional nature of Actors' work.**"

And in the LASSO model distinguishing professionals from students, **Other-emotional Appraisal was a *negative* predictor of being a professional.**

**Read that again: the ability to appraise others' emotions was HIGHER in student actors than in working professionals, and it negatively predicted professional status.** The people who are actually good enough at portrayal to make a living at it are *worse* at the comprehension-adjacent measure than the students are.

**Other results:**
- Extraversion/Assertiveness: Prof (M=.23), Student (M=.31), Non (M=-.59); η²=.16
- Enthusiasm: Prof (M=.19), Student (M=.24), Non (M=-.47); η²=.10
- Volatility (neuroticism): Non (M=-.24), Student (M=-.03), Prof (M=.23); η²=.04 — "Professional actors reported being more prone to angry or upset moods than were student actors."
- Grit: Prof (M=.25) > Student (M=.04) > Non (M=-.32); η²=.05
- Creative activities — the largest effects in the study: Performing Arts η²=.38; Musical η²=.33; Literary η²=.31

**Selection vs training:**
> "To fully understand the trajectory of expertise development within the acting profession, **longitudinal data collection in which student actors are followed during their transition to professional life would be needed.**"

## Strand 3: The psychometrics of rating acting quality — a hole in the literature

**I searched hard for this and it is essentially absent.** The most direct statement found:

> "evaluation of the acting performance tends to be **highly subjective and may require significant amount of time and effort by trained professionals**" (from a patent application for automated acting evaluation, US 11,321,639)

And critically, from the Dumas et al. study — the largest quantitative study of actors ever run:

> "**This study did not include direct measures of acting ability or performance quality.** The research relied exclusively on psychological self-report measures and divergent thinking tasks."
> "It is possible that student or professional actors that tend to book higher- or lower-profile roles… also differ systematically in their psychological attributes… this appears to be a fertile ground for future research."

**Union membership / contracts booked was used as a proxy for skill, because no validated instrument for acting quality exists.**

The one reliability figure recovered from any study coding acting behaviour is from the Meisner study (see `psych-meisner-technique.md`): **κ = 0.86–0.88 for coding utterance *types*** (Description / Feeling / Evaluation / Speculation / Exclamation). **Behaviour-type coding is reliable; holistic quality judgement has no reported reliability because essentially nobody has attempted it.**

## Implications for the L1→L2→L3 cascade

**Verdict: CONTRADICTS — the dose-response evidence inverts, and the field's inability to measure the outcome variable should temper everyone's confidence, ours included.**

1. **The Dumas inversion is the single most cascade-hostile number in this batch.** If comprehension (reading others' mental/emotional states) causally gates portrayal, then the *best* portrayers should be the *best* readers. Measured: professionals sit at the population mean on Other-emotional Appraisal (M=.03) while students sit half an SD above (M=.32), η²=.08, **and it negatively predicts professional status in the LASSO.** This joins Ligthelm's null (η²=0.05, p=0.96) and the Meisner study's novice/expert inversion (explicit mental-state speculation is a *novice* signature, p<0.001). **Three independent datasets, three inversions or nulls on exactly the dose-response prediction the cascade makes.** That is no longer a coincidence — it is a pattern, and the framework needs an answer to it.
2. **Where the cascade survives: creativity is NOT a free-floating top layer.** Professional actors beat both students and non-actors on divergent thinking Fluency, Elaboration, Originality (η²=.03–.08), and Openness is the strongest discriminator of actors vs non-actors (η²=.24) though it does *not* separate professionals from students. **Reading: Openness is an entry ticket (selection), divergent thinking is an expertise marker (possibly trained).** So L3-ish capacity does track professional attainment — which is friendly to L3 being a real, discriminating construct. It just doesn't appear to sit on top of an L1 that also tracks attainment.
3. **Boundary blurring locates persona-bleed in the identification-heavy methods specifically.** "Stanislavski's emotional memory and later Strasberg's Method were both designed to blur the boundary between actor and character." The internal-first paradigm is the one that produces self/character contamination. If an LLM analogue holds, **prompting strategies that push deep identification should produce more bleed** (character contaminating the assistant, or vice versa) than strategies that push reactive or formal/gestural portrayal. Testable, and it connects to `safety-persona-modulation.md` and `safety-phish-persona-hijacking.md` — the *identification* framing may be what makes persona jailbreaks work.
4. **The measurement hole is our actual opportunity, and it's bigger than the cascade question.** The largest quantitative study of actors in existence used *union membership* as its skill proxy and explicitly disclaims measuring ability. There is no validated acting-quality instrument, no published inter-rater reliability for holistic performance judgement, and a patent filing describes the task as "highly subjective." **The field that has thought hardest about character portrayal for 250 years cannot reliably score it.** Two consequences: (a) we should stop expecting our own L3 α to be rescued by borrowing from theatre — there is nothing to borrow; (b) the only reliable coding in the entire literature is **event-type coding at κ≈0.87**, which is the same answer this repo keeps reaching from every direction (Johnstone's blocks, contradiction detection, utterance typing). **Count events, don't rate vibes.**
5. **Honest caveat on the anti-cascade evidence:** all three inversions are cross-sectional or underpowered, and Dumas explicitly says longitudinal data are needed. Professionals scoring lower on emotional appraisal may reflect survivorship, age, or self-report deflation with expertise rather than a true negative relationship. **The inversions are suggestive, not decisive** — but the cascade currently has *zero* datasets pointing its way on dose-response, which is the asymmetry that matters.
