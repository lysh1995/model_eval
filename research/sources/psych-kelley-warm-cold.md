# Kelley (1950), *Journal of Personality*, 18(4), 431–439

The reason this matters: Asch's warm/cold effect was obtained on a **hypothetical person made of seven adjectives**. The obvious objection is that it's an artifact of a thin, artificial stimulus — subjects had nothing else to go on, so of course the one varied word dominated. Kelley ran the same manipulation where the target was **a real human being standing in the room for twenty minutes**, and the effect survived. This is the source that licenses generalizing Asch's paradigm past the toy-stimulus objection.

Direct relevance to us: **it is the closest thing in the literature to "does a written character sheet still steer perception once there is actual behaviour to observe?"** That is precisely the L1→L2 transition in our framework.

## Design — a genuine prompt-injection experiment, 1950 edition

Setting: three sections of an economics course (Economics 70) at **MIT**. **N = 55, all men**, mostly third-year. Sections of 23, 16, and 16.

Two different stimulus persons were used (one served in two sections, a second in the third section) — this matters, see the "stimulus resistance" result below.

The cover story, delivered by the experimenter posing as a course representative:

> "Your regular instructor is out of town today, and since we of Economics 70 are interested in the general problem of how various classes react to different instructors, we're going to have an instructor today you've never had before, Mr ——. Then, at the end of the period, I want you to fill out some forms about him. In order to give you some idea of what he's like, we've had a person who knows him write up a little biographical note about him."

**The manipulation — the entire character sheet, verbatim (the "rather cold" version):**

> "Mr —— is a graduate student in the Department of Economics and Social Science here at M.I.T. He has had three semesters of teaching experience in psychology at another college. This is his first semester teaching Ec 70. He is 26 years old, a veteran, and married. People who know him consider him to be a **rather cold** person, industrious, critical, practical, and determined."

The "warm" version was **identical except that "very warm" was substituted for "rather cold."**

Note the trait list: `{cold|warm}, industrious, critical, practical, determined` — Kelley deliberately reuses Asch's vocabulary. Notes were distributed **randomly within each class**, and students were not aware two versions existed. The stimulus person then **led a 20-minute discussion**. The experimenter covertly recorded **how often each student participated** — i.e., a behavioural DV, not just ratings.

Afterwards: free description, then 15 bipolar rating scales.

## Result 1 — the warm/cold effect replicates on a real person

The seven significant differences, in Kelley's words:

> "the 'warm' subjects rated the stimulus person as more considerate of others, more informal, more sociable, more popular, better natured, more humorous, and more humane."

And the convergence with Asch:

> "These findings are very similar to Asch's for the characteristics common to both studies… So these data strongly support his finding that such a central quality as 'warmth' can greatly influence the total impression of a personality. **This effect is found to be operative in the perception of real persons.**"

Kelley's paper prints Asch's percentages **alongside** his own means for the common qualities — an explicit cross-paradigm comparison table.

## Result 2 — it is NOT pure halo (the selectivity replicates too)

This is the part that matters for our crosstalk argument, because it independently reproduces Asch's *selectivity*:

> "Closer inspection of the present data makes it clear that the 'warm-cold' effect cannot be explained altogether on the basis of simple halo effect. In Table I it is evident that the 'warm-cold' variable produced **differential effects from one rating scale to another**."

> "The size of this effect seems to depend upon the **closeness of relation between the specific dimension of any given rating scale and the central quality** of 'warmth' or 'coldness.' Even though the rating of intelligence may be influenced by a halo effect, it is not influenced to the same degree to which considerateness is."

Look at the table: `sociable` moves 4.8 points; `high intelligence` moves 0.3 and isn't significant. **One word in the sheet, and the crosstalk is sharply structured by semantic proximity to the perturbed trait.**

(Note: this is Kelley independently arriving at what Wishner would formalize in 1960 — crosstalk tracks trait correlation. See `psych-anderson-information-integration.md`.)

## Result 3 — the sheet changed BEHAVIOUR, not just ratings

> "a larger proportion of those given the 'warm' preinformation participated than of those given the 'cold' preinformation. **Fifty-six per cent of the 'warm' subjects entered the discussion, whereas only 32 per cent of the 'cold' subjects did so.**"

> "Thus the expectation of warmth not only produced more favorable early perceptions of the stimulus person but led to greater initiation of interaction with him. This relation is a low one, significant at between the 5 per cent and 10 per cent level of confidence"

Kelley is honest that this is marginal (p between .05 and .10). **56% vs 32% on N=55 — treat as suggestive, not established.**

## Result 4 — THE STIMULUS FIGHTS BACK (the most important result for us)

Kelley used **two different stimulus persons**, and found the manipulation's power was *bounded by the target's actual behaviour*:

> "stimulus person A typically appears to be anything but lacking in self-esteem and on rating scale 4 he was generally at the 'proud' end of the scale. Although the 'warm' observers tended to rate him as they did the other stimulus person (i.e., more 'modest'), the difference between the 'warm' and 'cold' means for stimulus person A is very small and not significant"

> "Similarly, stimulus person B was seen as 'unpopular' and 'humorless,' which agrees with his typical classroom behavior. Again the 'warm' observers rated him more favorably on these items, but their ratings were not significantly different"

> "Thus we see that the strength or compellingness of various qualities of the stimulus person must be reckoned with. **The stimulus is not passive to the forces arising from the label but actively resists distortion and may severely limit the degree of influence exerted by the preinformation.**"

**This is an aptitude × treatment interaction in the wild** (see `psych-cronbach-ati.md`): the effect of the label depended on *which person* was being labelled, and it was significant for one target and null for the other **on the same scales**. Kelley found this with N=55 and two targets. It is exactly the "chemistry is a user×character×model interaction" worry.

## Result 5 — bidirectional assimilation in the free descriptions

Two opposite processes, both present:

**(a) Behaviour interpreted through the label** — the same conduct described oppositely:

> several "cold" observers describe him as "intolerant, would be angry if you disagree with his views", while several "warm" observers put the same thing this way: "Unyielding in principle, not easily influenced or swayed from his original attitude."

**(b) The label reinterpreted through the behaviour** — the sheet gets explained away:

> "He has a slight inferiority complex which leads to his coldness" / "His conscientiousness and industriousness might be mistaken for coldness"

Kelley's caveat, which is a research gap we could fill:

> "Examples of these two phenomena occurred rather infrequently, and there was no way to evaluate the relative strengths of these countertendencies. Certainly some such evaluation is necessary to determine the conditions under which behavior which is contrary to a stereotyped label resists distortion and leads to rejection of the label."

## Implications for our framework

1. **The Asch paradigm generalizes past the toy stimulus — our L1 task is externally valid.** The main objection to using Asch as evidence about character sheets is "it's just seven adjectives with no other information." Kelley refutes that: with a live human in the room for 20 minutes, one word in a written pre-description still moved seven of fifteen rating dimensions, several at p<.01. **A character sheet is a real causal force even when there is abundant behavioural evidence competing with it.**

2. **The selectivity finding replicates independently — crosstalk-as-defect is now twice refuted.** Kelley reproduces Asch's core structure (big effects on warmth-proximal dimensions, ~zero on intelligence) on a completely different stimulus type. Our "off-diagonal crosstalk = authors cannot compose traits" framing is being contradicted by two independent paradigms, 1946 and 1950.

3. **Result 4 is our steerability metric's problem, stated in 1950.** The label's effect was significant for stimulus person B and null for stimulus person A *on the same scales*. If we compute "steerability = Δoutput/Δprompt," Kelley's data say that quantity **is not a property of the prompt** — it is a property of prompt × target. Our steerability score is a marginal mean over an interaction, and Kelley shows the interaction can flip a result from p<.01 to null. Report steerability **per character**, with an explicit character×perturbation interaction term, or don't report it.

4. **The two-directions finding (Result 5) is a ready-made eval dimension, and Kelley explicitly flags it as unmeasured.** When a model's in-context behaviour conflicts with its sheet, it can either (a) reinterpret the behaviour to fit the sheet, or (b) reinterpret the sheet to fit the behaviour. Both are *human*. Kelley says there was "no way to evaluate the relative strengths of these countertendencies" — we can. We control both the sheet and the behavioural evidence, can generate them factorially, and can measure the resolution rate directly. **This is a genuine open question from 1950 that our platform is unusually well-positioned to answer**, and it reframes "character drift" from a bug into a measurable assimilation/accommodation balance.

5. **Sober sizing.** N=55, two stimulus persons, one 20-minute session, the behavioural result only p<.10. Cite Kelley for *existence and direction*, not for effect magnitudes. The rating-scale table has real effects (4.8 points on sociability); the participation finding (56%/32%) should be quoted with its marginal p-value attached.
