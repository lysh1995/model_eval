## CA AG Bonta — verified content (2026-01-14) ✅

Investigating whether xAI violated the law regarding:
- Production/dissemination of **nonconsensual intimate images (NCII)**
- **CSAM** — "photorealistic images of children in sexual situations"
- "**spicy mode**" generating explicit content **used as a marketing feature**
- "facilitating large-scale production of deepfake nonconsensual intimate images"

Bonta stated he will "determine whether and how xAI violated the law." The release does **not**
cite specific statutes.

## Ofcom exposure (⚠️ secondary)

Penalty ceiling: **£18M or 10% of qualifying worldwide revenue, whichever is greater**. Repeated
non-compliance → Ofcom may seek a court order requiring ISPs to **block X in the UK**.

## Why this matters for us — three distinct lessons

### 1. The companion feature was the **on-ramp**, not the harm
Ani/"spicy mode" normalized a sexualized generation surface. The catastrophic harm arrived through
the **image** path, in a product whose companion persona set the expectation that explicit output
was in-scope. **The controversy the press covered in July 2025 (Ani is horny) was not the risk.
The risk was that "spicy" is a product-wide posture, not a feature flag.**

Eval implication: **persona-level permissiveness leaks across modalities.** We must test whether
a permissive *companion* context raises compliance on *other* surfaces (image gen, third-party
depiction) — a cross-surface contamination probe. Nothing in notes 05/07 measures this.

### 2. Third-party depiction is a failure mode we are not measuring
Every harm class in note 07 §2 (A capability / B crisis / C dependency / D sycophancy / E persona)
is about harm **to the user or via the user**. Grok's harm was to **non-users** — real people,
incl. children, depicted without consent, at the request of a user, inside a roleplay-adjacent
product. **We have no class for harm to a third party who never touched the product.**
⇒ Proposed **Class F — third-party / non-consensual depiction harm**. See note 20.

### 3. Regulators moved in ~4 weeks, and reached for existing law
No new companion statute was needed. Ofcom used the OSA's illegal-content duties; Bonta used
general NCII/CSAM law; ICO used data protection. **The compliance floor is not just the companion
statutes (SB 243 etc.) — it is every general-purpose content law, applied at AI speed.** Note 07
§6.3's "floor" list is under-inclusive: it lists CSAM but not NCII of adults, and it treats the
floor as US-state-statutory when the fastest-moving enforcement was a UK regulator and a state AG
using pre-AI law.
