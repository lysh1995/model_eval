# UK Online Safety Act — how it applies (and does NOT apply) to companion/roleplay chatbots

Three Ofcom primary documents combine to answer the "are user-created chatbot personas UGC?"
question. Retrieved via browser (Ofcom serves 403 to plain fetches; site sits behind a bot check).

1. **Open letter to UK online service providers regarding Generative AI and chatbots** — published
   **8 November 2024**, signed Lindsey Fussell, Interim Group Director for Online Safety.
   https://www.ofcom.org.uk/online-safety/illegal-and-harmful-content/open-letter-to-uk-online-service-providers-regarding-generative-ai-and-chatbots
   (Pre-dates the mid-2025 window but is the substantive analysis; expressly cross-referenced by the
   Dec 2025 explainer as "A more detailed explanation is included in our previously published open
   letter".)
2. **AI chatbots and online regulation – what you need to know** — published **18 December 2025**.
3. **Ofcom update: Investigation into X, and scope of the Online Safety Act** — published
   **3 February 2026**.
   https://www.ofcom.org.uk/online-safety/illegal-and-harmful-content/investigation-into-x-and-scope-of-the-online-safety-act

## The trigger for the open letter (8 Nov 2024)

Verbatim: *"These include the tragic death of an American teenager who had developed a relationship
with a chatbot based on a Game of Thrones character, and last week, we were alerted to a
particularly concerning case, where users of a Generative AI chatbot platform had created chatbots
to act as 'virtual clones' of real people and deceased children, including Molly Russell and
Brianna Ghey."*

## ANSWER: user-created chatbot personas ARE user-generated content

Verbatim from the open letter (bullet 2):

> "Where a site or app allows users to upload or create their own Generative AI chatbots – **'user
> chatbots'** – which are also made available to other users, **it is also a user-to-user service**.
> This includes services that provide tools for users to create chatbots that mimic the personas of
> real and fictional people, which can be submitted to a chatbot library for others to interact
> with. **Any text, images or videos created by these 'user chatbots' is 'user-generated content'
> and is regulated by the Act.**"

Bullet 1: a service including a GenAI chatbot *"that enables users to share text, images or videos
generated by the chatbot with other users"* is a U2U service — including *"'group chat' functionality
that enables multiple users to interact with a chatbot at the same time – whether this chatbot
functionality is the main feature of the service, or is just part of a bigger service"*.

Bullet 3: *"any AI-generated text, audio, images or videos that are shared by users on a user-to-user
service is user-generated content and would be regulated in exactly the same way as human-generated
content ... It does not matter whether that content was created on the platform where it is shared
or has been uploaded by a user from elsewhere."*

Footnote 2 defines UGC: *"images, videos, messages or comments, as well as other forms of data, that
are generated, uploaded or shared by the user and can be encountered by other users."*

Also from the open letter:
- **Search services**: GenAI tools enabling search of more than one website/database are search
  services (Part 3). Includes tools that *"modify, augment or facilitate the delivery of search
  results on an existing search engine, or which provide 'live' internet results"*.
- **Part 5**: *"Sites and apps that include Generative AI tools that can generate pornographic
  material are also regulated under the Act."* These must use HEAA.

## The limits — Ofcom's own statement of the gap (3 Feb 2026)

This is the most important passage for scoping. Verbatim:

> "Chatbots are not subject to regulation at all if they:
> - only allow people to interact with the chatbot itself and no other users (i.e. they are not
>   user-to-user services);
> - do not search multiple websites or databases when giving responses to users (i.e. are not
>   search services); and
> - cannot generate pornographic content."

And:

> "Whether they fall within one or more of these categories depends on how they work, and even where
> some AI chatbots are in scope of the Act, this does not mean that all content they generate will be
> regulated. Images and videos that are created by a chatbot without it searching the internet are
> not generally in scope. They will only be in scope if they are pornographic – in which case they
> need to be age-gated – or can be shared with other users of the chatbot."

Footnote 3 of the 3 Feb 2026 update states the Part 3/Part 5 split precisely:

> "AI-generated content is only covered by the illegal content and children's safety duties in Part
> 3 of the Act if it is 'user-generated' (shared by users with each other) or 'search content'
> (encountered in or via search results). **Generation of other chatbot content – such as in a
> one-to-one interaction between a user and a chatbot that does not involve searching the internet
> or sharing with other users – is not regulated under Part 3, but could be under Part 5 if it is
> pornographic.**"

Consequence Ofcom itself draws: *"Because of the way the Act relates to chatbots, as explained
above, we are currently unable to investigate the creation of illegal images by the standalone Grok
service in this case."*

=> **A pure 1:1 companion bot that cannot produce porn and does not search the web is currently
OUTSIDE the OSA.** The regulatory hooks for companion platforms are: (a) the user-chatbot library /
sharing model => U2U/Part 3; (b) pornographic output => Part 5 HEAA.

## Legislative change flagged

*"We can only take action on online harms covered by the Act, using the powers we have been granted.
Any changes to these powers would be a matter for Government and Parliament. The Secretary of State
has said that the Government will look at how chatbots should be regulated and we are supporting
that work."* — sourced (footnote 4) to House of Commons Science, Innovation and Technology Committee,
Oral evidence: Work of the Secretary of State for DSIT, **3 December 2025**.

## Duties that follow if in scope (from the open letter)

For U2U and search providers: risk assessments; proportionate mitigation measures; easy reporting.
Code measures named: named person accountable for OSA compliance; adequately resourced and
well-trained content moderation function allowing swift takedown; HEAA to prevent children
encountering the most harmful content; easy-to-access reporting and complaints processes.

## Relevance to a companion-eval platform

- Binds **customers** operating character/companion platforms in the UK — most strongly where users
  create and share personas (U2U) or where the bot can output porn (Part 5).
- Does **not** bind an eval platform. Also note the 1:1 non-porn carve-out means UK OSA coverage of
  companion bots is narrower than the EU AI Act's.
- Evaluable surfaces: age assurance effectiveness, illegal-content generation in shared/persona
  contexts, porn generation triggering Part 5, self-harm/suicide content (Ofcom's stated concern).

## Could NOT verify

- Whether the UK Government has actually introduced legislation extending the OSA to 1:1 chatbots.
  As of the 3 Feb 2026 Ofcom update this was only "the Government will look at how chatbots should
  be regulated". No enacted extension found.
