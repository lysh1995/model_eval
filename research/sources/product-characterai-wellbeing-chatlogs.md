# Character.AI users — donated chat logs + survey, wellbeing outcomes

**The best available ground truth on what actually happens inside companion conversations**, because
it uses donated real chat histories rather than self-report alone.

## Method / sample

- **1,131 survey respondents** (from 2,836 initial enrollees), U.S.-based native English speakers via
  Prolific, all Character.AI users of >1 month who had used ≥3 distinct chatbots.
- **244 participants donated chat histories**: **4,363 sessions**, **413,509 messages**.
- Measures: companionship use ("interaction characterized by emotional, interpersonal, or affective
  engagement"), interaction intensity, self-disclosure, offline social support (close network size).

## What users actually do

- Only **11.8%** name companionship as their **primary** motive…
- …yet **51.1%** use companionship terms ("friend," "companion," "romantic partner") when describing the
  relationship. **Stated motive massively understates revealed relational framing.** Do not trust
  onboarding surveys or self-reported use-case for segmentation.
- Conversation topics: **emotional and social support in 80.3% of sessions**; **romantic/intimacy
  roleplay in 68.0%**.
- High self-disclosure conversations involved: **emotional distress (60.8%)**, desire for romantic
  connection (41.4%), current life challenges (31.5%), **suicidal thoughts (18.0%)**, substance use (17.5%).

**18% of high-disclosure conversations touch suicidal ideation.** That is the safety-surface sizing
number for the platform. Crisis content is not an edge case in this product; it is a routine load.

## Wellbeing findings

- **Companionship use consistently predicted lower well-being**, across three measurement approaches
  (β = −0.47, p<.001 for self-reported motivation). The negative association **strengthened with greater
  interaction intensity and greater self-disclosure**.
- Users with **smaller social networks** showed stronger companionship-seeking and stronger self-disclosure
  (β = −0.11, p<.001).
- **Paradox**: general chatbot *intensity* correlated with **higher** wellbeing (β = 0.26, p<.001), but
  **companionship-specific use reversed this benefit.**
- Social Compensation hypothesis supported for *motivation*, but **"negligible interaction effect between
  social network scale and chatbot companionship"** — AI companions **failed to compensate** for lacking
  human relationships. The authors describe a **"reinforcing cycle of vulnerability."**

## The key methodological lesson

The direction of the intensity effect **flips sign depending on whether the use is companionship-framed.**
Raw intensity (messages, sessions, time) is *positively* associated with wellbeing in general; the same
intensity within companionship use is *negatively* associated.

**Therefore: an undifferentiated engagement metric is not merely noisy, it is sign-ambiguous.** The same
+10% in session length can be a good outcome or a harm depending on a latent variable (companionship
framing × self-disclosure × offline support) that the engagement metric cannot see. Any engagement signal
we collect must be **conditioned on conversation type**, or it will average a benefit and a harm together
and report zero.
