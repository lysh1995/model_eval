# Funder's Realistic Accuracy Model — a validated CASCADE, and it says our cascade is at the wrong altitude

**RAM is the closest structural analogue to the ability model that psychology has**, and it is
30 years old. It decomposes "accurately judging another person" into four **necessary, ordered,
multiplicative** stages. The ability model's central and best claim — *failures cascade downward,
never upward* — is **not novel and is supported**. But RAM locates the cascade **inside L1**, not
across L1→L2→L3, and that reframing is the most useful thing in this file.

---

## 1. The four stages

> "The four stages in the process of making accurate judgments are **relevance, availability,
> detection, and utilization**."

| stage | RAM | whose property? |
|---|---|---|
| **Relevance** | the target must actually *do* something relevant to the trait | **the target / the sheet** |
| **Availability** | that information must be *available* to the judge | **the target / the sheet** |
| **Detection** | the judge must *notice* the available cue | the judge |
| **Utilization** | the judge must *correctly use* the detected cue | the judge |

On utilization:

> "Utilization includes several more specific abilities, including being able to determine which cues
> are relevant to the trait being judged, **giving appropriate weights to various cues, combining the
> cues with each other**, and accounting for other characteristics, such as those of the situation,
> that may influence behavior."

## 2. The structural claim — necessity + multiplicativeness ★★★

Section heading verbatim: **"Necessity of Each Stage and Multiplicativeness of the Stages"**.

> "Within the RAM, these stages are all described as **necessary**, which means that **if any stage
> is unsuccessful, an accurate judgment is not possible** (Funder, 1995). If the target does not do
> anything relevant to creativity, then the process cannot even be started. If cues are not made
> available, then there is nothing for the judge to detect. If the judge does not detect the
> available cues, then there is nothing to be used to make a judgment. And if the judge does not
> correctly use the detected cue, or uses the wrong cues, then the judgment will not be accurate."

> "The stages are also **multiplicative**, meaning that the level of accuracy of the judgment could
> theoretically be determined by **multiplying the levels of success for each stage**."

> "Another implication of the multiplicative nature of the stages is that **100% accuracy is
> unlikely, because this is only possible with 100% success at each stage of the model**."

**Honest caveat RAM itself makes — and we should copy the honesty:**

> "However, success is **not measured in a way that is objective and quantitative enough to actually
> assign a number to each stage**, so levels of accuracy are not really determined in this way.
> Rather, **this is a conceptual idea** and demonstrates that accuracy is influenced by the level of
> success at each stage."

⚠️ **Read that last quote carefully.** RAM has had 30 years, a research program, and dozens of
labs — and it *still* cannot assign numbers to its stages. Its cascade remains "a conceptual idea".
The ability model proposes to measure L1/L2/L3 separately and stop at the first failure. **RAM is the
warning that the stages of a person-perception cascade are much easier to name than to isolate.** Our
§5 "Honest status" table should absorb this: the cascade's *diagnostic* value (which layer broke?)
depends on being able to attribute a failure to a layer, and RAM's 30-year track record says
attribution is the hard part, not the taxonomy.

## 3. The moderators — and why two of them are the character sheet, not the model ★★

> "The four moderators are the **good judge, good target, good trait, and good information**."

- **Good judge** — "good judges have high levels of intelligence and cognitive ability, knowledge
  about how behaviors and personality are related, agreeableness and social skills, perspective
  taking and empathy, and good psychological adjustment"
- **Good target** — "good targets have high levels of psychological adjustment, **behavioral
  consistency**, social skills, extraversion, and expressiveness"
- **Good trait** — "**good traits are easily observable in most situations**"
- **Good information** — two aspects:
  > "**Information quantity** reflects that having more information tends to result in more accurate
  > judgments than having less information, which is referred to as the **acquaintanceship effect**"
  > "The other aspect, **information quality**, reflects that some types of information are more
  > useful for making more accurate judgments than other types of information. Highly useful
  > information includes information about **general and specific behaviors, and thoughts and
  > feelings**."

### Why this matters more than anything else in the file

**Relevance and availability are properties of the TARGET, not the judge.** Applied to us: the
character sheet is the target. **If the sheet doesn't specify anything relevant to a trait, no model
can be accurate about it, and that is not a model defect.**

The ability model already half-discovered this. §5 says:

> "*If the hypothesis holds, it's a product lever we own:* character quality would be partly an
> **authoring** problem, fixable in the authoring UI, rather than a model problem fixable only by
> buying a better model."

**RAM says this is not a hypothesis — it is two of the four stages, and it has 30 years of support.**
"Good target" and "good information" are *named, studied moderators*. We should stop framing L1 as a
model score and start reporting it as **model × sheet**, with the sheet-side variance broken out.
Concretely, RAM licenses:

- **`information quantity`** → the sheet-length perturbation (L2.2's design pointed at length). RAM
  predicts a positive effect and names it the **acquaintanceship effect**. Note 15's suggestive
  result (en +0.324, zh +0.274) is *this effect*, and RAM is the prior that makes n=45 at p=0.07
  worth pursuing rather than dropping.
- **`information quality`** → "general and specific behaviors, and thoughts and feelings" are the
  *high-quality* information types. **That is a directly testable, pre-registered prediction about
  what a good character sheet contains** — and it's the "house style guide for writing character
  sheets" deliverable (§3 L2.3) handed to us with an empirical basis instead of being derived from
  our own scratch experiments.
- **`good trait`** = "easily observable in most situations" → **L1 agreement will vary by trait
  type, systematically and predictably.** The ability model's flat κ≈0.78–0.94 for all of L1 is
  therefore wrong in shape: agreement should be high for behavioral/observable traits and low for
  internal ones. (See `psych-kenny-social-relations-model.md` for the matching consensus numbers.)
- **`good target` includes "behavioral consistency"** → note the circularity risk: consistent targets
  are judged more accurately. Our L2.1 (consistency) and L1 (comprehension) are **not independent**;
  a consistent character is easier to comprehend. This partially **undermines the claim that L1 and
  L2 are cleanly separable layers** — RAM says target consistency is an input to *judgment accuracy*.

## 4. The relationship moderator — our "chemistry" claim, already named ★★

> "In addition to these moderators, theoretical work on the RAM (Funder, 1995) also proposed that
> **each moderator could interact with every other moderator** and used the following terms to
> describe these interactions: **relationship** (interaction between judge and target), **expertise**
> (judge and trait), **sensitivity** (judge and information), **palpability** (target and trait),
> **divulgence** (target and information), and **diagnosticity** (trait and information)."

> "The **relationship moderator** operates when something about the **unique alignment between a
> target and a judge** results in that judge being more accurate about the target than are any other
> judges and more accurate than they are when they are judging anyone else. For example, the quality
> of relationship between a husband and a wife could result in the husband judging his wife more
> accurately than anyone else judges her. **His augmented ability to make an accurate judgment would
> not extend to other targets—just to his wife.**"

**That is precisely the ability model's "chemistry is a user×character×model interaction" worry,
formalized in 1995 and named `relationship`.** And it is a *documented, non-negligible* term, not a
nuisance — see `psych-kenny-social-relations-model.md` for the variance figures, which are worse than
we feared.

**Three-way interaction, empirically measured:**

> "A meta-analysis by Connelly and Ones (2010) went a step further and examined how accuracy for
> specific traits differed across types of relationships, and therefore examined a **three-way
> interaction between judge, target, and trait**. **Accuracy for judgments of extraversion differed
> little across relationships, but accuracy for judgments of emotional stability, openness, and
> agreeableness varied quite a bit across relationships.** In particular, accuracy for these traits
> tended to be higher in more intimate relationships (i.e., family and friends) than less intimate
> relationships."

**This is the single most actionable empirical result in this file.** It says the judge×target
interaction is **not uniform across traits** — it is ≈0 for observable traits (extraversion) and
large for internal ones (emotional stability, openness, agreeableness). Applied to us:

> **Our L1/L2 measurements will be stable and comparable for the observable/behavioral parts of a
> character sheet (speech style, catchphrases, overt behavior), and will be dominated by
> model×character interaction for the internal parts (motivations, "what they'd never admit",
> emotional depth).**

Note the irony: **L1.1's flagship probe — "*What does this character want that they'd never admit?*"
— targets exactly the trait class with the WORST cross-judge agreement.** Our highest-agreement claim
sits on our lowest-agreement construct. That probe is not wrong, but it cannot be the one we cite
κ≈0.9 for.

Funder also warns generally:

> "It is easy to see how the process could get **highly complex very quickly**, as judges have to
> deal with multiple cues and decide which cues are relevant to which traits ... Furthermore, the
> **situation in which the interactions take place can also change the meaning of the cues.** For
> example, a target laughing at a party could mean something different and be utilized quite
> differently than a target laughing while playing a violent video game."

(→ cues are situation-dependent; cf. Mischel/CAPS and Asch's configural finding. The "meaning of a
trait depends on context" theme recurs in every literature we checked.)

## 5. Realism about the ceiling

> "much research supports the conclusion that judges can often make **fairly accurate** judgments of
> the personality traits of others (Brown & Bernieri, 2017; Colman et al., 2017; Connelly & Ones,
> 2010), and therefore the level of success for each of the stages must usually be quite high."

RAM begins from realism about the construct:

> "RAM begins with the assumption that **personality traits are real attributes of individuals**."

(This matters: RAM is the *anti-Mischel* position. It presupposes traits are real and judgeable. See
`psych-mischel-person-situation.md` / `psych-caps-behavioral-signatures.md` for the other side. Our
framework currently assumes RAM's position without noticing there is a debate.)

## 6. Verdict for the ability model

**SUPPORTS:**
- The cascade grammar itself: necessary, ordered, multiplicative stages where an early failure makes
  later success impossible. **"Failures cascade downward, never upward" is a real and respectable
  model form.** RAM is the citation.
- The "test in order, stop at the first failure" logic follows from necessity.
- The intuition that authoring is a lever (relevance/availability/good information are target-side).

**COMPLICATES / CONTRADICTS:**
- **The cascade is inside L1, not across L1→L2→L3.** RAM spends four necessary stages getting to
  "did the judge understand the target?" — which is *all* of our L1. Our L1 is one box; RAM says it's
  four, and that two of them aren't about the judge at all. **Our L1 is under-decomposed, and its
  first two stages belong to the character sheet.**
- **RAM cannot assign numbers to its stages after 30 years** ("not measured in a way that is
  objective and quantitative enough"). Layer *attribution* is the hard part. Our §5 promise —
  convert a score into a diagnosis — is the expensive claim, not the taxonomy.
- **Flat κ across L1 is the wrong shape.** "Good trait" + Connelly & Ones say agreement is high for
  observable traits, low and interaction-dominated for internal ones.
- **L1 and L2 are not independent**: "good target" includes *behavioral consistency*, so consistency
  is an input to comprehension accuracy, not only a downstream consequence of it.
- **"Each moderator could interact with every other moderator"** — Funder proposed a full interaction
  lattice (relationship, expertise, sensitivity, palpability, divulgence, diagnosticity) and admits
  these "have not been tested as systematically". Cf. Cronbach's hall-of-mirrors
  (`psych-cronbach-ati.md`). Our steerability matrix is walking into this lattice.

**STEAL:**
1. Cite RAM as the precedent for the cascade. Stop presenting it as our invention.
2. **Split L1 into sheet-side (relevance/availability) and model-side (detection/utilization).**
   Report L1 as `model × sheet`, with sheet-side variance broken out. This is the single highest-value
   structural change RAM implies.
3. Adopt **good information (quantity × quality)** as the pre-registered authoring hypothesis. It
   turns note 15's underpowered finding into a confirmatory test of a 30-year-old effect.
4. Adopt **good trait / observability** as a *stratifier* on every L1 report. Never pool a κ across
   observable and internal traits.
5. Report the **relationship term explicitly** rather than hoping it's small (see SRM file).
