## The paradigm

Participants view black-and-white photographs cropped to the **eye region only** and choose which of
**four** emotion/mental-state words best describes what the person is thinking or feeling. Adult
revised version = 36 items. Scoring: "Participants received one point for each correct mental state
inference." (Warnell & Redcay's description of their administration.)

Child variants exist: 28 items / four options (school-age), and the **Simplified Eye Reading Test
(SERT)** — "nine black-and-white photos of an adult's eye region and asked which of two emotions (e.g.,
serious vs. joking) best described the picture."

This is the workhorse instrument of adult ToM research — and it is the one that the field's own
psychometricians have been dismantling for a decade.

## Reliability numbers (VERBATIM)

### Internal consistency — mediocre and inconsistent

Meta-analysis (Kittel et al. 2022), **119 effect sizes from 61 studies, 8,611 participants**:

> "the internal consistency of the test was acceptable (α = .73)"

An earlier meta-analytic estimate reported α = **.73, 95% CI [.65, .79]** across 21 effect sizes.

Individual studies scatter far below that:

| Study | Cronbach's α |
|---|---|
| Vellante et al. 2013 (Italian validation) | **.605** ("maximal weighted internal consistency reliability for the unidimensional model was .719") |
| Voracek & Dressler | ".63 in men, .60 in women" |
| Harkness et al. | ".58 in the 36 items version, .63 in the 28 items version" |
| Dehning et al. | "Cronbach's alpha: .70" |
| Persian version | **0.371** |
| College sample (n=93) | 0.58 |

Vellante's own summary of the prior literature: "Two studies found low internal coherence, as measured
by Cronbach's alpha" while "two other studies found acceptable internal coherence."

**Read against `psycho-classical-test-theory.md`: α = .73 is below the .70-.90 band for anything
consequential, and α = .371 is not a measurement at all.** And recall Schmitt's point — alpha rises
mechanically with item count. A 36-item test that can only reach .73 is a test whose items barely
agree with each other.

### Test-retest — the one decent number

Vellante et al. 2013, n=36, **one-month interval**:

> "Test–retest reliability for the Eyes test, as measured by ICC, was .833 (95%CI = .745 to .902)"

But Yildirim et al. in Turkish adults:

> "ICC: .65 (95% CI: .49 to .77)"

So test-retest ranges from .65 to .83 depending on sample. Note the tension: **test-retest (.83) far
exceeds internal consistency (.60-.73)**. That pattern means the test stably measures *something* — it
just isn't one coherent thing. You get the same score twice from a bundle of unrelated items.

### Factor structure — fails, repeatedly, at scale

Higgins et al. 2023, **1,181 adults, demographically representative U.S. sample**, EFA on full sample
plus AQ-28 high/low subgroups:

> "identified overlapping, but distinct, three-factor models for the full sample and the two subgroups"
> — but "each of the three models showed inadequate model fit."

> "Nearly one-quarter of RMET items did not satisfy inclusion standards established in the original
> validation study."

> "significant caution is warranted when using the RMET as a measure of ToM" due to its "weak
> psychometric properties and the uncertain validity of individual items."

Higgins et al. 2026 — the replication at scale. **Nine non-clinical datasets, N ranging 558 to 9,267
(median = 1,112)**:

> "Neither of the theoretically derived models was a good fit for any of the nine datasets"

> "were unable to identify any better fitting multidimensional models"

> "Internal consistency metrics were acceptable in six of the nine datasets, but these metrics are
> difficult to interpret given the uncertain factor structures."

> "contribute[s] to a growing body of evidence questioning the reliability and validity of RMET scores."

**That last quoted caveat is the methodological lesson of the whole file: they are saying alpha is
uninterpretable when you don't know what the factor structure is.** Reporting alpha for a construct
you haven't established is reporting a number about nothing.

## The validity critique: does RMET measure ToM at all?

### The nomological network (Kittel et al. 2022) — it loads on emotion perception, not ToM

> "more strongly related with emotion perception (r = .33, ρ = .48) relative to alternative theory of
> mind measures (r = .29, ρ = .39)"

Plus:

| Correlate | r | ρ (corrected) |
|---|---|---|
| Emotion perception | .33 | **.48** |
| Alternative ToM measures | .29 | **.39** |
| **Vocabulary** | **.25** | **.32** |
| Cognitive empathy | .14 | .20 |
| Affective empathy | .13 | .19 |

Conclusion: **"the RMET operates rather as an emotion perception measure than as a theory of mind
measure."**

Do the arithmetic on the ToM number: ρ = .39 ⇒ **ρ² ≈ .15. The RMET shares roughly 15% of its variance
with other ToM measures — and 23% with emotion perception.** The instrument the field calls its ToM
gold standard has more in common with an emotion-labelling task than with the rest of its own
construct. And vocabulary (ρ = .32) is nearly as good a predictor of RMET as other ToM tasks are —
i.e. **your word knowledge predicts your "mind reading" about as well as your mind reading does.**

### The dissociation experiment (Oakley et al. 2016)

Design: "Nineteen participants (five female) with a clinical diagnosis of ASD and 24 (11 female)
without ASD volunteered to take part in the study." Compared RMET against the **MASC** (Movie for the
Assessment of Social Cognition), with alexithymia (TAS-20) measured separately.

Results:

> "the alexithymic group (M = 24.71, SD = 2.84) exhibited significantly worse RMET performance than the
> nonalexithymic group, M = 27.39, SD = 3.24; t(40) = 2.63, p = .012, d = 0.861"

> "The ASD (M = 26.32, SD = 3.77) and alexithymia-matched control (M = 26.65, SD = 2.99) groups did not
> differ on RMET score, t(40) = 0.32, p = .749"

> "the control group (M = 35.70, SD = 3.31) performed significantly better on the MASC than the ASD
> group, M = 31.22, SD = 4.60; t(39) = 3.62, p = .001"

Conclusion:

> "Alexithymia, rather than ASD diagnosis, predicted RMET performance, while ASD diagnosis and symptom
> severity, rather than alexithymia, predicted performance on the MASC task. These results suggest that
> the RMET may be better characterized as a test of emotion recognition than of mental state
> understanding."

**A clean double dissociation.** RMET tracks alexithymia and is blind to ASD; MASC tracks ASD and is
blind to alexithymia. Two instruments both labelled "ToM" measure different constructs — and you only
discover this by testing them against each other in the same sample.

### Corroboration from the convergence literature

Warnell & Redcay 2019 (see `psych-tom-task-convergence.md`) found adult RMET correlating **-.069** with
higher-order ToM and **-.115** with spontaneous ToM — i.e. slightly *negative*. And RMET correlated
with **full-scale IQ at rho=.24 (p=.054)** in middle childhood. Warnell & Redcay flagged the dispute in
their own methods section:

> "although there is ongoing debate in the field about whether and how some of our employed measures
> capture ToM (e.g., Reading the Mind in the Eyes; Oakley et al., 2016; Peterson and Miller, 2012),
> these instruments continue to be widely used in the literature as ToM measures"

**That last clause is the sociological finding**: the field knows, and uses it anyway. Twenty-five
years, thousands of papers, an instrument that doesn't measure the thing in its name. Inertia beat
psychometrics because the test was easy to administer and produced a tidy number.

## Why this matters for the L1/L2/L3 framework

1. **RMET is the cautionary tale for a "comprehension score."** It is exactly what our L1 wants to be:
   a fast, objectively-scored, high-agreement probe of "can you read a mind from a thin slice of
   evidence." Every item has a keyed correct answer — maximally "bound" in the framework's sense. And
   after 25 years it turns out to measure vocabulary and emotion-word recognition. **Bound-ness bought
   scoring agreement and bought nothing else.** If L1 is operationalized as a set of keyed-answer
   probes, the RMET is the null result we should expect by default, not the success case.

2. **The specific failure mode is the one we're most exposed to: the construct collapses into general
   competence.** RMET ~ vocabulary at ρ=.32, nearly matching RMET ~ ToM at ρ=.39. The model analogue is
   direct — an L1 comprehension probe over a character card is likely to be substantially a reading-
   comprehension probe, i.e. a re-measurement of general model capability. It would rank models the way
   MMLU already does and tell us nothing character-specific. **The test: does our L1 score have
   incremental validity over a general-capability baseline?** If L1 rank ≈ MMLU rank, L1 is not a layer,
   it's a proxy. This is the cheapest possible falsification of L1's existence and we should run it first.

3. **"High inter-rater agreement" is not evidence of anything the framework wants it to be.** RMET has
   essentially perfect scoring agreement — it's multiple choice against a key. That fact coexists with
   α = .371 in one sample, model fit failing in all nine large datasets, and a construct label that is
   simply wrong. The framework's inference "bound ⇒ has a referent ⇒ high agreement ⇒ trustworthy" has
   a broken final link. **Agreement is necessary and nowhere near sufficient.** Meanwhile the framework
   treats L3's low agreement as its distinguishing weakness — but low agreement is at least *honest*
   about uncertainty. RMET's high agreement actively concealed a quarter-century of invalidity. High
   agreement on the wrong construct is worse than low agreement on the right one.

4. **Report structure before reliability.** Higgins et al. 2026's line — internal consistency "difficult
   to interpret given the uncertain factor structures" — is the rule for our own metrics. If we ship an
   L1 composite, we owe a factor analysis of its sub-probes *before* we quote its alpha. Otherwise alpha
   is a number about an object we haven't shown exists. Use parallel analysis (Warnell & Redcay's
   method), not eigenvalue>1.

5. **Discriminant validity is the missing test in the framework.** The framework asserts three layers.
   It has no proposed test that L1, L2, L3 are *distinct*. RMET's history says the risk isn't that the
   layers are hard to measure — it's that they'll turn out to be one factor (general capability) wearing
   three labels. The MASC/RMET double dissociation is the template for what we'd need to show: find a
   manipulation that moves L1 and not L3, and another that moves L3 and not L1. Absent that, three
   layers is a taxonomy, not a model.
