# John & Robins (1993), *Journal of Personality*, 61(4), 521–551; plus Funder's Realistic Accuracy Model

**This file directly attacks the framework's claim that L1 comprehension is measurable at κ ≈ 0.78–0.94.** The claim's justification is that L1 is "bound" to the character sheet as a referent — there's a written answer key, so raters should converge. The personality-judgment literature says: **having a referent is not sufficient. Agreement is a strong function of WHICH TRAIT is being judged, and the spread is enormous.** A single uniform κ band across an L1 rubric is not a thing that happens in this literature.

## John & Robins (1993) — the four determinants of interjudge agreement

Abstract, verbatim:

> "We examined several determinants of interjudge agreement on personality traits. The findings, which were cross-validated in two samples, suggest that **agreement is a function of four factors: which Big Five content domain the trait represents, how observable relevant behaviors are, how evaluative the trait is, and whether the self is one of the judges. Agreement was highest for traits related to Extraversion and lowest for traits related to Agreeableness. More observable and less evaluative traits elicited higher interjudge agreement.** On average, self-peer agreement was lower than peer-peer agreement. However, this effect was limited to evaluative traits; for neutral traits, self-peer agreement was as high as peer-peer agreement."

### The numbers (Study 1: 76 traits; Study 2: reanalysis of McCrae & Costa 1987, 80 bipolar scales)

**Observability → agreement:**

> "As expected, **observability correlated positively with both peer-peer (r = .36) and self-peer agreement (r = .38)**; that is, agreement was higher on observable traits."

> "Peer-peer agreement averaged **.27 for relatively observable traits, as contrasted with .22 for unobservable traits**, and self-peer agreement averaged **.22 for observable traits, as contrasted with .16 for unobservable traits.**"

Study 2 cross-validation: observability × peer-peer = **.37**, × self-peer = **.50**.

**Evaluativeness → agreement (negative):**

> "the relation between evaluativeness and agreement across the 76 traits was **stronger for self-peer (r = −.53) than for peer-peer agreement (r = −.35)**"

Study 2: **−.29** (peer-peer), **−.53** (self-peer).

**Table 1 — correlations of agreement indices with trait properties (across traits):**

| Trait property | Study 1 peer-peer | Study 1 self-peer | Study 2 peer-peer | Study 2 self-peer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Observability | .36** | .38** | .37** | .50** |
| Desirability | −.04 | .09 | — | — |
| Evaluativeness | −.35** | −.53** | −.29* | −.53** |
| Extraversion (domain) | .37** | .32** | .17 | .18* |
| Agreeableness (domain) | −.34** | −.14 | −.19* | −.27* |
| Conscientiousness | −.17 | .22* | −.06 | −.16 |
| Emotional Stability | .08 | −.02 | −.07 | .13 |
| Intellect/Openness | .04 | .51** | .09 | .01 |
| **Multiple R** | **.48** | **.63** | **.29** | **.65** |

*(Transcribed from the scanned original; ** p<.01, * p<.05. Some Study 2 cells not computed.)*

**Note the multiple R: .48 to .65.** Trait properties predict agreement *substantially*. Agreement is not a constant plus noise — **it is systematically driven by what kind of thing you're asking raters to judge.**

**Desirability shows a curvilinear (not linear) relation:** traits that were *either* highly desirable or highly undesirable elicited lower agreement; neutral traits elicited the most. Mechanism: evaluative traits are ego-involving, which distorts self-perception:

> "Judging oneself on traits that are extremely evaluative (either desirable or undesirable) is more ego-involving than judging oneself on neutral traits. In contrast, making judgments about another person is typically less ego-involving… Consequently, evaluative traits should produce more bias in self-perceptions than in peer perceptions"

## Funder & Dobroth (1987) — the origin of the visibility finding

> "Funder & Dobroth (1987) provided evidence linking the observability of a trait and its social desirability to interjudge agreement"

Their measure: "**apparent ease of observation or easy visibility**." Result: **more observable Q-sort items elicited higher levels of agreement than less observable items.**

## Funder's Realistic Accuracy Model (RAM) — the mechanism

Accuracy in judging a trait requires **four sequential conditions**, all of which must be met:

> "first, the targets have to show **observable cues** that are relevant to the trait; second, these observable cues must be **available** to the informant; third, the informant must **detect** the cues; and finally, the cues must be **utilized** by the informant when making the judgment."

Four stages: **Relevance → Availability → Detection → Utilization.** Because they're multiplicative, **the whole chain fails if any link fails** — which is why agreement varies by trait rather than being uniform.

The canonical contrast:

> "**Extraversion is a good trait** because it's related to behaviors such as **talking in a loud voice, moving quickly, and behaving boldly — all of which are highly available.** In terms of self-other agreement, people of all perspectives were equally good at judging extraversion-related traits"

> "In contrast, **neuroticism is related to how people interpret situations and feelings of anxiety and other negative emotions — which are often not highly available — and therefore neuroticism is not a good trait.** However, although neuroticism is difficult to judge in another person, the thoughts and feelings related to neuroticism are available to the self and therefore this trait is more accurately judged by the self than by others."

RAM's four moderators of accuracy: **good judge, good target, good trait, good information** (quantity and quality). The "good trait" moderator is the one that kills a uniform-κ assumption.

## The direct empirical confirmation on FICTIONAL characters

**See `psych-rating-character-portrayal.md` for the full source.** The headline, because it belongs here too — Rasmussen (2005) had 8 psychology students rate the Big Five (via TIPI) of four **movie characters**. Inter-rater ICCs:

| Trait | ICC | Visibility prediction |
|---|---|---|
| **Extraversion** | **0.88** | highly visible → high agreement ✓ |
| Conscientiousness | 0.68 | moderately visible ✓ |
| Agreeableness | 0.50 | evaluative → depressed ✓ (matches J&R: lowest domain) |
| **Neuroticism** | **0.37** | internal → low agreement ✓ |
| **Openness** | **0.05** | internal/abstract → near-zero ✓ |

> "agreement for the five-factor model ranged from **0.05 to 0.88**"

**This is the trait-visibility ordering reproducing almost perfectly — on fictional characters, with a fixed referent (the film), with trained-ish raters.** Extraversion .88 vs Openness .05 is an **18-fold** spread on the same instrument, same raters, same targets.

Corroborating, at scale: the *Game of Thrones* study (309 fans rating 56 characters on Big Five + Dark Tetrad) found consensus correlations "ranging from **.54 for narcissism to .83 for agreeableness** (M = .66, SD = .10)" — a narrower spread (aggregation across 309 raters helps enormously) but still a **~1.5× range**, and still significantly trait-dependent.

## Does rater training fix it?

Partially — and this is the constructive half of the file.

**Woehr & Huffcutt (1994)** meta-analysis of rater training, and **Roch & Woehr (2012)** update ("Rater training revisited: An updated meta-analytic review of frame-of-reference training," *JOOP*, 85, 370–395, ~4× as many studies):

- Four training types compared: rater error training, behavioural observation training, performance dimension training, and **frame-of-reference (FOR) training**.
- **FOR training is the most effective**, with the others showing more moderate effects.
- Effect sizes: **d = .77 on appraisal accuracy, d = .49 on observational accuracy.**
- Mechanism: "Frame-of-reference training builds a common construct between raters, which they use to evaluate subjects, and is accomplished by **explicitly defining terms on the tool and giving examples of performance levels** that would be expected for a given rating."
- Important limit from the 2012 update: "**not all operationalizations of accuracy are equally improved by FOR training**"; Borman's *differential accuracy* and behavioural accuracy improve most.

**So: training helps, substantially (d≈.77 is large), but it works by installing a shared frame — anchored exemplars. It does not make an invisible trait visible.** FOR training can fix "we disagree about what 'warm' means on this scale." It cannot fix "there is no observable cue for openness in this stimulus."

## Implications for our framework

1. **The uniform κ = 0.78–0.94 claim for L1 is not plausible as stated.** The best available evidence — on *fictional characters*, with a *fixed referent*, which is our exact condition — shows agreement ranging **0.05 to 0.88 across the Big Five.** Our claimed band (.78–.94) would sit at or *above* the Extraversion ceiling (.88) **for every trait**, including ones the literature reliably scores near zero. The pilot that produced .78–.94 was almost certainly run on visible, concrete, behaviourally-anchored sheet attributes. **Predicted failure: the moment the rubric covers inner-life attributes ("curious," "open-minded," "conflicted," "insecure"), κ will collapse.**

2. **"Bound to the character sheet as a referent" does not deliver uniform agreement — this is the framework's core L1 error.** The movie-character raters *had* a fixed referent (the film) and still hit ICC = .05 on Openness. A referent constrains *what there is to be right about*; it does not create observable cues for internal states. **A character sheet that says "she is deeply curious" gives raters a proposition, not a cue.** Whether a given response manifests curiosity is exactly as unobservable as it is in real people. The referent fixes the target; it does not fix the evidence.

3. **We can predict, in advance, which sheet attributes will be measurable — this is actionable, not just critical.** Score every sheet attribute on **observability** and **evaluativeness** *before* the rating study (both are ratable properties; John & Robins did exactly this for 76 traits). Predicted κ ordering:
   - **HIGH agreement** (κ ≈ .7–.9): behaviourally-manifest, low-evaluative attributes — speech register, verbosity, catchphrases, stated facts/backstory consistency, extraversion-like sociability, formality.
   - **MEDIUM** (κ ≈ .4–.6): conscientiousness-like, agreeableness-like (evaluative → depressed; note J&R found Agreeableness the *lowest* Big Five domain for peer-peer, r = −.34 with agreement).
   - **LOW** (κ ≈ .0–.4): neuroticism-like inner states, openness/intellect, motivations, "depth," anything evaluative-and-internal.
   
   **This gives L1 a defensible structure: report κ per attribute class, and only claim high-agreement measurement for the observable band.** That is a *stronger* framework than a uniform claim, because it will survive contact with data.

4. **Stratify the L1 construct or the causal ordering breaks.** The framework's L1→L2→L3 ordering assumes L1 is the solid foundation. If L1 agreement is .88 on some attributes and .05 on others, **L1 is not one construct** — it is a high-reliability subscale plus a noise subscale, and averaging them produces a number whose reliability depends entirely on the item mix. Worse, a low-reliability L1 cannot support any claim that L1 *causes* or *bounds* L2 — attenuation guarantees the correlation will be near zero regardless of the truth.

5. **Invest in FOR training — it's the highest-leverage cheap win here.** d = .77 on appraisal accuracy is a large effect, and the mechanism ("explicitly defining terms on the tool and giving examples of performance levels") is exactly what an eval rubric with anchored exemplars *is*. Concrete: for every L1 attribute, ship 3–5 anchored example responses per rating level. But set expectations honestly — **FOR training raises agreement on judgeable traits; it does not rescue unjudgeable ones**, and the 2012 update warns not all accuracy operationalizations improve equally.

6. **Aggregate raters, and report the aggregation.** The GoT study got .54–.83 with **309 raters**; the movie study got .05–.88 with **8**. Spearman-Brown means agreement is partly a budget decision. But note what aggregation does and doesn't do: it improves the *reliability of the mean rating*, not the *judgeability of the trait*. Openness at ICC .05 with 8 raters is not a solved problem at 300 raters — it's a more precisely measured consensus about something raters have no evidence for, which is a validity problem wearing a reliability solution's clothes.
