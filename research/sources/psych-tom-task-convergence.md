# Warnell & Redcay 2019 — ToM tasks do not converge (Cognition 191:103997)

Preprint full text: http://dscn.umd.edu/papers/Warnell%20and%20Redcay%20-%202019%20-%20Minimal%20coherence%20among%20varied%20theory%20of%20mind%20meas.pdf

**This is the single most damaging paper for any framework that treats "comprehension" as one
measurable layer.** It is the direct empirical test of whether ToM is one construct. Answer: no.

## The question

> "As a result, theoretical and meta-analytic work has begun to question whether such varied
> approaches to theory of mind should be categorized as capturing a single construct."

Framing of the two competing accounts (Introduction):

> "Apperly (2012) similarly compared conceptual theories of ToM—which would argue for coherence
> among tasks—with cognitive theories, in which ToM is modelled not as a state of conceptual
> knowledge but as an interactive process spanning multiple cognitive abilities."

> "Schaafsma and colleagues (2015) surveyed the vast array of different tasks measuring ToM and
> argued for the deconstruction of ToM into varied component processes (e.g., gaze processing,
> tracking intentions) rather than for ToM to be considered a single construct."

Critically, they note the prior literature is **publication-biased toward convergence**:

> "Papers introducing new ToM tasks often examine their relation with one or two existing tasks
> (e.g., Peterson & Slaughter, 2009; Beaumont et al., 2008; Devine & Hughes, 2013), but this
> literature may be biased to include positive relations, as new tasks that fail to show such
> relations may remain unpublished."

And that fMRI overlap does not rescue it:

> "In the realm of neuroimaging research, meta-analytic evidence of overlapping activation across
> ToM tasks (e.g., Schurz et al., 2014; Molenberghs et al., 2016) does not necessarily indicate that
> such tasks tap into the same underlying mental process in particular individuals."

## Design

Three samples, all given a *deliberately diverse* battery (varying modality, affective vs cognitive,
explicit vs spontaneous):

| Sample | Final N | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Four-year-olds | 63 (25 males, avg age 54 months) | |
| Six-year-olds | 64 (29 males, avg age 78 months) | |
| Middle childhood (7-12) | 66 (28 males, avg age 9.82y) | |
| Adults | 222 (102 males, avg age 20.3y, SD=3.0y) | n=137 completed all five tasks |

Note the power decision — they *increased* N after a null result specifically to rule out power:

> "Our analyses suggested that ToM measures were not related to each other. To ensure that these
> results were not due to limited power, we then increased our sample size. Specifically, we
> targeted a sample size for each age group that would have 80% power to detect moderate
> correlations (approximately r=.35)"

### Tasks — early childhood (4 & 6-year-olds)
- **False belief battery** — "False belief content: a box contained an object different from that on
  the label, and children were asked what a character would think was in the box (2 trials
  presented)"; "False belief location: an object was moved unbeknownst to a character, and children
  were asked where the character would look (2 trials presented)"; "Second-order false belief".
- **Simplified Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (SERT)** — "nine black-and-white photos of an
  adult's eye region and asked which of two emotions (e.g., serious vs. joking) best described the
  picture."
- **Appearance-Reality Emotion** — "five stories which the protagonist had reason to hide an
  emotional state and children had to identify the discrepancy between real and apparent emotion."
- **Appearance-Reality Object** (4yo only) — "an object that had a false appearance (e.g., chocolate
  that looked like a rock)."
- **Faux Pas** (6yo only) — "four short vignettes that each presented a social scenario and had to
  identify whether a faux pas was committed and, if so, why it was a faux pas."
- **Restricted View** (6yo only) — occluded picture, what would two naive dolls think it was.

### Tasks — middle childhood (7-12)
- **School-age RMET** — "28 black-and-white photos of an adult's eye region and asked which of four
  emotions best described the picture."
- **Strange Stories (Happé)** — "eight stories involving mental states (e.g., double crossing, white
  lie) and were asked to explain the motivation behind a character's statement."
- **Faux Pas** — eight vignettes.

### Tasks — adults (five tasks)
1. **Spontaneous ToM Protocol (STOMP)** — two silent film clips, free written description.
   Scoring: "The STOMP ratio was calculated by taking the number of internal statements and dividing
   by the number of total statements and multiplying by 100."
2. **Belief Reasoning Speed** (Apperly et al. 2011) — "Belief reasoning speed was calculated by
   subtracting reaction time for the true belief trials … from RT for the false belief trials …
   This provided a measure of how much participants were slowed down by representing a false belief."
3. **Pragmatic language comprehension** — sentence-pair rejoinder judgments incl. sarcasm.
   "a composite score of pragmatic language ability was created, which subtracted out the percent
   accuracy score of the 44 physical causality items from percent accuracy on the 88 pragmatic items."
4. **Adult RMET** — four emotion options per photo.
5. **Higher-order ToM** — "analysis of the ToM stories was restricted to second and third order
   questions (45 memory and 45 ToM) and a composite score (Higher Order ToM) was calculated by
   subtracting percent accuracy on the memory items from percent accuracy on the ToM items."

**Note the design principle**: each adult composite *subtracts a matched control condition* to
isolate the ToM component from the general-cognition component. This is exactly the "does the
measure have discriminant validity" move.

## The factor analysis: a ZERO-FACTOR SOLUTION

Method — parallel analysis (not scree/eigenvalue>1, which they explicitly criticize):

> "we generated one thousand random, normally distributed datasets that were similar to the original
> data in sample size and number of items simulated. Then, the eigenvalues from the real data set
> were compared to eigenvalues derived from the 95th percentile of the simulated data sets and we
> retained any components with eigenvalues greater than 95% of those generated by random simulation."

Result:

> "Results from the parallel analysis suggest the data has a **zero-factor solution** (Figure 1).
> Specifically, none of the eigenvalues exceeded the 95th percentile cutoff from randomly generated
> data that mimicked the actual data in sample size and number of items."

That is: **the five adult ToM tasks share no common variance distinguishable from random noise.**

## Bayesian evidence FOR the null

> "For all pairwise comparisons except between the false belief composite and object
> appearance-reality task, evidence was in favor of the null. Depending on the specific task
> comparison, the data were 2 to 6 times more likely to have occurred under the null (i.e., no
> relation) than the alternative (i.e., a relation)"

This is not a power failure dressed up as a null. They evidenced the null directly.

## What DID cohere (the important nuance)

Within-paradigm, things cohere fine:

> "The three false belief tasks (location, contents, and second-order) were related to each other
> across both age groups, even after controlling for syntactic ability, overall verbal performance,
> and age (rhos > 0.36, ps < 0.001)."

And the one cross-task relation (4yo FB ↔ object appearance-reality, rho=.274) is discounted by the
authors themselves:

> "As some researchers, however, classify the two tasks as conceptually identical (Liu, Wellman,
> Tardif, & Sabbagh, 2008), these relations have limited bearing on the question of whether a
> unitary construct underlies all facets of ToM."

**So: coherence exists *within* a paradigm and vanishes *between* paradigms.** The variance is in the
task, not the person.

## Confound check: ToM tasks are contaminated by language/IQ

- 4 & 6yo: "there was a significant relation between full-scale IQ and performance on the False
  Belief Composite (rho=.214, p=.02) and Appearance-Reality Emotion (rho=.295, p=.001), with stronger
  correlations with verbal IQ than non-verbal IQ for all three tasks."
- "Even controlling for age, CELF age equivalency scores remained significantly associated with
  performance on all three of these tasks common to both age groups (rhos>.33, ps<.001)."
- Middle childhood: "full-scale IQ was significantly related to Strange Stories performance
  (rho=.39, p<.01) and marginally related to Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (rho=.24, p=.054)"

**ToM tasks correlate better with IQ (rho .21-.39) than with each other (rho ~.00-.20).** A "ToM"
task is a better measure of verbal IQ than it is of any other "ToM" task.

## Authors' conclusion (verbatim)

> "For the tasks used, no clear structure underlying ToM emerged for any developmental period.
> Specifically, after controlling for potential confounding variables (e.g., age, verbal ability),
> ToM tasks were minimally correlated in early childhood, in middle childhood, and in adulthood, a
> finding which was supported by Bayesian analysis that endorsed the null hypothesis."

> "This finding could not be attributed to measurement issues such as dichotomous participant
> performance; all measures, even those typically conceptualized to represent all-or-nothing
> abilities, produced a range of scores."

> "these results … suggest that ToM is a diverse construct that likely intersects with an array of
> other social and cognitive abilities"

From the abstract:

> "All tasks showed wide variability in performance, indicating that children and adults often have
> inconsistent and partial mastery of theory of mind concepts. Further, for all ages studied, the
> selected theory of mind tasks showed minimal correlations with each other. That is, having high
> levels of theory of mind on one task did not predict performance on another task designed to
> measure the same underlying ability."

> "understanding others' internal states may be a multidimensional process that interacts with other
> abilities, a process which may not occur in a single conceptual framework."

Note also: "Our findings suggest that ToM does not fractionate over development, but rather shows
diverse structure from the preschool years." — it was never one thing, at any age.

## Why this matters for the L1/L2/L3 framework

This is the **strongest single piece of counter-evidence to the framework's L1 assumption**, and it
should be confronted rather than filed away.

1. **"Comprehension" is not one thing — and psychology already ran this experiment.** The team's L1
   ("can it understand a character from a description?") is structurally identical to "does this
   person have ToM?" Psychology spent 40 years assuming that was one construct, built dozens of
   tasks for it, and when someone finally administered them together the tasks did not correlate and
   parallel analysis returned **zero factors**. The prior on "our comprehension layer is a single
   measurable ability" should be low. If we build 5 comprehension probes for a character card, the
   default expectation from this literature is that a model's rank on probe 1 will not predict its
   rank on probe 5.

2. **"Bound ⇒ high inter-rater agreement" conflates two different reliabilities.** Warnell & Redcay
   is not about rater disagreement — the scoring of a false-belief task is nearly perfectly
   objective. Two raters agree completely on whether the child said "the basket." The failure is
   **convergent validity across items/tasks**, not inter-rater reliability. The framework's claim
   that L1/L2 are "bound" because they "have a referent" only buys you the *first* kind (raters
   agree on a given item). It buys you nothing on the second (items measure a common construct).
   **A layer can have perfect inter-rater agreement and still not be a construct.** This is the
   distinction the framework currently collapses, and this paper is the proof that the collapse is
   not merely theoretical.

3. **The within-paradigm/between-paradigm split is the practical design lesson.** rhos > 0.36 within
   the false-belief family; ~.00 between families. If we build a comprehension score out of one
   paradigm (say, 20 "what would the character do next" items), it will look beautifully reliable
   (high alpha) and will be measuring *that paradigm*, not comprehension. High alpha here is a
   symptom of narrowness, not a sign of validity — cf. `psycho-classical-test-theory.md` on alpha ≠
   unidimensionality. We would be shipping a number whose reliability is an artifact.

4. **Contamination**: their tasks correlated more with verbal IQ than with each other. The analogue:
   our L1 probes may correlate more with general model capability (parameter count, benchmark
   average) than with anything character-specific. Their mitigation is directly stealable — every
   adult composite **subtracts a matched non-ToM control condition** (higher-order ToM minus memory
   items of equal syntactic complexity; pragmatic minus physical-causality items). If we want an L1
   score that isn't just a re-measurement of raw model strength, each comprehension probe needs a
   matched control probe with identical surface demands and no character inference, and we should
   score the **difference**. Note their adult composites came out *negative* (Pragmatic -3.6,
   Higher-Order -3.4) — once you subtract the matched control, the "ToM advantage" is not merely
   small, it is absent.

5. **Cascade check**: the cascade claim requires L1 to be causally upstream of L2. But if L1 isn't a
   single thing, "L1 failure cascades to L2" is unfalsifiable — you can always find *some* probe the
   model failed and call it the upstream cause. A cascade needs a well-defined upstream variable.
   Zero factors means there is no such variable to put on the left side of the arrow.

**Caveat on transfer**: this is a human individual-differences result. Its logic (task variance
swamping person variance) transfers to models only if models likewise show idiosyncratic
task-specific profiles. The LLM ToM perturbation literature (see
`psych-llm-tom-ullman-perturbations.md`) says they do — arguably far more so. So the transfer is
if anything conservative.
