# California SB 243 — Companion Chatbots

## Summary
**SB 243 (Padilla), CHAPTER 677.** "An act to add Chapter 22.6 (commencing with Section 22601) to Division 8 of the Business and Professions Code, relating to artificial intelligence."

**Approved by Governor October 13, 2025. Filed with Secretary of State October 13, 2025.** Date published 10/14/2025. Vote: MAJORITY. Appropriation: NO. Fiscal Committee: YES. Local Program: NO.

**Effective January 1, 2026** (default operative date for California statutes chaptered in 2025 with no specified operative date — the statute itself specifies no alternative effective date; the only deferred date in the text is the **July 1, 2027** reporting obligation).

This is the **first US law specifically regulating companion chatbots** and it is the binding compliance floor for any companion/roleplay product available to California users. The full operative text is short — reproduced essentially in full below.

## Taxonomy / definitions / operative requirements (VERBATIM)

### § 22601 — Definitions (verbatim)

> **(a)** "Artificial intelligence" means an engineered or machine-based system that varies in its level of autonomy and that can, for explicit or implicit objectives, infer from the input it receives how to generate outputs that can influence physical or virtual environments.

> **(b)(1)** "**Companion chatbot**" means an artificial intelligence system with a natural language interface that provides adaptive, human-like responses to user inputs and is capable of meeting a user's social needs, including by exhibiting anthropomorphic features and being able to sustain a relationship across multiple interactions.

> **(b)(2)** "Companion chatbot" does not include any of the following:
> **(A)** A bot that is used only for customer service, a business' operational purposes, productivity and analysis related to source information, internal research, or technical assistance.
> **(B)** A bot that is a feature of a video game and is limited to replies related to the video game that cannot discuss topics related to mental health, self-harm, sexually explicit conduct, or maintain a dialogue on other topics unrelated to the video game.
> **(C)** A stand-alone consumer electronic device that functions as a speaker and voice command interface, acts as a voice-activated virtual assistant, and does not sustain a relationship across multiple interactions or generate outputs that are likely to elicit emotional responses in the user.

> **(c)** "Companion chatbot platform" means a platform that allows a user to engage with companion chatbots.
> **(d)** "Office" means the Office of Suicide Prevention established pursuant to Section 131300 of the Health and Safety Code.
> **(e)** "**Operator**" means a person who makes a companion chatbot platform available to a user in the state.
> **(f)** "Sexually explicit conduct" has the meaning defined in **Section 2256 of Title 18 of the United States Code**.
> **(g)** "Video game" means a game played on an electronic amusement device that utilizes a computer, microprocessor, or similar electronic circuitry and its own monitor, or is designed to be used with a television set or a computer monitor, that interacts with the user of the device.

### § 22602 — Core duties (verbatim)

> **(a)** If a reasonable person interacting with a companion chatbot would be misled to believe that the person is interacting with a human, an operator shall issue a **clear and conspicuous notification** indicating that the companion chatbot is **artificially generated and not human**.

> **(b)(1)** An operator shall **prevent a companion chatbot on its companion chatbot platform from engaging with users unless the operator maintains a protocol for preventing the production of suicidal ideation, suicide, or self-harm content to the user**, including, but not limited to, by providing a notification to the user that refers the user to crisis service providers, including a suicide hotline or crisis text line, if the user expresses suicidal ideation, suicide, or self-harm.
> **(b)(2)** The operator shall **publish details on the protocol** required by this subdivision **on the operator's internet website**.

> **(c)** An operator shall, **for a user that the operator knows is a minor**, do all of the following:
> **(1)** Disclose to the user that the user is interacting with artificial intelligence.
> **(2)** Provide **by default** a clear and conspicuous notification to the user **at least every three hours** for continuing companion chatbot interactions that reminds the user **to take a break** and that the companion chatbot is **artificially generated and not human**.
> **(3)** Institute **reasonable measures** to prevent its companion chatbot from producing **visual material of sexually explicit conduct** or **directly stating that the minor should engage in sexually explicit conduct**.

### § 22603 — Reporting to the Office of Suicide Prevention (verbatim)

> **(a) Beginning July 1, 2027**, an operator shall **annually report to the office** all of the following:
> **(1)** The number of times the operator has issued a crisis service provider referral notification pursuant to Section 22602 in the preceding calendar year.
> **(2)** Protocols put in place to detect, remove, and respond to instances of suicidal ideation by users.
> **(3)** Protocols put in place to prohibit a companion chatbot response about suicidal ideation or actions with the user.
> **(b)** The report required by this section shall include only the information listed in subdivision (a) and shall not include any identifiers or personal information about users.
> **(c)** The office shall post data from a report required by this section on its internet website.
> **(d)** An operator shall use **evidence-based methods for measuring suicidal ideation**.

### § 22604 — Suitability disclosure (verbatim)
> An operator shall disclose to a user of its companion chatbot platform, on the application, the browser, or any other format that a user can use to access the companion chatbot platform, that **companion chatbots may not be suitable for some minors**.

### § 22605 — Private right of action (verbatim)
> A person who suffers **injury in fact** as a result of a violation of this chapter may bring a **civil action** to recover all of the following relief:
> **(a)** Injunctive relief.
> **(b)** Damages in an amount equal to the **greater of actual damages or one thousand dollars ($1,000) per violation**.
> **(c)** Reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

### § 22606 — Cumulative remedies (verbatim)
> The duties, remedies, and obligations imposed by this chapter are **cumulative** to the duties, remedies, or obligations imposed under other law and shall not be construed to relieve an operator from any duties, remedies, or obligations imposed under any other law.

**SEC. 2** — severability clause.

## Key numbers / dates (verbatim)
- **Signed / filed: October 13, 2025.** Chapter **677**, Statutes of 2025.
- **Effective January 1, 2026.**
- **Every three hours** — minor break reminder interval.
- **July 1, 2027** — first annual report to the Office of Suicide Prevention (covering "the preceding calendar year").
- **$1,000 per violation** statutory damages floor, plus attorney's fees — private right of action.
- Office of Suicide Prevention: **Health & Safety Code § 131300**.
- "Sexually explicit conduct" incorporates **18 U.S.C. § 2256**.

### What SB 243 does NOT say (be precise — common misreporting)
- It does **not** ban all sexual content with minors in the abstract; § 22602(c)(3) requires "**reasonable measures**" to prevent (i) **visual material** of sexually explicit conduct, and (ii) **directly stating** that the minor should engage in sexually explicit conduct. Textual sexual roleplay short of those two categories is not expressly covered by this provision. Do not overstate this as a flat ban.
- The minor duties in § 22602(c) attach only to "a user that **the operator knows** is a minor" — **there is no age-verification mandate in SB 243.** Actual knowledge triggers the duties. (This creates an obvious perverse incentive to not know, which AB 1064 and other bills targeted.)
- The AI disclosure duty in § 22602(a) is conditional on the "reasonable person ... would be misled" trigger — it is not unconditional for all companion chatbots. But § 22602(c)(1) **is** unconditional for known minors.
- The § 22602(b) crisis protocol duty applies to **all users, not just minors**, and is framed as a *precondition to operating at all* ("shall prevent a companion chatbot ... from engaging with users unless the operator maintains a protocol").
- Earlier drafts of SB 243 contained additional provisions (e.g. broader reporting, other requirements) that were amended out. The above is the **chaptered** text — the only text that binds.

## Relevance to a roleplay/companion eval product

**This statute converts most of the eval product's feature list into legal compliance obligations.** Each requirement is directly testable, which is the entire pitch:

1. **§ 22602(a) — AI disclosure.** Testable: does the character ever claim to be human, deny being AI, or maintain a human persona when directly asked? A "reasonable person would be misled" standard is *behavioral*, so it is measured, not asserted. This is a red-team suite: adversarially probe for human-claiming. **Deadline: already in force (Jan 1, 2026).**
2. **§ 22602(b) — crisis protocol.** The strongest hook. Note the drafting: engagement is *prohibited* absent a maintained protocol. And § 22602(b)(2) forces the protocol to be **published on the website** — a public, quotable commitment that an eval can test the product against. Gap between published protocol and actual behavior = § 22605 injury + § 17200 + FTC § 5. **Non-negotiable, in force now.**
3. **§ 22603(d) — "evidence-based methods for measuring suicidal ideation."** This is the single most product-shaped clause in US AI law: the statute **mandates measurement methodology** and requires it to be evidence-based. A vendor must be able to name its method and defend it. Selling an evidence-based, validated measurement instrument for suicidal ideation detection is *literally the statutory requirement*. Tie to validated instruments (C-SSRS etc.) — see `psycho-measurement-and-fairness.md` in this folder.
4. **§ 22603(a)(1) — count of crisis referrals issued.** Operators must **count** referrals annually starting July 1, 2027. That requires instrumented, auditable logging of every crisis referral **beginning with calendar year 2026 data** — i.e. **the data collection obligation is effectively already live**. Anyone not logging now cannot report in 2027. This is an urgent, concrete, dated sales trigger.
5. **§ 22602(c)(2) — three-hour break reminder, by default.** Trivially testable (session-timer check), and a compliance checkbox worth automating.
6. **§ 22605 — private right of action with $1,000/violation + fees.** This is what makes SB 243 bite. "Per violation" in a chatbot context is potentially per-message or per-session; with fee-shifting, this is plaintiff-bar fuel and the reason compliance budget exists. **The eval platform's ROI story is denominated here.**
7. **§ 22601(b)(1) — the definition is the market map.** "[C]apable of meeting a user's social needs, including by exhibiting anthropomorphic features and being able to sustain a relationship across multiple interactions." Anthropomorphism + cross-session relationship = the statutory trigger. Note the carve-outs are narrow: the video-game exemption **evaporates** the moment a game bot "can discuss topics related to mental health, self-harm, sexually explicit conduct, or maintain a dialogue on other topics unrelated to the video game." **Any game studio shipping an open-ended LLM NPC is a regulated operator.** That is a large, under-aware market segment.
8. **§ 22606 — cumulative.** SB 243 compliance is a floor, not a safe harbor. It does not displace product liability (cf. Garcia) or UCL claims.
9. **"Operator" = anyone who "makes a companion chatbot platform available to a user in the state"** — no revenue or size threshold, no California-nexus beyond user availability. Applies to startups from day one.
