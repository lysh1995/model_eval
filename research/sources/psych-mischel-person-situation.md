## THE FAMOUS NUMBER — verbatim, with the actual wording

The quote everyone paraphrases and almost nobody quotes. Mischel (1968), **p. 78**, as reproduced in
Funder et al. (2012):

> "…the phrase **'personality coefficient' might be coined to describe the correlation between .20
> and .30** … when virtually any personality dimension inferred from a questionnaire is related to
> almost any … external criterion" (Mischel, 1968, p. 78).

**Note the actual range is .20–.30, not ".30" or ".40".** The commonly cited ".30 barrier" and ".40
ceiling" are later accretions:

> "A fellow-adherent to this position, Richard Nisbett, later **raised the putative limit for the
> predictive power of personality to about r = .40** (Nisbett, 1980, p. 124)." (Funder et al., 2012)

Kihlstrom's framing of the same point:

> "Across a wide variety of studies, **the typical correlation between questionnaire scores and some
> trait-relevant criterion** observed either in laboratory settings or in the real world **is only
> about .30**."

> "Mischel (1968) **dubbed this figure the 'personality coefficient', implying that by and large it
> represents the upper limit on prediction** of criterion behavior from questionnaires."

The variance-accounted-for framing that made it feel devastating:

> "If we take Mischel's personality coefficient as representative… thus **(r=.30)² = .09, accounting
> for only 9% of criterion variance**." (Kihlstrom)

## WHAT MISCHEL ACTUALLY ARGUED — get this right

**Mischel did NOT claim that personality does not exist.** This is the single most common misreading
and the team should not repeat it.

What he actually wrote (Mischel, 1968, p. 146):

> "...with the possible exception of intelligence, **highly generalised behavioral consistencies have
> not been demonstrated, and the concept of personality traits as broad dispositions is thus
> untenable**" (p. 146)

The target is precisely and narrowly **"traits as broad, cross-situationally generalized
dispositions"** — i.e. *exactly the model the team's character sheet assumes*. It is not personality,
not stability, not individuality.

> "More recently, **Mischel has retracted some of his original claims, protesting that some
> psychologists misinterpreted his argument to mean he believes personality does not exist.**"
> (Wikipedia, Person–situation debate)

Kihlstrom, in a *critique* of trait psychology, still states plainly:

> "**This evidence should not be taken to mean that there are no generalized behavioral tendencies.
> Obviously there are.**"

And Mischel spent the next 30 years building a **positive** theory of personality invariance (CAPS —
see `psych-caps-behavioral-signatures.md`). The 1995 *Psychological Review* paper's own words:

> "Although this finding has been interpreted as evidence against the utility of the personality
> construct, **we show that it need not be** and, on the contrary, that **this variability reflects
> some of the essence of personality coherence.**"

**Summary of the correct reading:** Mischel argued that *behavior is not cross-situationally
invariant*, that *the trait-as-broad-disposition model therefore fails as a predictor of specific
acts*, and that *the real invariance lies elsewhere*. He did not argue that people lack stable
characters. **This is exactly the distinction the team's metric collapses.**

## Mischel's own later distinction: temporal stability ≠ cross-situational consistency

Reported in Diener & Larsen (1984, p. 872) — **critical for the team's "turn 3 vs turn 97" framing**:

> "**Mischel (1979) recently wrote that there appears to be substantial temporal stability in
> behavior, but a small amount of cross-situational consistency.** Indeed, he writes that **the belief
> in personality traits comes about because of the perception of the stability in behavior** (Mischel
> & Peake, 1982)."

**Two different axes, two different empirical verdicts:**

| Axis | Mischel's verdict | Team's metric conflates them |
|---|---|---|
| **Temporal stability** (same situation, later time) | **substantial** — high | "turn 3 vs turn 97" |
| **Cross-situational consistency** (different situation, any time) | **small** — the .20–.30 ceiling | "across many different contexts" |

The team's metric — "same person in turn 3 **and** turn 97, **across many different contexts**" —
**multiplies these two together into one number**. One of them should be high and one of them should
be *low in a patterned way*. A single scalar cannot express that. **This is the core measurement
error in the framework**, and it is diagnosable directly from Mischel's own 1979 distinction.

## Was .30 ever actually small? — the Funder & Ozer reanalysis

Funder et al. (2012) pose the two questions the debate turned on:

> "The claim of such a limit to the predictive power of personality immediately raises two questions:
> **(1) Is a correlation between .30 and .40 small or large? (2) Are correlations between attributes
> of situations and behavior substantially higher than correlations between personality traits and
> behavior?**"

### Answer to (1): squaring r is the wrong move

> "On technical grounds, **it actually makes little sense to square correlation coefficients in order
> to estimate the power of the relationship they describe** (Ozer, 1985)."

Using the **Binomial Effect Size Display** (Rosenthal & Rubin, 1982), on a hypothetical sample of 200:

> "...a criterion correlation of **.40**, which is the alleged upper limit of the personality
> coefficient. **If an individual is above the median on the predictor, he or she has a 70% chance of
> being above the median on the criterion**; conversely, if he or she is below the median on the
> predictor, the individual has a 70% chance of also being below the median on the criterion. **If the
> correlation is .30, this figure remains a still-impressive 65%.**"

> "It implies that even if this coefficient does lie in range of .30 to .40 ... **personality
> variables can still predict behavioral criteria with a degree of accuracy likely to yield
> predictions that are correct about twice as often as they are wrong. It remains surprising that so
> few research psychologists seem to know this.**"

### Answer to (2): **NO — situational effects are the same size.** This is the Funder & Ozer (1983) reanalysis

> "In a more psychological vein, **Funder and Ozer (1983) examined three classical studies in social
> psychology** that demonstrated effects of situational variables on behaviors."

The three studies:
- **Festinger & Carlsmith (1959)** — the "reverse incentive effect," that smaller incentives lead to
  greater attitude change in the forced compliance paradigm (cognitive dissonance).
- **Darley & Batson (1967); Darley & Latané (1968)** — bystander intervention: "people were less
  likely to stop and help others in need when in a hurry, or if other bystanders were present."
- **Milgram (1963)** — obedience: "more likely to occur if the authority figure was close by or the
  victim far away."

The finding:

> "**In each case, the size of the effects of the situational variables on behaviors, translated using
> simple arithmetic to the metric of the correlation coefficient, was between r = .30 and r = .40.**"

> "The effects of these classic studies -- all of which are basic topics of every textbook in social
> psychology -- are **remarkably close in magnitude to the putative personality coefficient**."

**The punchline:** the situationist critique used ".30 is pathetically small" to attack personality —
but the most famous situational effects in the history of social psychology are *the same size*.
Either both are small, or neither is.

Broader confirmation:

> "One comprehensive review concluded that **the average size of the effect of situational variables
> on behaviors, in social psychological experiments, is r = .21** (Richard et al., 2003)."

**Situations are, on average, *weaker* predictors than personality.** A calibration anchor from the
same paper:

> "it is well known that cities at higher elevations tend to have lower temperatures... **The
> correlation describing this relationship, based on weather station records, is -.34**"

Nobody calls altitude→temperature a negligible relationship.

## The verdict on the phrase itself

> "**The coining of the phrase 'personality coefficient' led to decades of confusion, not all of which
> has been alleviated, to this day. The phrase was misleading** in that correlations in the range
> from .30 to .40 are useful for behavioral prediction, and the sizes of some of the effects of
> situations contained in the literature of social psychology are of equivalent, or even smaller
> magnitude."

> "**While the person-situation debate was largely based on a misunderstanding of the magnitude of the
> correlations** that characterize relations between personality traits and behavior, it drew
> much-needed attention to the importance of situations." (abstract)

## The false dichotomy that drove the whole debate

> "**A persistent assumption underlying the person-situation debate was that the two factors compete in
> the determination of behavior: The more important one of them was, the less important the other one
> must be. But this assumption is false. As people adjust their behavior to each situation they
> encounter, they also maintain their individual differences.**"

**This sentence is the whole answer to the team's framing.** "Adjusts to the situation" and
"maintains individual differences" are **not opposites**. The team's metric treats them as opposites:
any situational adjustment is scored as loss of individual difference. The literature's central,
hard-won conclusion is that this trade-off does not exist.

The resolution the field reached:

> "**Most personality researchers have now concluded that both the person and the situation contribute
> to behavior.**" / "**The interactionism perspective recognizes that the effect of personality depends
> on the situation and that situations are affected by the personalities of the people who are
> present.**" (Wikipedia)

## Supporting historical numbers (Kihlstrom)

**Hartshorne & May (1928)**, the honesty studies that started it all — children, deceit across
settings:
> within-category intercorrelations "were quite substantial, **averaging .57**"; intercorrelations
> among tests in different categories "were quite low, **averaging .20**."

**Bem & Allen (1974)**: "The two indices of friendliness correlated **.30**"; for conscientiousness
"The average correlation among these different situations was **-.19**."

**Mischel & Peake (1982)**, Carleton College conscientiousness study (63 students):
> "The average correlation across the 19 different behavioral variables was **a mere .08**."
> "Even when the variables were aggregated over time, so that 2-12 different observations went into
> the measurement, **the average cross-situation correlation was only 0.13**."

**Note carefully**: aggregating *over time* did **not** rescue *cross-situational* consistency
(.08 → .13). Aggregation fixes temporal unreliability; it does **not** manufacture cross-situational
invariance, because the cross-situational variance **is not error**. See
`psych-epstein-aggregation.md` for why this distinction decides how many turns to sample.

## Implications for the team's "character consistency" metric

1. **The team has independently reinvented the 1968 situationist assumption — and adopted it as a
   scoring rubric.** The framework assumes behavior *should* be cross-situationally invariant and
   scores deviation as defect. Mischel's entire empirical case is that **this invariance does not
   exist in humans, at a documented ceiling of r ≈ .20–.30.** A model that achieved the team's ideal
   (invariance ≈ 1.0) would be **3–5× more cross-situationally consistent than any human ever
   measured**. That is not fidelity to a character; that is a tell for a machine.

2. **The metric's implicit target is empirically out of human range. Set the target to the human
   number.** If they keep any cross-situational consistency measure at all, the calibration band is
   **r ≈ .20–.40**, not 1.0. Scores *above* that band should raise a **rigidity** flag, not earn
   praise. Concretely: **a "character consistency" score near ceiling is evidence of a failure
   mode**, and the current framework has no way to report that.

3. **Do not repeat the "personality doesn't exist" misreading — the correct claim is sharper and more
   useful to them.** Mischel attacked *traits as broad dispositions*, which is precisely the
   character-sheet-as-specification model. He did not attack *stable individuality*. So the team's
   goal ("this feels like the same person") is sound; their **operationalization** (invariance to
   context) is the thing that was refuted. **The goal survives; the metric does not.**

4. **Split the metric on Mischel's own 1979 axis — this is the single highest-value change.**
   - **Temporal stability**: hold situation type constant, compare early turns vs late turns.
     Expect **high**. Deviations here are **real drift** and *should* be scored as defects. This is
     the legitimate core of "turn 3 vs turn 97."
   - **Cross-situational consistency**: vary situation, measure invariance. Expect **~.20–.40**.
     Deviations here are **not** defects — they are the character responding to context.
   Their current single number cannot distinguish "the model forgot who it was by turn 97" (a real
   bug, temporal) from "the model behaved differently at the funeral than at the party" (correct
   behavior, situational). **Right now both are scored identically as failures.** Splitting the
   metric on this axis converts a broken number into two working ones — at essentially no
   engineering cost, since they already have the situation labels.

5. **Their "punishing realism" suspicion is confirmed, and Funder & Ozer sharpens it.** The famous
   situational effects (Milgram, Festinger, Darley & Latané) are **r = .30–.40** — situations move
   humans about as much as traits do. A character sheet that survives contact with a strong situation
   *unchanged* is not a well-specified character; it is one that isn't responding to the scene.
   **Milgram's obedience effect would register as a massive character-sheet violation** under the
   team's metric — a "gentle" character sheet, placed in the Milgram situation, should deviate,
   because that is what people do.

6. **Stop squaring the correlation, and adopt the BESD for stakeholder communication.** If the team
   reports "our consistency metric only explains 9% of variance" they will be making the exact error
   that misled the field for 30 years ("it actually makes little sense to square correlation
   coefficients" — Ozer, 1985). At r = .30, the BESD gives **65% correct classification**; at r =
   .40, **70%** — "correct about twice as often as they are wrong." This is also the right frame for
   any judge-agreement or eval-validity number they report.

7. **On trait entanglement:** the person-situation literature is mostly silent on this — it is the
   metatrait and halo literatures that speak to it directly. But one relevant lesson transfers: the
   field's error was assuming **person and situation compete** ("the more important one of them was,
   the less important the other one must be. But this assumption is false"). The team is making the
   structurally identical assumption about traits: **that traits compete/partition, so a well-formed
   character sheet has independent, separately-perturbable dimensions.** That assumption should be
   tested, not built into the scoring — see `psych-metatraits-trait-intercorrelation.md`.
